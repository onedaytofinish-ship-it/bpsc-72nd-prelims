Post-Mauryan — Sungas, Kushanas, Satavahanas
📂 Ancient History ⚖️ Weight: 0.7 🎯 Expected: 2–3 Qs 🔶 Bihar: Shungas ruled from Pataliputra
📚 Core Content
EXAM TIP Post-Mauryan period (185 BCE - 320 CE): Fragmented into multiple kingdoms — NOT a single empire. Shungas (north, Pataliputra), Indo-Greeks (northwest, Menander/Milinda), Shakas (west, Rudradaman), Kushanas (northwest, Kanishka, Saka Era 78 CE), Satavahanas (Deccan, Gautamiputra Satakarni). Key innovations: gold coins (Indo-Greeks), Gandhara + Mathura art (Kushanas), Fourth Buddhist Council (Kanishka, Kashmir), Silk Route trade, Sanskrit inscriptions (Rudradaman's Junagadh).
Gandhara School Buddha (1st–3rd c. CE) — Greco-Roman influence from Indo-Greek kingdoms. Wavy hair, muscular body, Greek drapery, blue-grey schist. The first human images of the Buddha were produced during the Post-Mauryan period (Kushana era). Compare with Mathura School (indigenous, red sandstone).
Gold coin of Kanishka I (c. 78 CE) — the greatest Kushana ruler. Kanishka initiated the Saka Era (78 CE, still used by the Indian government). He convened the Fourth Buddhist Council in Kashmir. The Kushanas controlled the Silk Route and issued the finest gold coins of ancient India.

🔶 Bihar Connection
Bihar — Shungas Ruled from Pataliputra
  • Pataliputra (Patna): the Shungas continued to rule from Pataliputra (Bihar) — the former Mauryan capital. Pushyamitra Shunga (founder, c. 185 BCE) ruled from Pataliputra. The Shungas controlled Bihar, Bengal, and central India.
  • Brahminical revival: Pushyamitra Shunga, a Brahmin, revived Vedic traditions (performed the Ashvamedha sacrifice) in the heartland of Buddhism (Bihar) — where Ashoka had promoted Buddhism. This represents a cultural shift in Bihar.
  • Buddhism continued: despite the Brahminical revival, Buddhism continued to flourish in Bihar. The Bharhut Stupa (built during the Shunga period, c. 2nd c. BCE) shows Buddhist art was still patronised — its reliefs depict Jataka stories. The Sanchi Stupa was also enlarged during this period.
  • Pataliputra as Buddhist centre: even after the Shungas, Pataliputra (Bihar) remained an important Buddhist centre. The Third Buddhist Council had already been held there (Ashoka, c. 250 BCE). Buddhist scholars and monks continued to study and teach in Bihar throughout the Post-Mauryan period — laying the groundwork for Nalanda University (founded 5th c. CE, Gupta period).
  • Kharavela's incursion: the Hathigumpha inscription (Kharavela of Kalinga, c. 1st c. BCE) mentions that Kharavela marched north and reached Rajgir and Pataliputra — showing Bihar was still a significant political centre that attracted southern rulers.
  • Barabar Caves (Bihar): the Ajivika caves (given by Ashoka and Dasharatha Maurya) continued in use during the Post-Mauryan period — showing Bihar's rock-cut cave tradition. The Nagarjuni Hill caves were dedicated by Dasharatha (grandson of Ashoka, Post-Mauryan).
  • After the Shungas: Bihar came under the Guptas (c. 320 CE) — who made Pataliputra their capital again, beginning the 'Golden Age.' The Kushanas did NOT directly rule Bihar (they ruled the northwest + upper Ganga plains to Varanasi). The Vakatakas (central India) did not rule Bihar either — but their alliance with the Guptas connected them to Bihar's political sphere.
🔗 Cross-Topic Connections

  • Topic 101 (Buddhism & Jainism): The Fourth Buddhist Council (Kanishka, Kashmir, 72 CE) split Buddhism into Hinayana and Mahayana. The Milinda Panha records Indo-Greek king Menander's dialogue with Buddhist monk Nagasena.
  • Topic 104 (Gupta Empire): After the Post-Mauryan period, the Guptas (c. 320 CE) reunified north India from Pataliputra — beginning the 'Golden Age.' The Guptas inherited the Mauryan capital and the legacy of centralized rule.
📰 Current Relevance
  • Saka Era (78 CE): the official calendar of India — used by the Government of India for the National Calendar. 2026 CE = 1948-49 Saka. Started by Kanishka's accession.
  • Silk Route: the Kushanas controlled the ancient Silk Route (China to Rome). Today, China's 'Belt and Road Initiative' (BRI) evokes the same route — connecting Asia, Europe, and Africa.
  • Gandhara art: the first human images of the Buddha (Gandhara + Mathura schools, Kushana period) — now in museums worldwide. The Bamiyan Buddhas (Afghanistan, destroyed by Taliban in 2001) were also Kushana-era.
  • Milinda Panha: the dialogue between Indo-Greek king Menander and Buddhist monk Nagasena — a classic of Buddhist philosophy, still studied today.

Q1. Who killed the last Mauryan emperor Brihadratha and founded the Shunga dynasty?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Pushyamitra Shunga, a Brahmin general, killed Brihadratha c. 185 BCE. Founded Shunga dynasty. Patron of Brahminical culture, performed Ashvamedha.
Q2. The Shunga dynasty ruled from which capital?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Shungas ruled from Pataliputra (Bihar) — continuing the Mauryan capital. Pushyamitra (c. 185-149 BCE). Extended from Punjab to Bengal, south to Narmada.
Q3. Pushyamitra Shunga was a patron of which religion?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Pushyamitra patronised Brahminical Hinduism. Performed Ashvamedha (Vedic horse sacrifice). Buddhist texts accuse him of persecuting Buddhists (debated). Brahminical revival.
Q4. The famous Bharhut Stupa (with magnificent Buddhist sculptures) was built during which period?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Bharhut Stupa (Madhya Pradesh) built/enlarged during Shunga period (c. 2nd c. BCE). Buddhist sculptures (railings, gateways) with inscriptions. Now in Indian Museum, Kolkata.
Q5. Patanjali, the famous grammarian who wrote the Mahabhashya (commentary on Panini's grammar), lived during which period?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Patanjali lived during the Shunga period (c. 2nd c. BCE), contemporary of Pushyamitra. Mahabhashya = greatest commentary on Panini's Ashtadhyayi. Also Yoga Sutras (possibly different Patanjali).
Q6. After the decline of the Mauryan Empire, which foreign invaders entered India from the northwest?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Indo-Greeks (Bactrian Greeks) invaded c. 180 BCE. Most famous: Menander I (Milinda, c. 165-130 BCE) — ruled from Punjab, possibly converted to Buddhism (Milinda Panha).
Q7. The most famous Indo-Greek king, who ruled from Punjab and possibly converted to Buddhism, was:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Menander I (Milinda, c. 165-130 BCE). Ruled from Sakala (Sialkot, Punjab). Milinda Panha records dialogue with monk Nagasena. Possible Buddhist conversion.
Q8. The Indo-Greeks introduced which innovation to Indian coinage?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Indo-Greeks introduced gold/silver coins with realistic portraits + bilingual legends (Greek on obverse, Kharoshthi/Prakrit on reverse). Earliest Indian coins with portraits.
Q9. The Kushanas were a branch of which nomadic tribe?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Kushanas = branch of Yuezhi (Yueh-chi) from Central Asia (modern Xinjiang/Gansu, China). Migrated west, displaced Indo-Greeks, established Kushana Empire (c. 1st-3rd c. CE).
Q10. Who was the most famous Kushana emperor, known for the Fourth Buddhist Council and the Saka Era?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Kanishka (c. 78 CE). Fourth Buddhist Council (Kashmir, 72 CE). Saka Era (78 CE). Empire from Central Asia to Varanasi. Gandhara + Mathura art. Silk Route.
Q11. The Fourth Buddhist Council was held under Kanishka's patronage at which place?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Fourth Council: Kashmir (c. 72 CE), Kanishka. Presided by Vasumitra (deputy Ashvaghosha). Split: Hinayana (Theravada) vs Mahayana. Sanskrit became Buddhist scholarly language.
Q12. The Saka Era (starting 78 CE) is associated with which ruler?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Saka Era (78 CE) = Kanishka's accession. Official calendar of India (National Calendar). 2026 CE = 1948-49 Saka. Still used by Government of India.
Q13. The famous Silk Route trade flourished under which empire?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Kushanas controlled the Silk Route (China to Rome via Central Asia). Prospered from trade (silk, spices, gold, gems). Kanishka's empire extended to Central Asia (Bactria, Kashgar, Yarkand).
Q14. The Gandhara School of Art (Indo-Greek Buddhist art) flourished under which empire?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Gandhara School flourished under Kushanas (1st-3rd c. CE) in Gandhara region (Peshawar, Taxila). Greco-Roman + Indian styles. First human images of Buddha (Hellenistic features).
Q15. The Mathura School of Art (indigenous Indian Buddhist art) also flourished under the Kushanas. Where is Mathura?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Mathura School at Mathura (UP, south of Delhi). Indigenous Indian style (unlike Greco-Roman Gandhara). Buddha with Indian features (usnisa, serene, transparent robes). Also Jain + Hindu images.
Q16. Ashvaghosha, the famous Buddhist scholar who wrote the Buddhacharita (a biography of the Buddha in Sanskrit), lived during which period?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Ashvaghosha = Kanishka's court poet. Wrote Buddhacharita (first biography of the Buddha, in Sanskrit). Also Sutralankara. Deputy president of Fourth Buddhist Council.
Q17. The Satavahana dynasty ruled which region of India (c. 1st century BCE - 3rd century CE)?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Satavahanas ruled the Deccan (Maharashtra, Telangana, AP, Karnataka). Capital: Pratishthana (Paithan, Maharashtra). First major Deccan empire. Founder: Simuka. Greatest: Gautamiputra Satakarni.
Q18. The greatest Satavahana king, who defeated the Shaka ruler Nahapana and extended the empire, was:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Gautamiputra Satakarni (c. 106-130 CE). Defeated Shaka Nahapana. Extended Malwa to Karnataka. Mother Gautami Balasri's Nashik inscription. 'Destroyer of the pride of the Kshaharatas.'
Q19. The Satavahanas were known for which unique administrative feature?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Satavahanas used matronymics: Gautamiputra ('son of Gautami'), Vasishtiputra, Pulumavi. High status for women. Women donated land and made religious gifts.
Q20. The Satavahanas issued which type of coins primarily?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Satavahanas issued lead, potin (copper alloy), copper coins — unique to Deccan, for local trade. Also some silver/gold (imitating Indo-Greek/Shaka styles). Lead coins: elephants, horses, Ujjain symbols.
Q21. The Satavahanas patronised which religions?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Satavahanas patronised both Buddhism and Brahminical Hinduism. Land grants (agraharas) to Brahmins, donations to Buddhist monasteries. Amaravati Stupa, Nashik caves. Religious eclecticism.
Q22. The Shaka (Western Kshatrapa) dynasty ruled which region?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Shakas (Western Kshatrapas) ruled Saurashtra (Gujarat) and Malwa (western India), c. 1st-4th c. CE. Capital: Ujjain. Famous ruler: Rudradaman I (Junagadh inscription, 150 CE).
Q23. Rudradaman I, the famous Shaka ruler, is known for which inscription?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Rudradaman's Junagadh Rock Inscription (c. 150 CE, Gujarat) = first long inscription in pure Sanskrit (earlier were Prakrit). Records repair of Sudarshana Lake (built by Chandragupta Maurya, enhanced by Ashoka). Defeated Satavahanas twice.
Q24. Bihar's connection with the Post-Mauryan period is primarily through which fact?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Shungas ruled from Pataliputra (Bihar) — the Mauryan capital. Pushyamitra Shunga controlled Bihar, Bengal, central India. After Shungas, Bihar came under the Guptas (c. 320 CE). Kushanas ruled northwest, not Bihar directly.
Q25. Which of the following is NOT a feature of the Post-Mauryan period (c. 185 BCE - 320 CE)?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Post-Mauryan period was NOT a single unified empire — fragmented: Shungas (north), Indo-Greeks (northwest), Shakas (west), Kushanas (northwest+north), Satavahanas (Deccan). Unlike the unified Mauryan Empire.
