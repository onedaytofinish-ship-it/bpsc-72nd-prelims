B. Portuguese in India — Key Facts

  • First to arrive (1498), last to leave (1961): Goa, Daman and Diu were liberated by India in 1961 — 14 years after the British left. This "first to come, last to leave" paradox is a BPSC favourite.
  • Vasco da Gama: arrived at Calicut (Kozhikode) in May 1498, received by the Zamorin (local ruler). He sailed around the Cape of Good Hope (Africa).
  • Cabral (1500): second voyage, established first factory at Calicut.
  • Almeida (1505): first Portuguese viceroy; introduced the "Blue Water Policy" — naval dominance over the Indian Ocean.
  • Albuquerque (1506–1515): captured Goa in 1510 from Bijapur; consolidated the "Estado da Índia" (Portuguese maritime empire).
  • Goa: Portuguese political HQ from 1510; also the seat of the Inquisition in India (1560–1812).
  • Portuguese contributions: introduced tobacco, cashew nuts, chillies (red pepper), and the printing press (first printed book in India: "Conclusões e outras coisas" in Goa, 1556). Also introduced the "Cartaze" system — a pass system requiring Indian ships to buy Portuguese permits.
  • Decline: defeated by the English and Dutch in the 17th century; lost their commercial dominance but held Goa, Daman, Diu, and Dadra & Nagar Haveli until 1954–1961.
Portuguese settlements across India — Goa (HQ, 1510), Daman, Diu on the west coast; Cochin and Calicut on the Malabar coast. The Portuguese held these until 1954–1961.

C. Dutch in India — Key Facts

  • Dutch East India Company (VOC): established 1602 — the world's first joint-stock company.
  • First voyage: Cornelis de Houtman reached India in 1595 (before the company was even formed).
  • Headquarters: Pulicat (1610, on the Coromandel Coast), later shifted to Cochin.
  • Bihar connection: Dutch factory at Patna (1632) — 69th BPSC PYQ. Traded in saltpetre (for gunpowder), indigo, opium and textiles. Also had a factory at Rajmahal.
  • Main trade: spices (pepper, cloves, nutmeg) from the East Indies (Indonesia), textiles and indigo from India. India was a secondary theatre — Indonesia was their primary focus.
  • Decline: defeated by the English at the Battle of Bidara (1759); gradually withdrew to focus on Indonesia. Last factory closed in 1825.

D. English (British) in India — Key Facts

  • English East India Company: chartered by Queen Elizabeth I on 31 December 1600.
  • First arrival: William Hawkins reached Surat in 1608, went to Jahangir's court, was given the title "English Khan".
  • Sir Thomas Roe (1615–1619): official ambassador to Jahangir; secured farman for trade — the diplomatic foundation.
  • First factory: Surat (1613), after the Battle of Swally (1612) broke Portuguese naval power.
  • Three Presidency settlements: Madras / Fort St. George (1639), Bombay (1661, dowry), Calcutta / Fort William (1690, Job Charnock).
  • Farman of 1717: Farrukhsiyar granted duty-free trade in Bengal for ₹3,000/year — the commercial foundation that made Bengal the richest English post.
  • From trade to empire: after Plassey (1757), the English shifted from commerce to political control — see Topic #25.
European factories on the Hooghly River (Bengal, 18th century): all five powers — English (Calcutta), French (Chandernagore), Danish (Serampore), Dutch (Chinsurah), Portuguese (Hugli) — within 40 km of each other.
European maritime trade routes — the Portuguese came around Africa, the Dutch focused on the East Indies, and the English built three coastal presidency cities.

E. French in India — Key Facts

  • French East India Company: founded 1664 by Jean-Baptiste Colbert (finance minister of Louis XIV). State-sponsored, unlike the English/Dutch joint-stock model.
  • Headquarters: Pondicherry (founded 1674 by François Martin).
  • Other French posts: Chandernagore (Bengal, 1673), Masulipatnam, Karaikal, Mahe, Yanam.
  • Dupleix (1742–1754): governor who introduced the "Policy of Imperialism" — using Indian sepoys under European officers and interfering in Indian succession disputes (Carnatic Wars). The English copied this strategy.
  • Anglo-French Wars: three Carnatic Wars (1746–1763); the French were ultimately defeated and marginalised.
  • Pondicherry remained French until 1954 (de facto transfer to India); de jure in 1962.

F. Danes in India — Quick Facts

  • Danish East India Company: established 1616; first settlement at Tranquebar (Tarangambadi, Tamil Nadu, 1620).
  • Serampore (Bengal, 1755): the Danish base that became famous for the Serampore Mission (1799) — William Carey, Joshua Marshman, William Ward — the first printing press in Indian languages and pioneering work in Bengali literature and Bible translation.
  • Nicobar Islands: also held by the Danes (sold to British in 1868).
  • Sold out: the Danes sold all Indian possessions to the British in 1845 (Tranquebar and Serampore).

🔶 Bihar Connection
🏛️ Bihar & the European Trading Posts
  • Dutch factory at Patna (1632): the 69th BPSC PYQ asked this exact date. The Dutch traded in saltpetre (potassium nitrate, for gunpowder), indigo, opium and textiles from Patna. Bihar was a major saltpetre-producing region — the Dutch and English both sourced it from here.
  • Dutch at Rajmahal: the Dutch also had a factory at Rajmahal (in the Bengal subah, near the Bihar border) — another Bihar-adjacent trading post.
  • English interest in Bihar: after the Farman of 1717, English commercial interest expanded into Bihar's interior. After Plassey (1757) and the Diwani of Bengal (1765, which included Bihar), the English established factories at Patna and Munger for saltpetre, opium and indigo — the same goods the Dutch had traded a century earlier.
  • Opium trade: Bihar (particularly the Gangetic plains around Patna and Gaya) became the centre of the English East India Company's opium monopoly from the 1770s onwards. The Patna Opium Factory (established 1780) was the largest opium-processing facility in the world — opium was exported to China, generating enormous revenue for the Company.
  • Saltpetre: Bihar's saltpetre (used in gunpowder) was a strategic commodity — European powers competed for it because it was essential for military supremacy. The English eventually monopolised it after the Dutch were driven out.

📰 Current Relevance & Potential Traps
  • Date traps: 1498 (da Gama) vs 1492 (Columbus) vs 1500 (Cabral) — all are "European explorer" dates that BPSC swaps. 1498 = India, 1492 = Americas.
  • Company formation vs arrival: English EIC was chartered in 1600 (before Dutch VOC in 1602), but the Dutch reached India first (1595). BPSC tests this distinction.
  • First to come, last to leave: the Portuguese came first (1498) and left last (1961) — the British left in 1947, French in 1954, Danes in 1845, Dutch in 1825. BPSC asks "which was the last European power to leave India?"
  • HQ confusion: Dutch HQ was Pulicat (not Cochin — Cochin was secondary). French HQ was Pondicherry (not Chandernagore — that was a Bengal subordinate post). English first factory was Surat (not Madras — Madras came in 1639).
  • Bombay dowry trap: Bombay came as the dowry of Catherine of Braganza (1661) — a Portuguese princess marrying Charles II. The Portuguese connection to Bombay is often missed.
  • Bihar PYQ: Dutch factory at Patna = 1632. Also know: Patna became an English factory post-1765 (after Diwani); the Patna Opium Factory (1780) was the world's largest.
✍️ MCQ Practice Set — 25 Questions (BPSC Level)
Q1. Vasco da Gama reached the port of Calicut (Kozhikode) in which year?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Vasco da Gama arrived at Calicut in May 1498, after sailing around the Cape of Good Hope. 1492 is Columbus reaching the Americas — a classic date-swap trap. 1500 is Cabral's arrival; 1505 is Almeida becoming viceroy.
Q2. Vasco da Gama's sea route to India went around which cape?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Da Gama sailed around the Cape of Good Hope (southern tip of Africa), then north through the Indian Ocean to Calicut. Cape Horn is in South America; the Suez Canal opened only in 1869.
Q3. The first European power to arrive in India and the last to leave were respectively:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — The Portuguese were the first to arrive (1498) AND the last to leave (Goa liberated in 1961). The British left in 1947, French in 1954 — the Portuguese held on for 14 more years after Independence.
Q4. The Portuguese established their first factory (trading post) in India at:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — The first Portuguese factory was at Calicut (1500, after Cabral's arrival). Goa was captured in 1510 (became HQ); Cochin was the commercial HQ from 1503. The trap is Goa — it became the capital later.
Q5. Afonso de Albuquerque captured Goa in which year?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Albuquerque captured Goa in 1510 from the Bijapur Sultanate. 1498 is da Gama's arrival; 1505 is Almeida's appointment as viceroy. Albuquerque also introduced the 'Blue Water Policy' of naval dominance.
Q6. The Dutch East India Company (VOC) was established in:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — The Dutch VOC was established in 1602 — the world's first joint-stock company. 1600 is the English EIC; 1608 is Hawkins' arrival; 1612 is the Battle of Swally. The Dutch company was formed 2 years after the English charter.
Q7. The Dutch East India Company established its factory at Patna in which year? (69th BPSC PYQ)
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — The Dutch established a factory at Patna in 1632 — a direct 69th BPSC PYQ. The Dutch traded in saltpetre, indigo and opium from Bihar. 1635, 1648 and 1664 are distractors.
Q8. The English East India Company was established in the year:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — The English EIC was chartered by Queen Elizabeth I on 31 December 1600. 1602 is the Dutch VOC; 1608 is Hawkins' arrival at Surat. The English charter predates the Dutch company, but the Dutch reached India first (1595).
Q9. Who was the first English ambassador to the Mughal court (arrived at Surat in 1608)?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — William Hawkins arrived at Surat in 1608 and reached Jahangir's court — the first English ambassador. He was given the title 'English Khan'. Sir Thomas Roe came later (1615) and was more successful diplomatically — a common confusion.
Q10. Sir Thomas Roe came to the court of which Mughal emperor, and in which year?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Sir Thomas Roe arrived at Jahangir's court in 1615 as the official ambassador of King James I. He stayed until 1619 and secured trading concessions. Akbar died in 1605, before the English arrived in India.
Q11. The English East India Company established its first factory in India at:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — The first English factory was at Surat (1613), after Jahangir's farman. Madras came in 1639; Bombay in 1661; Calcutta in 1690. Surat was the first English foothold in India.
Q12. The English acquired Bombay as part of the dowry of which royal?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Bombay was given to King Charles II in 1661 as part of the dowry of Catherine of Braganza, a Portuguese princess. It was transferred to the EIC in 1668 for £10/year. The Portuguese connection to Bombay is often missed.
Q13. Match the European power with its main headquarters in India:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Portuguese HQ: Goa (1510); Dutch HQ: Pulicat (1610); French HQ: Pondicherry (1674); English HQ: initially Surat, then Madras (1639). Traps: Dutch HQ was Pulicat (not Cochin); French HQ was Pondicherry (not Chandernagore).
Q14. The French East India Company was founded by:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — The French East India Company was founded in 1664 by Jean-Baptiste Colbert, finance minister of Louis XIV. François Martin founded Pondicherry (1674); Dupleix was the governor who initiated the Carnatic Wars. The French company was state-sponsored, unlike the English/Dutch joint-stock model.
Q15. The French founded their headquarters at Pondicherry in which year?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — François Martin founded Pondicherry in 1674. 1664 is the founding of the French East India Company by Colbert. Pondicherry remained French until 1954 — the second-longest European presence after the Portuguese in Goa.
Q16. The Danes (Danish East India Company) established their main settlement at:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The Danes established Tranquebar (1620, Tamil Nadu) as HQ and Serampore (1755, Bengal) as a secondary post. Serampore became famous for the Serampore Mission (1799) — the first printing press in Indian languages. The Danes sold all Indian possessions to the British in 1845.
Q17. Which European power was associated with the 'Blue Water Policy'?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — The 'Blue Water Policy' (Estado da Índia) was introduced by Albuquerque — aiming at Portuguese naval supremacy over the Indian Ocean rather than territorial conquest. No other European power used this maritime-first strategy.
Q18. Consider the chronological order of European arrival in India: 1. Portuguese 2. English 3. Dutch 4. French. The correct sequence is:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Portuguese (1498) → Dutch (1595) → English (1608) → French (1664). The English company was chartered in 1600 (before Dutch VOC in 1602), but the Dutch reached India first (1595). BPSC tests this distinction between company formation and actual arrival.
Q19. The Battle of Swally (1612) was fought between the English and which power?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — The Battle of Swally (1612, near Surat) was a naval battle where the English under Captain Best defeated the Portuguese. This broke the Portuguese naval monopoly and led to the Mughal farman allowing the English factory at Surat (1613).
Q20. The English East India Company's factory at Surat was established in which year?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The English factory at Surat was established in 1613, after Jahangir's farman (following the Battle of Swally 1612 and Roe's embassy 1615-1619). 1608 is Hawkins' arrival; 1615 is Roe's arrival. Surat (1613) was the first English factory in India.
Q21. Which Mughal emperor granted the farman allowing the English to trade at Surat?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Jahangir granted the farman allowing the English to establish a factory at Surat (1613), following Sir Thomas Roe's embassy. Akbar died in 1605, before the English arrived in 1608. Shah Jahan later attacked the English at Hooghly (1686).
Q22. Which European power introduced the 'Policy of Imperialism' through the Carnatic Wars?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The French under Dupleix introduced the 'Policy of Imperialism' — using Indian sepoys under European officers and interfering in Indian succession disputes. The English copied this strategy and eventually defeated the French in the Carnatic Wars (1746–1763).
Q23. Which European power was the first to be eliminated from the Indian trade rivalry?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — The Dutch were the first to be effectively eliminated after their defeat at the Battle of Bidara (1759). They focused on the East Indies (Indonesia) and ceded the Indian field to the English. The Portuguese held Goa until 1961; the French until 1954; the Danes sold out in 1845.
Q24. The 'Farman of 1717' granted by Farrukhsiyar to the English was significant because it:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — The Farman of 1717 granted by Farrukhsiyar allowed the EIC duty-free trade in Bengal for ₹3,000/year. It also permitted renting 38 villages around Calcutta and minting coins at Madras. This farman made Bengal the most profitable English post — leading to political control after Plassey (1757).
Q25. Which of the following is NOT correctly matched?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The Battle of Swally (1612) was fought between the English and the Portuguese, NOT the Dutch. The Dutch were defeated at the Battle of Bidara (1759). The other three pairings are correct: Portuguese → Blue Water Policy, French → Policy of Imperialism, English → Farman of 1717.
