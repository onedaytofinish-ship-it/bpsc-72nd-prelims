TOPIC #72 • TIER A
Diseases and Pathogens — Communicable vs Non-Communicable
📂 Biology ⚖️ Weight: 1.0 🎯 Expected: 2–3 Qs 🔶 Bihar: Kala-azar, AES Muzaffarpur, Leprosy
📚 Core Content
EXAM TIP Diseases classification (Tier A, high-yield): (1) Communicable (infectious): caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, protozoa, fungi, helminths) — transmitted from person to person, via vectors, contaminated water/food, or air. Examples: TB, malaria, cholera, dengue, COVID-19. (2) Non-communicable (NCDs): not caused by infectious agents — genetic, lifestyle, environmental factors. Examples: diabetes, hypertension, cancer, heart disease, autoimmune diseases. (3) Bihar-specific diseases: Kala-azar (Leishmania donovani, sandfly), AES (Muzaffarpur, litchi toxin), Leprosy (high burden state), TB, Lymphatic filariasis.
Diseases are classified as communicable (infectious — caused by pathogens like bacteria, viruses, protozoa, fungi) and non-communicable (chronic — caused by genetic, lifestyle, or environmental factors). Bihar faces a dual burden: infectious diseases (kala-azar, TB, AES, malaria) and rising non-communicable diseases (diabetes, hypertension, heart disease).
Bihar's disease control infrastructure: Rajendra Memorial Research Institute (Patna) leads kala-azar research. SKMCH Muzaffarpur studies AES. AIIMS Patna and IGIMS manage TB, HIV, and NCDs. The state implements NVBDCP (vector-borne diseases), NTEP (TB), NLEP (leprosy), and NPCDCS (NCDs).

🔶 Bihar Connection
Bihar — Disease Endemic Zones & Health Challenges
  • Kala-azar (Visceral Leishmaniasis): Bihar accounts for ~70% of India's kala-azar cases. Endemic districts: Muzaffarpur, Vaishali, East Champaran, Sitamarhi, Saharsa, Purnia. Caused by Leishmania donovani (protozoan), transmitted by sandfly Phlebotomus argentipes. Symptoms: fever, spleen/liver enlargement, darkened skin. Treatment: Miltefosine, AmBisome. Rajendra Memorial Research Institute (RMRI), Patna — dedicated kala-azar research institute. India's elimination target: <1 case per 10,000 (near achievement).
  • Acute Encephalitis Syndrome (AES): Muzaffarpur (Bihar) has seen recurrent AES outbreaks since 1995, primarily affecting children (especially undernourished). Linked to litchi fruit consumption (hypoglycin A and MCPG toxins) combined with malnutrition and heat. Outbreaks during litchi harvest (May-June). 2019 outbreak: >150 child deaths. SKMCH (Sri Krishna Medical College, Muzaffarpur) leads treatment and research. Prevention: ensure evening meal, limit early morning litchi consumption, improve nutrition.
  • Leprosy: Bihar is one of India's high-burden leprosy states (along with UP, Maharashtra, Odisha). India achieved leprosy elimination (<1 per 10,000) nationally in 2005, but Bihar has districts above the elimination threshold. Caused by Mycobacterium leprae. Affects skin and peripheral nerves. Curable with Multi-Drug Therapy (MDT: rifampicin + dapsone + clofazimine). NLEP (National Leprosy Eradication Programme) — active case detection drives in Bihar.
  • Tuberculosis: Bihar has a high TB burden. Part of India's NTEP (National TB Elimination Programme) — target: eliminate TB by 2025 (5 years ahead of SDG target). Bihar faces challenges: undernourishment, diabetes, smoking, delayed diagnosis. Nikshay Poshan Yojana provides ₹500/month nutritional support to TB patients. BCG vaccine given at birth.
  • Lymphatic Filariasis (Elephantiasis): Bihar is endemic for lymphatic filariasis (Wuchereria bancrofti, Culex mosquito). India's MDA (Mass Drug Administration) programme uses DEC + albendazole — annual single-dose to all eligible persons in endemic districts. India aims to eliminate LF by 2027. Bihar districts with high LF prevalence include Muzaffarpur, Darbhanga, and East Champaran.
  • Japanese Encephalitis (JE): JE affects children in Bihar (mainly in northern districts bordering UP). Caused by JE virus (Flavivirus), transmitted by Culex mosquito (pigs are amplifying hosts). Affects the brain — high case fatality. JE vaccine included in UIP in endemic districts. Bihar is part of the 'JE belt' along with UP, Assam, and West Bengal.
  • Bihar's disease control infrastructure: (1) RMRI Patna (kala-azar research), (2) SKMCH Muzaffarpur (AES), (3) AIIMS Patna (TB, HIV, NCDs), (4) IGIMS Patna (tertiary care), (5) State Health Society Bihar (implements national programmes), (6) Phulo Jano Medical College (Dumka) — outreach. Bihar faces dual disease burden: infectious diseases (kala-azar, AES, TB, leprosy, filariasis) + rising NCDs (diabetes, hypertension, heart disease).
🔗 Cross-Topic Connections
  • Topic 70 (Human Body Systems): Diseases affect specific body systems: pneumonia (respiratory), hepatitis (liver/digestive), meningitis/encephalitis (nervous), heart disease (circulatory). Understanding pathogens requires understanding which body system they attack.
  • Topic 71 (Nutrition & Vitamins): Malnutrition weakens the immune system, making the body more susceptible to infectious diseases. Undernutrition is a key risk factor for TB, AES (Muzaffarpur), and kala-azar. Vitamin A deficiency increases infection risk. Vitamin C and D support immunity.
  • Topic 69 (Cell & Division): Viruses hijack the host cell's machinery to replicate. HIV attacks CD4+ T cells. Cancer involves uncontrolled cell division — a non-communicable disease. Understanding cell biology is essential for understanding how pathogens cause disease.
📰 Current Relevance
  • COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2023): SARS-CoV-2 caused a global pandemic — >770 million cases, >7 million deaths globally. India's response: lockdown (March 2020), vaccination drive (from 16 January 2021 — Covishield, Covaxin). India administered >2.2 billion vaccine doses. Lesson: zoonotic diseases (animal-to-human jump) can cause pandemics. WHO declared COVID-19 PHEIC over on 5 May 2023.
  • India's TB elimination target (2025): India aims to eliminate TB by 2025 — 5 years ahead of the global SDG target. NTEP (National TB Elimination Programme). Nikshay Poshan Yojana (₹500/month to TB patients). Nikshay portal (case tracking). India has ~2.8 million TB cases annually (highest globally). Bihar is a high-TB-burden state.
  • Kala-azar elimination: India is close to eliminating kala-azar (<1 case per 10,000). Bihar accounts for most cases. WHO validated India's kala-azar elimination progress (2023). The Kala-azar Elimination Programme uses indoor residual spraying (IRS), active case detection, and treatment (Miltefosine, AmBisome, Paromomycin). Bihar's RMRI Patna leads research.
  • NCD epidemic in India: Non-communicable diseases cause ~71% of global deaths. India faces a rising NCD burden: diabetes (~77 million, 2nd globally), hypertension (~200 million), cardiovascular disease (leading cause of death). NPCDCS (National Programme for Prevention and Control of Cancer, Diabetes, Cardiovascular Diseases and Stroke). Bihar is seeing rising NCD rates with urbanisation.
  • Monkeypox (mpox) outbreak (2024): WHO declared mpox a PHEIC (Public Health Emergency of International Concern) in August 2024 — new clade (Clade Ib) spreading in Africa. India has screening at airports. Mpox is caused by monkeypox virus (Orthopoxvirus — related to smallpox). Transmitted through close contact. Smallpox vaccine provides some cross-protection.
  • Bihar's AES challenge: Recurrent AES outbreaks in Muzaffarpur highlight the intersection of agriculture (litchi), nutrition (undernourishment), and disease. Research at SKMCH and NCDC (National Centre for Disease Control) has linked AES to hypoglycin A and MCPG toxins in litchi combined with low blood sugar in undernourished children. Prevention: nutrition supplementation, evening meals, awareness campaigns during litchi season.

Q1. Which of the following is a communicable disease?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Tuberculosis is communicable (infectious) — caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis bacteria, spread through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Diabetes, hypertension, and heart disease are non-communicable (lifestyle/genetic).
Q2. The causative agent of malaria is:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Malaria is caused by Plasmodium protozoan parasites. P. falciparum = most severe form. Transmitted by female Anopheles mosquito. P. vivax = most common in India. Life cycle: sporozoites → liver (schizonts) → RBCs (merozoites, ring stage, trophozoite) → gametocytes (infect mosquito).
Q3. Kala-azar (visceral leishmaniasis), a disease endemic to Bihar, is caused by:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Kala-azar is caused by Leishmania donovani (protozoan parasite), transmitted by the sandfly Phlebotomus argentipes. Endemic in Bihar (Muzaffarpur, Vaishali, East Champaran). Symptoms: fever, spleen/liver enlargement, darkened skin. India aims to eliminate kala-azar (target: <1 case per 10,000).
Q4. Which of the following diseases is caused by a virus?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Influenza is caused by the Influenza virus (Orthomyxoviridae). Types: A (pandemics, animal hosts), B (seasonal, humans only), C (mild). Antigenic drift (minor changes) and antigenic shift (major changes) cause new strains. Cholera, typhoid, and tetanus are bacterial diseases.
Q5. The BCG vaccine, given at birth in India, provides protection against which disease?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — BCG (Bacille Calmette-Guérin) vaccine protects against tuberculosis. Made from attenuated Mycobacterium bovis. Given at birth or soon after. Protects mainly against severe childhood TB (TB meningitis, miliary TB). Part of India's Universal Immunization Programme (UIP).
Q6. Which of the following is NOT a mode of transmission of communicable diseases?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Genetic inheritance is a mode of transmission for non-communicable (genetic) diseases, NOT communicable diseases. Communicable diseases spread via: airborne (TB, flu), vector-borne (malaria, dengue), waterborne (cholera, typhoid), foodborne, direct contact, bloodborne, and sexual transmission.
Q7. Dengue fever is transmitted by which vector?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Dengue is transmitted by Aedes aegypti mosquito (day-biting, breeds in clean stagnant water). Also transmits chikungunya, Zika, and yellow fever. 4 serotypes (DENV-1 to DENV-4). Severe form = dengue haemorrhagic fever (DHF) — plasma leakage, low platelets. No specific antiviral treatment — supportive care only.
Q8. Which of the following is a non-communicable disease (NCD)?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Diabetes mellitus is a non-communicable disease (NCD) — a chronic metabolic disorder characterised by high blood glucose. Type 1 (autoimmune, no insulin) and Type 2 (insulin resistance, lifestyle-related). NCDs include cardiovascular disease, cancer, chronic respiratory disease, and diabetes — responsible for ~71% of global deaths.
Q9. The polio vaccine used in India's Pulse Polio campaign is:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV) = Sabin vaccine (live attenuated), given as oral drops. India was declared polio-free by WHO on 25 March 2014 (last case: 13 January 2011). Pulse Polio Immunization campaign (launched 1995) — mass OPV doses to children under 5. India uses both OPV and IPV (inactivated, injectable) in the national schedule.
Q10. Cholera is caused by which pathogen and transmitted through which mode?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Cholera is caused by Vibrio cholerae bacterium, transmitted through contaminated water and food. Produces cholera toxin → massive fluid secretion into intestines → 'rice-water' stools. Treatment: ORS (oral rehydration solution) + antibiotics. Major public health concern in areas with poor sanitation. Bihar reports cholera outbreaks during floods.
Q11. Acute Encephalitis Syndrome (AES), which has caused outbreaks in Muzaffarpur, Bihar, is primarily linked to:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — AES in Muzaffarpur, Bihar has been linked to litchi fruit consumption (hypoglycin A and MCPG toxins) combined with malnutrition and heat. The toxins cause hypoglycaemia (low blood sugar) in undernourished children, leading to brain inflammation. Outbreaks occur during litchi harvest season (May-June). Prevention: ensure children eat evening meal, limit early morning litchi consumption.
Q12. Which of the following diseases is caused by a fungus?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Ringworm (tinea) is caused by dermatophyte fungi (Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton). Affects skin, nails, scalp. NOT a worm — named for the ring-shaped rash. Transmitted by direct contact, contaminated surfaces, and pets. Treated with antifungal creams. Other fungal diseases: athlete's foot, candidiasis, aspergillosis.
Q13. The DPT vaccine protects against which three diseases?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — DPT vaccine protects against Diphtheria, Pertussis (whooping cough), and Tetanus. Diphtheria = Corynebacterium diphtheriae (throat infection, pseudo-membrane). Pertussis = Bordetella pertussis (severe cough, 'whoop' sound). Tetanus = Clostridium tetani (neurotoxin, muscle spasms). Part of India's UIP. Now given as pentavalent vaccine (DPT + Hep B + Hib).
Q14. Hepatitis B is caused by a ___ and primarily affects which organ?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Hepatitis B is caused by Hepatitis B virus (HBV, a DNA virus of Hepadnaviridae family). Affects the liver — causes inflammation, can lead to cirrhosis and liver cancer. Transmitted through blood, sexual contact, mother-to-child. Vaccine available (recombinant DNA vaccine, given at birth in India). Hepatitis A and E = waterborne; Hepatitis B, C, D = bloodborne.
Q15. Which of the following is a sexually transmitted infection (STI)?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — HIV/AIDS is a sexually transmitted infection. HIV (Retrovirus) attacks CD4+ T cells (immune system), leading to AIDS. Transmitted through: sexual contact, contaminated blood, shared needles, mother-to-child. India has ~2.4 million people living with HIV (3rd highest globally). ART (antiretroviral therapy) controls but does not cure. Bihar has low HIV prevalence but vulnerable due to migration.
Q16. Tetanus is caused by a toxin produced by Clostridium tetani, which enters the body through:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Tetanus is caused by Clostridium tetani, a spore-forming bacterium found in soil, dust, and animal faeces. Spores enter through wounds/cuts (especially deep puncture wounds, rusted metal, animal bites). Bacteria produce tetanospasmin neurotoxin → muscle spasms (lockjaw/trismus, risus sardonicus, opisthotonos). Preventable with TT vaccine. High case fatality without treatment.
Q17. The National Vector Borne Disease Control Programme (NVBDCP) in India addresses which diseases?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — NVBDCP addresses 6 vector-borne diseases: Malaria, Dengue, Lymphatic Filariasis, Kala-azar (visceral leishmaniasis), Japanese Encephalitis, and Chikungunya. Bihar is endemic for kala-azar and lymphatic filariasis. India aims to eliminate kala-azar by 2025 and lymphatic filariasis by 2027. NVBDCP uses indoor residual spraying, bed nets (LLINs), and case detection-treatment.
Q18. Which of the following is an autoimmune disease (where the immune system attacks the body's own cells)?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Rheumatoid arthritis is an autoimmune disease — the immune system mistakenly attacks the synovial membranes of joints, causing inflammation, pain, and deformity. Other autoimmune diseases: Type 1 diabetes, multiple sclerosis, lupus, psoriasis. Autoimmune diseases are non-communicable — not caused by a pathogen and not transmissible between people.
Q19. Leprosy is caused by which pathogen?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Leprosy (Hansen's disease) is caused by Mycobacterium leprae, an acid-fast bacillus. Affects skin, peripheral nerves, upper respiratory tract. Transmitted through prolonged close contact via respiratory droplets. Curable with MDT (Multi-Drug Therapy: rifampicin + dapsone + clofazimine). India achieved leprosy elimination (<1 per 10,000) in 2005 but still has highest global case load. Bihar is a high-burden state.
Q20. The Ritchie-Chestnut or 'rat flea' transmits which disease?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Plague is caused by Yersinia pestis (bacterium), transmitted by the rat flea Xenopsylla cheopis. Three forms: bubonic (swollen lymph nodes/buboes), septicaemic, and pneumonic (most fatal, spread by droplets). Historically caused pandemics (Black Death, killed ~50 million in 14th century Europe). India's Surat plague outbreak (1994). Prevented by flea/rodent control and antibiotics.
Q21. Which of the following is a helminth (parasitic worm) disease?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Lymphatic filariasis (elephantiasis) is caused by Wuchereria bancrofti (a nematode/helminth), transmitted by Culex mosquito. Adult worms live in lymphatic vessels → block lymph drainage → severe swelling of limbs (elephantiasis). Bihar is endemic. India's Mass Drug Administration (MDA) programme uses DEC + albendazole. India aims to eliminate LF by 2027.
Q22. COVID-19 is caused by which pathogen?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — COVID-19 is caused by SARS-CoV-2, a coronavirus (single-stranded RNA virus, family Coronaviridae). Named for the crown-like spike proteins (corona = crown). Primarily affects the respiratory system. Transmitted through respiratory droplets and aerosols. Vaccines: Covishield (AstraZeneca/Oxford), Covaxin (Bharat Biotech), Sputnik V. India's vaccination drive started 16 January 2021.
Q23. Which of the following diseases is NOT transmitted by mosquitoes?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Typhoid is NOT mosquito-transmitted. It is caused by Salmonella typhi bacterium, transmitted through contaminated food and water (faecal-oral route). Symptoms: sustained fever, abdominal pain, rose spots, diarrhoea. Treatment: antibiotics. Preventable with typhoid vaccine. Bihar reports typhoid cases during monsoon/flood season due to water contamination.
Q24. Smallpox, the first human disease to be officially eradicated, was declared eradicated by WHO in which year?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Smallpox was declared eradicated by WHO on 8 May 1980. Caused by Variola virus. The last natural case was in Somalia (1977). Eradication achieved through global vaccination campaign using the vaccinia vaccine. Edward Jenner discovered the smallpox vaccine (1796, using cowpox virus). Smallpox is the first and only human disease to be completely eradicated. Polio is close to eradication.
Q25. India was declared polio-free by WHO in which year?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — India was declared polio-free by WHO on 25 March 2014. The last wild polio case in India was on 13 January 2011 (Howrah, West Bengal). Pulse Polio Immunization campaign (launched 1995) achieved this through mass OPV administration. India uses both OPV (Sabin, oral) and IPV (Salk, injectable) in the national immunization schedule.
