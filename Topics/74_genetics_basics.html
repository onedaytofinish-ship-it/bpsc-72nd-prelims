Eukaryotic cell structure — the nucleus contains chromosomes (DNA wrapped around histone proteins). Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs: 22 autosomes + 1 sex pair). LawAlso Known AsStatementDemonstrated By

  • Gregor Johann Mendel (1822–1884) — "Father of Genetics". Experiments on pea plant (Pisum sativum) in monastery garden, Brunn (Austria, now Brno, Czech Republic). Published 1866; rediscovered in 1900 by de Vries, Correns, von Tschermak.
  • Test cross: cross unknown genotype with homozygous recessive → reveals if unknown is homozygous dominant (all dominant offspring) or heterozygous (1:1 ratio).
  • Back cross: cross of offspring with either parent.
🧠 Mnemonic — Mendel's Ratios:
Monohybrid (one trait, Tt × Tt): Phenotype 3:1 (3 tall : 1 short), Genotype 1:2:1 (1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt)
Dihybrid (two traits, RrYy × RrYy): Phenotype 9:3:3:1 (9 round-yellow : 3 round-green : 3 wrinkled-yellow : 1 wrinkled-green)
Test cross (Tt × tt): 1:1. "3-1 mono, 9-3-3-1 di" — the golden Mendelian ratios.

Sickle cell anaemia — a single point mutation (glutamic acid → valine at position 6 of the β-globin chain) produces abnormal haemoglobin HbS. Under low oxygen, HbS polymerises, distorting RBCs into elongated "sickle" shapes. These rigid, sickled cells block small blood vessels (vaso-occlusion) → severe pain crises, organ damage, and chronic anaemia. Inherited as autosomal recessive (HbSS = disease; HbAS = trait). The sickle cell trait (HbAS) confers protection against Plasmodium falciparum malaria — a classic example of balanced polymorphism in malaria-endemic regions, including Bihar's tribal populations.

Q1. The double-helix structure of DNA was discovered by which scientists?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — James Watson and Francis Crick proposed the DNA double-helix model in 1953 (published in Nature, April 25, 1953). They used X-ray diffraction data from Rosalind Franklin (Photo 51) and Maurice Wilkins. Watson, Crick, and Wilkins shared the Nobel Prize in Medicine (1962); Franklin had died (1958) and Nobels are not awarded posthumously. The model showed two antiparallel strands with A-T and G-C base pairing inside. Pasteur/Koch were microbiologists; Mendel was the father of genetics; Lamarck/Darwin proposed evolutionary theories.
Q2. In a DNA molecule, adenine (A) always pairs with which base?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — In DNA, Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T) via 2 hydrogen bonds (A=T). Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C) via 3 hydrogen bonds (G≡C). Uracil (U) replaces Thymine in RNA — it is NOT found in DNA. Chargaff's rule: A=T and G=C. GC pairs are stronger (3 H-bonds) making GC-rich DNA more stable (higher melting temperature).
Q3. Which nitrogenous base is present in RNA but NOT in DNA?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Uracil (U) is a pyrimidine base found only in RNA, where it replaces Thymine (T) and pairs with Adenine (A). DNA contains A, T, G, C; RNA contains A, U, G, C. Both share Adenine, Guanine, and Cytosine. Uracil is energetically cheaper to produce than Thymine, but Thymine's methyl group makes DNA more stable for long-term storage. RNA is more reactive (2′-OH on ribose) and suited for short-term messaging.
Q4. The synthesis of mRNA from a DNA template is called:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Transcription is the process of synthesising mRNA from a DNA template, occurring in the nucleus (catalysed by RNA polymerase). The central dogma: DNA → (transcription) → mRNA → (translation, at ribosomes) → Protein. Replication = DNA→DNA (DNA polymerase, S-phase). Translation = mRNA→protein (ribosomes, tRNA). Transduction = gene transfer between bacteria via bacteriophages (not part of central dogma).
Q5. The process of translation (protein synthesis) occurs at which organelle?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Translation (protein synthesis) occurs at ribosomes (in cytoplasm or on rough ER). The ribosome reads mRNA codons (3-nucleotide units); tRNA molecules bring the matching amino acids (anticodon pairs with codon); rRNA is a structural component of the ribosome. Nucleus = transcription/replication. Mitochondria = cellular respiration (ATP). Golgi = modifies, sorts, and packages proteins (post-translational).
Q6. Mendel's Law of Segregation is also known as:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Mendel's Law of Segregation is also called the "Law of Purity of Gametes": during gamete formation (meiosis), the two alleles for a gene separate, and each gamete receives only one allele. A Tt heterozygote produces 50% T gametes and 50% t gametes — gametes are "pure" (carry only one allele). This law is demonstrated by the monohybrid cross (Tt × Tt → 3:1 phenotype, 1:2:1 genotype). Law of Dominance = one allele masks another. Independent Assortment = different genes assort independently (dihybrid cross).
Q7. In a monohybrid cross between two heterozygous tall pea plants (Tt × Tt), the phenotypic ratio of the offspring is:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — In a monohybrid cross Tt × Tt, offspring genotypes are 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt (genotypic ratio 1:2:1). Since T (tall) is dominant over t (short), TT and Tt both appear tall → phenotypic ratio is 3 tall : 1 short (3:1). The 1:2:1 ratio is genotypic. The 1:1 ratio is seen in a test cross (Tt × tt). Mendel observed this 3:1 ratio in F₂ generation of pea plants for seven traits.
Q8. In a dihybrid cross (RrYy × RrYy), what is the phenotypic ratio in the F₂ generation?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — The dihybrid cross RrYy × RrYy gives a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio in F₂: 9 round-yellow (R_Y_), 3 round-green (R_yy), 3 wrinkled-yellow (rrY_), 1 wrinkled-green (rryy). This ratio demonstrates Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment (alleles of different genes assort independently during gamete formation). 3:1 is the monohybrid ratio. 1:1:1:1 is the test cross of a dihybrid. 9:7 is a modified ratio from complementary gene interaction (gene interaction deviations from Mendelian ratios).
Q9. The genotypic ratio of a monohybrid cross (Tt × Tt) in the F₂ generation is:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The genotypic ratio of a monohybrid cross (Tt × Tt) is 1:2:1 — 1 homozygous dominant (TT), 2 heterozygous (Tt), 1 homozygous recessive (tt). The phenotypic ratio is 3:1 (TT and Tt look the same = tall; tt = short). Many students confuse phenotype (appearance) with genotype (genetic makeup). The 1:2:1 genotypic ratio arises from random fertilisation of T and t gametes in equal proportions.
Q10. In humans, the sex of a child is determined by which parent?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — In humans, sex is determined by the father. Females are XX (homogametic — all eggs carry X). Males are XY (heterogametic — 50% sperm carry X, 50% carry Y). If an X sperm fertilises the egg → XX → daughter. If a Y sperm fertilises the egg → XY → son. The mother always contributes an X chromosome; the father's sperm determines sex. The Y chromosome carries the SRY gene (Sex-determining Region Y) that triggers male development; its absence leads to female development (default pathway).
Q11. Haemophilia is an example of which type of inheritance?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Haemophilia is X-linked recessive. Males (XY) are hemizygous for X — a single affected X causes disease. Females (XX) need two affected Xs to show disease (one affected X = carrier). Males cannot pass it to sons (pass Y) but pass the affected X to all daughters (carriers). Haemophilia A = factor VIII deficiency (most common); Haemophilia B = factor IX deficiency. Known as the "Royal disease" — spread through European royalty via Queen Victoria (a carrier). Sickle cell and thalassemia are autosomal recessive, not X-linked.
Q12. Red-green colour blindness is inherited in which pattern?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Red-green colour blindness is X-linked recessive (defect in red/green opsin genes on the X chromosome). Males (XY) express it with one affected X (~8% of males affected). Females (XX) need two affected Xs to be colour blind (<1% females); one affected X = carrier. A colour-blind father cannot pass it to his sons (passes Y) but all his daughters will be carriers. Same inheritance pattern as haemophilia. Y-linked traits are very rare (passed only father→son, e.g., hairy ears). Mitochondrial traits pass only mother→all children.
Q13. Down syndrome is caused by which chromosomal abnormality?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Down syndrome is caused by Trisomy 21 (three copies of chromosome 21 instead of two) — total 47 chromosomes. Features: mental retardation, flat facial profile, epicanthal folds, upward-slanting eyes, single palmar crease (simian crease), congenital heart defects, hypotonia. Risk increases with maternal age (especially ≥35 years). It is the most common autosomal aneuploidy. Turner = 45,X0 (monosomy X). Klinefelter = 47,XXY. Cri-du-chat = deletion of short arm of chromosome 5.
Q14. A person with Turner syndrome has which karyotype?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Turner syndrome = 45, X0 (only one X chromosome; the other sex chromosome is missing). Phenotype: female, short stature, webbed neck, broad chest, no puberty (no ovarian development), sterile, normal intelligence, coarctation of aorta. It is the only viable human monosomy (missing an entire autosome is lethal). Klinefelter = 47,XXY (male). Triple-X = 47, three-X (female, usually mild). XYY = 47,XYY (male, tall, usually normal phenotype).
Q15. Klinefelter syndrome is characterised by which karyotype?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Klinefelter syndrome = 47, XXY (an extra X chromosome in a male). Features: male phenotype, tall stature, long limbs, gynaecomastia (enlarged breasts), small firm testes, azoospermia (sterile), low testosterone, elevated FSH/LH. It is the most common sex chromosome aneuploidy (~1 in 500–1000 males). Intelligence is usually normal or mildly reduced. Turner = 45,X0 (female). XYY = 47,XYY (male, tall, usually normal). 46,XX = normal female.
Q16. Sickle cell anaemia is inherited in which pattern?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Sickle cell anaemia is autosomal recessive. The β-globin gene is on chromosome 11 (an autosome, not a sex chromosome). HbSS = disease (homozygous recessive); HbAS = sickle cell trait (heterozygous carrier, generally asymptomatic); HbAA = normal. Two carrier parents (HbAS × HbAS) have 25% chance of HbSS child (disease), 50% HbAS (trait), 25% HbAA (normal). The sickle cell trait (HbAS) confers resistance to Plasmodium falciparum malaria — balanced polymorphism. Common in Bihar's tribal populations.
Q17. The mutation causing sickle cell anaemia involves substitution of which amino acid?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Sickle cell anaemia is caused by a single point mutation in the β-globin gene (chromosome 11): the codon GAG (glutamic acid) changes to GTG (valine) at position 6 of the β-globin chain. This produces abnormal haemoglobin HbS (instead of normal HbA). Under low oxygen, HbS polymerises → RBCs distort into sickle shapes → block capillaries (pain crises, organ damage). This is a classic example of a single-nucleotide point mutation causing disease. Linus Pauling identified HbS by electrophoresis (1949) — first molecular disease.
Q18. Thalassemia is a genetic disorder affecting which of the following?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Thalassemia is an autosomal recessive disorder affecting haemoglobin synthesis — either reduced α-globin (α-thalassemia) or β-globin (β-thalassemia) chain production. This results in defective haemoglobin, microcytic hypochromic anaemia, and (in major forms) lifelong blood transfusion dependency + iron chelation. Common in Mediterranean, Middle East, South Asia (including Bihar). Prevention: premarital carrier screening, prenatal diagnosis. Haemophilia = clotting factor deficiency (VIII/IX). Diabetes = insulin. Colour blindness = opsin genes.
Q19. In a pedigree analysis, if a trait appears in every generation and affects both sexes equally, the most likely inheritance pattern is:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Autosomal dominant traits appear in every generation (no skipping), affect both sexes equally, and affected individuals have a 50% chance of passing the trait to each child. Examples: Huntington's disease, achondroplasia, Marfan syndrome. X-linked recessive skips generations and affects males more. Mitochondrial inheritance passes only from mother to all children (no father-to-child transmission). Y-linked traits affect only males (father→all sons). Recognising pedigree patterns is a key BPSC skill.
Q20. Which type of RNA carries amino acids to the ribosome during translation?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — tRNA (transfer RNA) carries specific amino acids to the ribosome during translation. Each tRNA has an anticodon (3 nucleotides) that is complementary to an mRNA codon. The tRNA matches its anticodon to the codon on mRNA at the ribosome, delivering the correct amino acid to the growing polypeptide chain. mRNA = messenger (carries the genetic code from DNA to ribosome). rRNA = ribosomal (structural + catalytic component of ribosome). snRNA = small nuclear RNA (involved in splicing introns from pre-mRNA).
Q21. How many chromosomes are present in a normal human somatic (body) cell?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Normal human somatic (body) cells are diploid (2n) with 46 chromosomes = 23 pairs: 22 pairs of autosomes (same in males and females) + 1 pair of sex chromosomes (XX in females, XY in males). Gametes (sperm, egg) are haploid (n) with 23 chromosomes (22 autosomes + 1 sex chromosome). Meiosis halves the chromosome number (46→23) during gamete formation; fertilisation restores it (23+23=46). Chimpanzees have 48 chromosomes (24 pairs) — humans have 46 due to a chromosome fusion event during evolution.
Q22. DNA replication is described as "semi-conservative" because:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — DNA replication is semi-conservative: each daughter DNA molecule contains one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesised strand. This was proven by the Meselson–Stahl experiment (1958) using 15N/14N labelling of E. coli DNA. The conservative model (option A) proposed one all-old and one all-new molecule. The dispersive model (option B) proposed a mix of old and new in both strands. Semi-conservative replication ensures faithful copying of genetic information and is universal across all life.
Q23. Gregor Mendel, the "Father of Genetics," performed his landmark experiments on which organism?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Gregor Johann Mendel (1822–1884), an Augustinian friar, performed his experiments on the garden pea (Pisum sativum) in the monastery garden at Brunn (now Brno, Czech Republic). He studied 7 contrasting traits (tall/short, round/wrinkled seed, yellow/green cotyledon, etc.) over 8 years (~29,000 pea plants). Published "Experiments on Plant Hybridisation" in 1866 — largely ignored until rediscovered in 1900 by de Vries, Correns, and von Tschermak. Drosophila was used by Thomas Hunt Morgan (chromosome theory of inheritance, Nobel 1933).
Q24. A "test cross" is performed by crossing an organism of unknown genotype with which type of organism?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — A test cross crosses an organism of unknown genotype (showing dominant phenotype, could be TT or Tt) with a homozygous recessive organism (tt). If all offspring are tall (dominant) → unknown was TT (homozygous dominant). If offspring are 1:1 tall:short → unknown was Tt (heterozygous). The homozygous recessive parent contributes only recessive alleles, so the offspring's phenotype directly reveals the unknown parent's gametes. This is a classic Mendelian technique to determine genotype.
Q25. Makhana (Euryale ferox), genetically researched at Darbhanga (Bihar), is significant because:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Makhana (Euryale ferox) is an aquatic crop cultivated in ponds and wetlands of North Bihar (Mithila region — Darbhanga, Madhubani, Saharsa, Supaul). Bihar produces >85% of India's makhana. "Mithila Makhana" received a GI tag in 2010. The Research Institute for Makhana (ICAR-RIU), Darbhanga works on germplasm conservation, genetic improvement, polyploidy, and high-yielding/disease-resistant varieties. Makhana is rich in protein, low in fat, and a premium health food — genetics research (selective breeding, polyploidy) improves yield and quality. It is not cotton, silk, or wheat.
