Photosynthesis drives the carbon cycle and energy flow in ecosystems. Producers (plants, algae, cyanobacteria) convert solar energy (~1% fixed) into chemical energy (glucose) via 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. This energy flows through trophic levels with ~10% transfer efficiency (Lindemann's 10% law). Respiration returns CO₂ to atmosphere, completing the carbon cycle.

🧠 Mnemonic — Trophic Levels (PCSTD):
Producer → Consumer (primary) → Secondary → Tertiary → Decomposers
Energy flows: Sun → Producer → Herbivore → Carnivore → Top carnivore. Decomposers recycle nutrients at every level. Only ~10% passes to the next level.

E. Energy Flow & 10% Law PYQ PATTERN

Trophic LevelEnergy AvailableExample% of Previous Level
Producers (T1)10,000 kcalGrass~1% of solar input fixed
Primary consumers (T2)1,000 kcalGrasshopper10% of T1
Secondary consumers (T3)100 kcalFrog10% of T2
Tertiary consumers (T4)10 kcalSnake10% of T3
Top carnivore (T5)1 kcalEagle10% of T4
  • Lindemann's 10% Law (1942): Only ~10% of energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next; ~90% is lost as heat (respiration, metabolism, undigested matter). This limits food chains to 4–5 trophic levels.
  • Energy flow is unidirectional (Sun → producers → consumers → heat). Nutrients cycle; energy does not cycle.
  • Gross Primary Productivity (GPP): total photosynthesis. Net Primary Productivity (NPP): GPP − respiration = energy available to consumers.

H. Ecological Succession

FeaturePrimary SuccessionSecondary Succession

TimeVery slow (hundreds–thousands of years)Faster (decades) — soil & seeds already present

  • Seral community: intermediate stage in succession. Climax community: stable, self-perpetuating final stage.
  • Pioneer species: first to colonize — lichens (primary), grasses (secondary). Lichens are "soil formers" — secrete acids that weather rock.
  • Hydrarch: succession in water (pond → marsh → meadow → forest). Xerarch: succession in dry areas (rock → lichen → moss → grass → shrub → forest).

  • Biodiversity Hotspot concept: by Norman Myers (1988). Criteria: (1) ≥1,500 endemic vascular plant species, AND (2) ≥30% original natural vegetation lost (threatened). Currently 36 hotspots globally; cover 2.4% of Earth's land but support >50% of endemic plant species.

  • Megadiverse countries: 17 countries hold ~70% of global biodiversity. India is one of them.

Q1. Restriction enzymes used in recombinant DNA technology are also called:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Restriction enzymes (restriction endonucleases) are called "molecular scissors" because they cut DNA at specific recognition sites (palindromic sequences). EcoRI cuts between G and A in GAATTC, leaving sticky ends. DNA ligase is the "molecular glue" that joins fragments. Together they enable recombinant DNA (first done by Cohen & Boyer, 1973).
Q2. PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) was invented by:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Kary Mullis invented PCR in 1983 (Nobel Chemistry 1993). PCR uses Taq polymerase (thermostable, from Thermus aquaticus found in Yellowstone hot springs) to amplify DNA through cycles of denaturation (95°C), annealing (55°C), and extension (72°C). Used in COVID-19 RT-PCR testing, DNA fingerprinting, and forensics.
Q3. The "Bt" in Bt cotton refers to:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Bt cotton gets its name from Bacillus thuringiensis, a soil bacterium. The cry (crystal) gene from this bacterium produces the Cry protein toxin, which is lethal to bollworm (cotton pest) but safe for humans. Bt cotton was India's first (and so far only) approved GM crop for commercial cultivation (2002, approved by GEAC). It is NOT a food crop.
Q4. Golden Rice is genetically modified to produce:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Golden Rice is engineered to produce β-carotene (pro-vitamin A), giving the grains a golden colour. Developed by Ingo Potrykus and Peter Beyer (1999), using the psy gene (from daffodil) and crtI gene (from bacterium Erwinia uredovora). The body converts β-carotene to vitamin A. Aim: combat vitamin A deficiency (leading cause of childhood blindness). Approved in Philippines (2021) and Bangladesh.
Q5. Recombinant insulin (Humulin), the first commercial product of rDNA technology, was produced in which organism?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Recombinant human insulin (Humulin) was the first commercial product of rDNA technology (1982, Eli Lilly). The human insulin gene was inserted into E. coli, which then produced human insulin. Developed by Genentech. This replaced animal-derived insulin (from pig/cow pancreas), eliminating allergy and supply issues. The A and B chains were produced separately and then joined.
Q6. CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in which year and for whom?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry (2020) for developing CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing — the first time two women alone shared a science Nobel. CRISPR-Cas9 acts as "genetic scissors": a guide RNA directs the Cas9 nuclease to a specific DNA sequence, where it makes a precise cut. Derived from a bacterial adaptive immune system against viruses (phages). Note: Feng Zhang (Broad Institute) pioneered mammalian applications but was not on the Nobel.
Q7. Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) were developed by which scientist?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Shinya Yamanaka developed iPSCs in 2006 (Nobel Medicine 2012, shared with John Gurdon). He reprogrammed adult mouse skin cells (fibroblasts) back to pluripotent state using 4 transcription factors (Yamanaka factors: Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc). iPSCs behave like embryonic stem cells but avoid ethical issues (no embryo destroyed) and can be patient-specific (no immune rejection). Thomson (1998) derived the first human ESC lines. Wilmut cloned Dolly (1996). Edwards pioneered IVF (Nobel 2010).
Q8. According to Lindemann's 10% law of energy flow, if producers receive 10,000 kcal of energy, how much reaches the secondary consumer (T3)?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Lindemann's 10% law (1942): only ~10% of energy at one trophic level transfers to the next (90% lost as heat, respiration, undigested matter). Producers (T1) = 10,000 kcal → Primary consumers (T2) = 1,000 kcal (10% of 10,000) → Secondary consumers (T3) = 100 kcal (10% of 1,000). This explains why food chains are limited to 4–5 levels — insufficient energy remains at the top.
Q9. Which ecological pyramid is ALWAYS upright and can NEVER be inverted?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The pyramid of energy is always upright because energy decreases at each trophic level (Lindemann's 10% law — only 10% transfers, 90% lost as heat/respiration). Energy flow is unidirectional (Sun → producers → consumers → heat), so it cannot increase at higher levels. Pyramid of numbers can be inverted (one tree → many insects). Pyramid of biomass is inverted in aquatic ecosystems (phytoplankton have high turnover but low standing biomass).
Q10. In the nitrogen cycle, which bacterium converts ammonia (NH₃) to nitrite (NO₂⁻)?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Nitrosomonas performs the first step of nitrification: ammonia (NH₃) → nitrite (NO₂⁻). Nitrobacter performs the second step: nitrite (NO₂⁻) → nitrate (NO₃⁻). Plants absorb nitrate. Rhizobium fixes N₂ symbiotically in legume root nodules. Azotobacter is a free-living N₂ fixer. Pseudomonas denitrificans returns NO₃⁻ to N₂ (denitrification), completing the cycle.
Q11. In primary ecological succession on bare rock, which are the pioneer species?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — In primary succession (bare rock, no soil), lichens are the pioneer species. Crustose lichens (e.g., Rhizocarpon) attach to rock and secrete acids that weather it, beginning soil formation. Sequence: crustose lichens → foliose lichens → mosses → grasses → shrubs → trees (climax forest). Lichens are "soil formers" — symbiotic association of fungus + algae/cyanobacteria. In secondary succession (soil present), grasses/weeds are pioneers.
Q12. The Ganges river dolphin (Platanista gangetica) is classified as India's:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — The Ganges river dolphin (Platanista gangetica) was declared India's National Aquatic Animal in 2009. It is IUCN Endangered, blind (navigates by echolocation), and found in the Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna river system. The Vikramshila Gangetic Dolphin Sanctuary in Bhagalpur, Bihar (50 km stretch of Ganga) is Asia's only dolphin sanctuary. Bihar's Ganga stretch (~445 km) is a key habitat. Namami Gange Programme focuses on its conservation. It is also the State Animal of... actually, the state animal of Bihar is the ox/gaur, not the dolphin.
Q13. Which of the following is India's only GM crop approved for commercial cultivation (as of 2024)?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Bt cotton (approved 2002 by GEAC) is India's only GM crop approved for commercial cultivation. It carries the cry gene from Bacillus thuringiensis for bollworm resistance. Bt brinjal was approved by GEAC (2009) but a moratorium was imposed (2010) by then Environment Minister Jairam Ramesh. GM mustard DMH-11 (Delhi University) received GEAC conditional approval (2022) but final commercial cultivation is still pending. Golden Rice is not approved in India.
Q14. Eutrophication of water bodies is caused by excessive input of which nutrients?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Eutrophication is caused by excessive nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) from agricultural fertilizers, sewage, and detergents entering water bodies. This nutrient enrichment triggers algal blooms → algae block sunlight → submerged plants die → algae die & decompose → bacteria consume dissolved O₂ (BOD↑, DO↓) → fish & aquatic life die. Key indicators: high BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand) and low DO (Dissolved Oxygen).
Q15. Biodiversity hotspots are regions that satisfy which two criteria (as defined by Norman Myers)?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Norman Myers (1988) defined biodiversity hotspots by two criteria: (1) ≥1,500 endemic vascular plant species (found nowhere else), AND (2) ≥30% of original natural vegetation lost (threatened by human activity). Currently 36 hotspots globally (updated from original 25), covering just 2.4% of Earth's land but supporting >50% of the world's endemic plant species. India has 4 hotspots: Himalaya, Western Ghats, Indo-Burma (NE India), and Sundaland (Nicobar Islands).
Q16. The ozone layer depletion is primarily caused by which class of chemicals?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) are the primary cause of ozone layer depletion. In the stratosphere, UV radiation breaks CFCs, releasing chlorine radicals (Cl•) that catalytically destroy ozone (O₃): one Cl atom can destroy ~100,000 O₃ molecules. The ozone hole is most pronounced over Antarctica (spring). The Montreal Protocol (1987) phased out CFCs — the most successful environmental treaty. Ozone layer projected to recover by 2040 (global) and 2066 (Antarctica). CO₂ and CH₄ are greenhouse gases (climate change), not ozone depleters. SO₂ causes acid rain.
Q17. Biomagnification refers to:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Biomagnification (bioaccumulation) is the increase in concentration of persistent, non-biodegradable pollutants (like DDT, mercury, PCBs) as they move up the food chain. Because these toxins are fat-soluble and not excreted, they accumulate in organisms. Top predators have the highest concentrations. Classic example: DDT in fish-eating birds (e.g., bald eagle) → eggshell thinning → population decline (Rachel Carson's "Silent Spring," 1962). Minamata disease (Japan) = mercury biomagnification in seafood.
Q18. Which statement correctly distinguishes a food chain from a food web?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — A food chain is a single linear sequence showing "who eats whom" (e.g., grass → grasshopper → frog → snake → eagle). A food web is a complex, interconnected network of many overlapping food chains in an ecosystem — more realistic because most organisms eat multiple things and are eaten by multiple predators. Food webs show the true complexity of energy flow. Both start with producers. Decomposers operate alongside both (not typically shown in chain). Food chains are usually limited to 4–5 levels (10% energy law).
Q19. In the IUCN Red List, which three categories collectively constitute "threatened species"?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — IUCN Red List "threatened species" = Vulnerable (VU) + Endangered (EN) + Critically Endangered (CR). The full sequence from least to most threatened: LC (Least Concern) → NT (Near Threatened) → VU → EN → CR → EW (Extinct in the Wild) → EX (Extinct). Examples: Bengal tiger = EN, Snow leopard = VU, Great Indian Bustard = CR, Ganges dolphin = EN.
Q20. NRC Makhana, India's only dedicated makhana research institute, is located in which Bihar city?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The ICAR-National Research Centre for Makhana (NRC Makhana) is in Darbhanga, Bihar (established 2002). It is India's only dedicated makhana (fox nut, Euryale ferox) research institute. Research areas: tissue culture, variety improvement (Makhana-1, Darbhanga Makhana-1), disease resistance, post-harvest processing. Bihar produces >85% of India's makhana. Mithila Makhana received a GI tag in 2022. RAU/RPCAU Pusa (Samastipur) is a separate agricultural university.
Q21. Valmiki Tiger Reserve, Bihar's only tiger reserve, is located in which district?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Valmiki Tiger Reserve (VTR) is in West Champaran district, Bihar, in the Himalayan foothills (Terai region) near the Nepal border. Established as a tiger reserve in 1994. Core area: ~335 sq km; buffer: ~580 sq km. Tiger population ~54 (2022 estimate, growing). Also hosts leopards, sloth bears, Indian bison (gaur), sambar, chital, and 241 bird species. Part of the Terai Arc Landscape. Named after sage Valmiki (author of the Ramayana). It is Bihar's only tiger reserve. East Champaran is Motihari (Champaran Satyagraha, 1917).
Q22. The concept of biodiversity hotspots was introduced by which scientist?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Norman Myers introduced the biodiversity hotspot concept in 1988 (Conservation International later adopted & expanded it). Criteria: ≥1,500 endemic vascular plant species AND ≥30% original habitat lost. Currently 36 hotspots globally. India has 4: Himalaya, Western Ghats, Indo-Burma (NE India), Sundaland (Nicobar). E.O. Wilson coined "biodiversity" and is a conservation icon but did not define hotspots. Rachel Carson raised pesticide awareness (Silent Spring). J.C. Bose pioneered plant physiology.
Q23. The phosphorus cycle differs from the carbon and nitrogen cycles because:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The phosphorus cycle is unique because it has no significant atmospheric (gaseous) phase. Phosphorus (P) remains mostly in rocks, sediments, and soil. Weathering releases phosphate (PO₄³⁻) → plants absorb it → moves through food chain → decomposers return it to soil → eventually returns to ocean sediments (long geological timescale). This makes it the slowest biogeochemical cycle. Phosphorus is a limiting nutrient in many ecosystems (especially freshwater). Key for DNA, RNA, ATP, phospholipids, bones. Carbon and nitrogen cycles both have major atmospheric phases (CO₂, N₂).
Q24. Bioremediation of oil spills utilizes bacteria that break down hydrocarbons. Which bacterium is specifically known for this?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Alcanivorax borkumensis is a marine bacterium that dominates oil-polluted waters and degrades alkanes (a major component of crude oil). It became abundant after the Deepwater Horizon spill (2010, Gulf of Mexico). Other oil-degraders include Pseudomonas species. Bioremediation also uses Ideonella sakaiensis (PET plastic degradation, discovered 2016) and Pseudomonas putida (mercury cleanup). Lactobacillus (dairy), Rhizobium (N₂ fixation), and Nitrosomonas (nitrification) serve other ecological roles.
Q25. Which of the following correctly lists India's 4 biodiversity hotspots?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — India's 4 biodiversity hotspots (of 36 globally): (1) Himalaya (Eastern Himalaya — high endemism), (2) Western Ghats (~5,000 plant species, 139 mammals — UNESCO World Heritage), (3) Indo-Burma (NE India — Assam, Manipur, Mizoram, etc.), (4) Sundaland (Nicobar Islands — part of the larger Sundaland hotspot including Indonesia/Malaysia). Eastern Ghats, Deccan, Thar, Sundarbans, and Lakshadweep are biodiversity-rich but do NOT meet the hotspot criteria (≥1,500 endemic vascular plants + ≥30% habitat loss). India is also a megadiverse country (1 of 17 globally).
