Blood, Immunity & Vaccines — Blood Groups, Immunity, Vaccination

📚 Core Content
EXAM TIP Blood, Immunity & Vaccines (high-yield Tier A): Blood components: RBCs (haemoglobin, O₂ transport, no nucleus, 120-day lifespan, produced in bone marrow, destroyed in spleen), WBCs (5 types — neutrophils, lymphocytes B & T, monocytes, eosinophils, basophils), platelets (clotting — fibrinogen→fibrin). Blood groups: ABO (A, B, AB, O) + Rh factor (+/−). AB = universal recipient, O− = universal donor. Immunity: innate (born with) vs adaptive (acquired — active: infection/vaccine, passive: mother/antiserum). Vaccines: live attenuated (BCG, OPV, MMR), inactivated (rabies, pertussis), toxoid (tetanus, diphtheria), subunit (Hep B, HPV), mRNA (COVID-19). Universal Immunisation Programme (UIP) — 12 vaccines.

Blood = 55% plasma + 45% cells. Total blood volume: ~5 litres in adults.

  • Rh Factor: Rh+ (antigen present, 85% people) vs Rh− (no antigen, 15%). Rh− person receiving Rh+ blood → anti-Rh antibodies → haemolytic reaction on second exposure.
  • Haemolytic Disease of Newborn (Erythroblastosis fetalis): Rh− mother + Rh+ father → Rh+ fetus. First delivery: sensitisation. Second Rh+ pregnancy: maternal anti-Rh antibodies cross placenta → fetal RBC destruction. Prevention: Anti-D (RhoGAM) injection to mother within 72 hours of delivery.
  • ABO system discovered by Karl Landsteiner (1900) — Nobel Prize 1930. Rh factor discovered by Landsteiner & Wiener (1937) using Rhesus monkey blood.
🧠 Mnemonic — Blood Groups:
AB = Accept Both (universal recipient — receives from all)
O = Offer to all (universal donor — donates to all)
"AB accepts, O offers" — the golden rule of blood transfusion.

🔶 Bihar Connection
Bihar — Vector-Borne & Infectious Diseases, Vaccination Programmes
  • Kala-azar (Visceral Leishmaniasis): Bihar accounts for >70% of India's kala-azar cases. Caused by Leishmania donovani, transmitted by sandfly (Phlebotomus argentipes). Endemic in 33 districts (Muzaffarpur, Vaishali, East Champaran). Symptoms: fever, splenomegaly, pancytopenia, hyperpigmentation ("black fever"). Treatment: Amphotericin B (liposomal), Miltefosine (oral). Rajendra Memorial Research Institute (RMRI), Patna — premier kala-azar research centre. India's Kala-azar Elimination target: 2025.
  • Acute Encephalitis Syndrome (AES): Muzaffarpur and adjoining districts face recurring AES outbreaks (May–July). Multiple causes: litchi toxin (hypoglycin A & MCPG → hypoglycaemia in malnourished children), heat stroke, Japanese Encephalitis (JE) virus. SKMCH Muzaffarpur is the main treatment centre. Government response: JE vaccination in endemic districts, nutrition supplementation, awareness campaigns.
  • Sickle Cell Anaemia: prevalent among tribal populations in Bihar (Santhal, Oraon, Munda). HbS mutation (glutamic acid → valine at position 6 of β-globin chain). Sickle cell trait (HbAS) confers malaria resistance — balanced polymorphism. AIIMS Patna and IGIMS provide sickle cell screening and treatment.
  • Tuberculosis (TB): Bihar is a high-burden state. NTEP (National TB Elimination Programme, formerly RNTCP) — DOTS therapy (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course). India's TB elimination target: 2025 (SDG target: 2030). Nikshay Poshan Yojana: ₹500/month to TB patients for nutrition.
  • Lymphatic Filariasis (Elephantiasis): caused by Wuchereria bancrofti, transmitted by Culex mosquito. Bihar is endemic. Mass Drug Administration (MDA) with DEC + Albendazole. Target: elimination by 2027 (revised from 2020).
  • Leprosy: Bihar is among high-burden states. National Leprosy Eradication Programme (NLEP). Multi-Drug Therapy (MDT): Dapsone + Rifampicin + Clofazimine. Bihar achieved elimination threshold (<1/10,000) but pockets of high prevalence remain.
  • COVID-19 vaccination: Bihar administered >15 crore doses. Gargi Sansthan and AIIMS Patna were key centres. Rural outreach via Mobile Vaccination Teams.

📰 Current Relevance
  • COVID-19 vaccines (2020–2026): fastest vaccine development in history. mRNA vaccines (Pfizer, Moderna) — Karikó & Weissman won Nobel Prize 2023 for mRNA technology. India: Covishield (viral vector), Covaxin (inactivated), Corbevax (protein subunit), GEMCOVAC-19 (mRNA, first indigenous, 2023).
  • Polio eradication: India polio-free since 2014. OPV being phased out globally (risk of VDPV — Vaccine-Derived Polio Virus). Switch from trivalent OPV to bivalent OPV (2016). Fractional IPV introduced in India (2024–25).
  • Malaria vaccine: WHO recommended RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix) in 2021 — world's first malaria vaccine. R21/Matrix-M (Oxford-Serum Institute India) approved 2023 — cheaper, higher efficacy (75%). Deployed in Africa. Relevant for Bihar (malaria endemic).
  • HPV vaccination: Cervavac (Serum Institute India, 2023) — first indigenous HPV vaccine. ₹2,000/dose (vs imported ₹3,500+). India launched HPV vaccination for girls 9–14 years (2024). Prevents cervical cancer (HPV types 16, 18 cause 70% of cases).
  • Universal Immunisation Programme expansion: Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine (PCV) launched nationally 2021. Rotavirus vaccine (2016). JE vaccine in endemic districts. India aims to eliminate measles & rubella by 2025.
  • Bihar kala-azar elimination: India committed to eliminate kala-azar by 2025 (revised from 2017, 2020). Active case detection, indoor residual spraying (IRS), sandfly control. Cases declined from >77,000 (2007) to <1,000 (2024).

Q1. Which blood group is known as the "universal donor"?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Blood group O has no A or B antigens on RBCs, so it does not trigger anti-A or anti-B antibodies in any recipient. O− is the true universal donor (no Rh antigen either). However, O+ can donate to any Rh+ recipient. AB+ is the universal recipient (no anti-A, anti-B, or anti-Rh antibodies).
Q2. The ABO blood group system was discovered by which scientist?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Karl Landsteiner discovered the ABO blood group system in 1900 (Nobel Prize 1930). He also co-discovered the Rh factor (1937) with Alexander Wiener, using Rhesus monkey blood. Landsteiner's classification enabled safe blood transfusions.
Q3. Red blood cells (RBCs) do NOT contain which of the following?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Mature mammalian RBCs (including humans) lack a nucleus. They extrude their nucleus during maturation in bone marrow (erythropoiesis) to accommodate more haemoglobin (~270 million Hb molecules per RBC) and increase O₂ carrying capacity. Biconcave shape increases surface area for gas exchange. Lifespan: 120 days. Produced in bone marrow, destroyed in spleen and liver.
Q4. Which antibody class crosses the placenta and provides immunity to the newborn?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — IgG is the only antibody class that crosses the placenta (via the FcRn receptor on placental cells). This provides passive immunity to the newborn for the first 3–6 months of life. IgG is also the most abundant antibody (75–80% of serum Ig). IgA is passed via breast milk (colostrum) — protects the infant's gut.
Q5. Which type of immunity is provided by vaccination?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Vaccination provides artificial active immunity: the vaccine introduces a weakened/killed pathogen or its antigen → the body produces its own antibodies and memory B/T cells → long-lasting immunity. Natural active = getting the infection. Artificial passive = receiving pre-made antibodies (antiserum, e.g., snake bite, tetanus immunoglobulin). Natural passive = maternal IgG via placenta/IgA via breast milk.
Q6. The BCG vaccine provides protection against which disease?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — BCG (Bacille Calmette-Guérin) vaccine protects against tuberculosis (TB). It is a live attenuated vaccine derived from Mycobacterium bovis (attenuated by Albert Calmette and Camille Guérin, 1921). Given at birth (or within first year) in India. BCG also protects against leprosy (cross-protection) and is used in bladder cancer treatment (intravesical BCG).
Q7. B-lymphocytes mature in which organ?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — B-lymphocytes mature in the bone marrow (in mammals). "B" originally stood for the Bursa of Fabricius (a bird organ where B-cells were first discovered). In mammals, B-cells mature in the bone marrow. T-lymphocytes mature in the thymus. Both originate from haematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow.
Q8. Which WBC type is most abundant in human blood?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Neutrophils are the most abundant WBC (60–70%). They are the first responders to bacterial infection and perform phagocytosis. "Neutrophilia" (high neutrophil count) indicates bacterial infection. Order of abundance: Neutrophils > Lymphocytes > Monocytes > Eosinophils > Basophils. Mnemonic: "Never Let Monkeys Eat Bananas."
Q9. Which vaccine type is the tetanus vaccine (TT)?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Tetanus vaccine is a toxoid vaccine. The tetanus toxoid is an inactivated form of the tetanus toxin (produced by Clostridium tetani). The vaccine stimulates antibodies against the toxin, not the bacterium. Given as DPT (Diphtheria + Pertussis + Tetanus) in infancy, with boosters (Td) at 10 years and pregnancy. Diphtheria vaccine is also a toxoid.
Q10. Kala-azar (visceral leishmaniasis), endemic in Bihar, is caused by which pathogen and transmitted by which vector?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Kala-azar (visceral leishmaniasis) is caused by the protozoan Leishmania donovani and transmitted by the sandfly Phlebotomus argentipes. Bihar accounts for >70% of India's kala-azar cases (33 endemic districts). Symptoms: fever, splenomegaly (enlarged spleen), pancytopenia, hyperpigmentation ("black fever" = kala-azar). Treatment: liposomal Amphotericin B, Miltefosine. RMRI Patna is the premier research centre. India's elimination target: 2025.
Q11. Which antibody is the FIRST produced during a primary immune response?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — IgM is the first antibody produced in a primary immune response (new infection). It is a pentamer (5 immunoglobulin units) — the largest antibody. IgM is the most efficient activator of the complement system. IgG is produced later (secondary response) and is the most abundant. Diagnosis of recent infection: high IgM. Diagnosis of past infection/vaccination: high IgG.
Q12. The Rh factor is named after which animal?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The Rh factor (Rhesus factor) was discovered by Karl Landsteiner and Alexander Wiener in 1937 using blood from Rhesus monkeys. Rh+ (85% of people) have the Rh antigen on their RBCs; Rh− (15%) do not. Rh incompatibility can cause haemolytic disease of the newborn (erythroblastosis fetalis) — prevented by Anti-D (RhoGAM) injection to Rh− mothers.
Q13. Match the vaccine with its type: 1. BCG 2. Tetanus 3. Hepatitis B 4. Rabies
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — BCG = Live attenuated (Mycobacterium bovis, against TB). Tetanus = Toxoid (inactivated tetanus toxin). Hepatitis B = Recombinant subunit (first recombinant vaccine, 1986 — HBsAg produced in yeast). Rabies = Inactivated (killed virus — HDCV, Human Diploid Cell Vaccine; given post-exposure for dog bite).
Q14. Which cells are primarily attacked by the HIV virus?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) primarily attacks Helper T-lymphocytes (CD4+ T cells) by binding to the CD4 receptor and CCR5/CXCR4 co-receptors. Destruction of CD4+ T cells cripples the immune system → AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome). Normal CD4 count: 500–1,500/μL. AIDS diagnosis: CD4 <200/μL or opportunistic infection. ART (Anti-Retroviral Therapy) controls but does not cure HIV.
Q15. What is erythroblastosis fetalis (haemolytic disease of the newborn)?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Erythroblastosis fetalis occurs when an Rh− mother carries an Rh+ fetus (inherited from Rh+ father). During first delivery (or miscarriage), fetal Rh+ blood enters maternal circulation → mother produces anti-Rh IgG. In subsequent Rh+ pregnancies, maternal anti-Rh IgG crosses placenta → destroys fetal RBCs → severe anaemia, jaundice, hydrops fetalis. Prevention: Anti-D (RhoGAM) injection to Rh− mother within 72 hours of delivery.
Q16. Which immunity type is provided by maternal antibodies transferred through breast milk?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Maternal antibodies provide natural passive immunity: IgG crosses the placenta (protects fetus/newborn systemically for 3–6 months), and IgA in colostrum (first breast milk) protects the infant's mucosal surfaces (gut, respiratory tract). This is "passive" because the infant receives pre-made antibodies (no memory cells formed) — protection is temporary (weeks to months).
Q17. The polio vaccine used in India's pulse polio programme (oral drops) is which type?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — India's Pulse Polio programme uses OPV (Oral Polio Vaccine, Sabin) — a live attenuated vaccine given as oral drops. OPV is easier to administer (no injection), provides gut immunity (stops person-to-person transmission), and is cheaper. However, OPV carries a tiny risk of VDPV (Vaccine-Derived Polio Virus) — rare reversion to virulence. IPV (Salk, inactivated, injection) is now being introduced alongside OPV for safer transition. India was declared polio-free on March 27, 2014.
Q18. Which vitamin is essential for blood clotting?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Vitamin K is essential for blood clotting. It is required for the synthesis of clotting factors II (prothrombin), VII, IX, and X in the liver (these are "vitamin K-dependent" factors). Vitamin K deficiency → excessive bleeding. Newborns are given vitamin K injection at birth (they lack gut bacteria that produce vitamin K). Vitamin K is fat-soluble; produced by gut bacteria (E. coli); found in green leafy vegetables (spinach, kale).
Q19. The thymus gland, where T-lymphocytes mature, is located in which part of the human body?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The thymus is located in the upper chest (mediastinum), behind the sternum (breastbone), between the lungs, just above the heart. It is where T-lymphocytes (T-cells) mature — "T" stands for Thymus. The thymus is largest and most active during childhood and puberty; it shrinks after puberty (thymic involution) and is mostly replaced by fat in adults. Thymosin hormone promotes T-cell maturation.
Q20. Which of the following is an example of artificial passive immunity?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Anti-rabies immunoglobulin (RIG) after a dog bite is artificial passive immunity: pre-made antibodies are injected directly, providing immediate protection while the body mounts its own active immune response (via the rabies vaccine given simultaneously). Passive immunity is immediate but temporary (no memory cells). Other examples of artificial passive: snake antivenom, tetanus immunoglobulin (TIG), monoclonal antibodies.
Q21. Acute Encephalitis Syndrome (AES), seen in Muzaffarpur (Bihar), has been linked to which factor?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — AES in Muzaffarpur (Bihar) occurs in May–June (litchi harvesting season). Unripe litchi contains hypoglycin A and MCPG (methylenecyclopropylglycine) — toxins that inhibit fatty acid β-oxidation and gluconeogenesis → severe hypoglycaemia in undernourished children (who have low glycogen reserves). Other contributing factors: Japanese Encephalitis (JE) virus and extreme heat. SKMCH Muzaffarpur is the main treatment centre. Government response: nutrition supplementation, JE vaccination, awareness to avoid unripe litchi on empty stomach.
Q22. The clotting of blood involves the conversion of which protein?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Blood clotting (coagulation) involves converting soluble fibrinogen (plasma protein) into insoluble fibrin threads. The cascade: Prothrombin → Thrombin (enzyme) → Fibrinogen → Fibrin (mesh) → traps RBCs and platelets → clot. Platelets initiate the process. Haemophilia is a genetic disorder where clotting factors (VIII or IX) are deficient → uncontrolled bleeding. Vitamin K is needed for factors II, VII, IX, X.
Q23. India was declared polio-free by WHO in which year?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — India was declared polio-free on March 27, 2014 (WHO South-East Asia Region certification). The last wild polio case in India was reported on January 13, 2011 (Howrah, West Bengal). India's Pulse Polio Immunisation Programme (launched 1995) used OPV in mass campaigns. India's success was remarkable given the high population density, poor sanitation, and tropical climate — all risk factors for polio transmission.
Q24. Sickle cell anaemia is caused by a mutation affecting which molecule?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Sickle cell anaemia is caused by a single point mutation in the β-globin gene (chromosome 11): glutamic acid (GAG) → valine (GTG) at position 6. This produces abnormal haemoglobin HbS (instead of normal HbA). Under low O₂, HbS polymerises → RBCs sickle (elongated, rigid) → block capillaries → pain crises, organ damage. Inherited as autosomal recessive (HbSS = disease, HbAS = trait). Sickle cell trait (HbAS) confers resistance to malaria — balanced polymorphism in malaria-endemic regions (including Bihar's tribal populations).
Q25. Which of the following statements about COVID-19 mRNA vaccines is correct?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — mRNA vaccines (Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna) contain messenger RNA that codes for the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein. The mRNA is delivered in lipid nanoparticles → enters host cells → host cells produce the spike protein → immune system recognises it as foreign → produces antibodies and memory cells. The mRNA is degraded after use (does not enter the nucleus or alter DNA). Katalin Karikó and Drew Weissman won the Nobel Prize in Medicine (2023) for the mRNA technology that enabled COVID-19 vaccines. India's Covishield = viral vector (adenovirus), Covaxin = inactivated whole-virion.
