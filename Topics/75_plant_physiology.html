Plant Physiology — Photosynthesis, Respiration, Transport
📂 Biology ⚖️ Weight: 1.0 🎯 Expected: 1–2 Qs 🔶 Bihar: Rice, Makhana, RAU Pusa
📚 Core Content
EXAM TIP Plant Physiology (high-yield Tier A): Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 12H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ + 6H₂O (chloroplast, thylakoid + stroma). Light reaction (thylakoid): photophosphorylation (cyclic & non-cyclic), PS-I (P700, far-red 700 nm) & PS-II (P680, 680 nm), Hill reaction (O₂ evolution), Z-scheme electron flow. Dark reaction (stroma): Calvin cycle (C₃) — RuBisCO fixes CO₂ to RuBP → 3-PGA → G3P → glucose; 3 ATP + 2 NADPH per CO₂. C₄ pathway (Hatch-Slack, Kranz anatomy, maize/sugarcane) — CO₂ concentrator, no photorespiration. CAM (pineapple, cacti) — night CO₂ fixation. Respiration: glycolysis (cytoplasm, 2 ATP), Krebs cycle (mitochondrial matrix, 2 ATP + CO₂), ETS (inner mitochondrial membrane, ~32-34 ATP). Total aerobic: ~36-38 ATP/glucose. Transport: xylem (water+minerals, transpiration pull, cohesion-tension), phloem (translocation, pressure-flow hypothesis, source→sink). Transpiration: stomata, guard cells (K⁺ influx → opening). Guttation (hydathodes, root pressure). Plant hormones: auxin (apical dominance, phototropism), gibberellin (stem elongation, GA₃), cytokinin (cell division), ethylene (fruit ripening, senescence), abscisic acid (stomatal closure, dormancy). Photoperiodism: short-day, long-day, day-neutral.

Photosynthesis overview — Light reactions occur in thylakoid membranes (PS-II → PS-I → ATP synthase, producing ATP + NADPH + O₂). Dark reactions (Calvin cycle) occur in the stroma, using ATP + NADPH to fix CO₂ via RuBisCO into glucose. Chlorophyll-a (absorbs red 660 nm & blue 430 nm) is the primary photosynthetic pigment; chlorophyll-b, carotenoids, xanthophylls are accessory pigments.

C. Dark Reaction — Calvin Cycle, C₃/C₄/CAM, RuBisCO EXAM CORE

PathwayDiscovererFirst ProductAnatomyPhotorespirationExamples
C₃ (Calvin)Melvin Calvin (Nobel 1961; ¹⁴C tracer)3-PGA (3C)Normal (no Kranz)Yes (high — wastes CO₂ & ATP)Rice, wheat, barley, potato, soybean, spinach
C₄ (Hatch-Slack)Hatch & Slack (1966)Oxaloacetate (4C)Kranz anatomy (wreath-like bundle sheath)Minimal (CO₂ concentrator)Maize, sugarcane, sorghum, pearl millet, amaranthus
CAMRanson & ThomasOxaloacetate (4C, night)No Kranz; succulentMinimal (night fixation)Pineapple, cacti, orchids, Agave, Bryophyllum
  • Calvin cycle (C₃) — 3 phases: (1) Carboxylation: RuBisCO fixes CO₂ to 5C RuBP → unstable 6C → 2× 3-PGA. (2) Reduction: 3-PGA → G3P (uses ATP + NADPH). (3) Regeneration: G3P → RuBP (uses ATP). Net: 3 CO₂ → 1 G3P (3C) using 9 ATP + 6 NADPH per G3P; per glucose (2 G3P): 18 ATP + 12 NADPH.
  • RuBisCO (Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate Carboxylase-Oxygenase): most abundant protein on Earth; dual enzyme — carboxylase (photosynthesis) & oxygenase (photorespiration). At high temperature & high O₂/low CO₂, oxygenase activity dominates → photorespiration (wasteful: releases CO₂, no ATP sugar, only in C₃ plants).
  • C₄ mechanism: PEPC (PEP carboxylase) in mesophyll fixes CO₂ to PEP → oxaloacetate (4C) → malate → transported to bundle sheath → decarboxylated → releases CO₂ → Calvin cycle (high CO₂ suppresses photorespiration). PEPC has high affinity for CO₂ and no oxygenase activity.
  • CAM (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism): stomata open at night (minimise transpiration) → CO₂ fixed into malic acid (stored in vacuole). Day: stomata closed, malic acid decarboxylated → CO₂ → Calvin cycle. Adaptation to arid climates.
🧠 Mnemonic — C₃ vs C₄ crops (Bihar-relevant):
C₃ = Cool Climate Crops: Rice, Wheat, Potato, Soybean (temperate, high photorespiration)
C₄ = Hot Hardy Crops: Maize, Sugarcane, Sorghum, Pearl millet (tropical, low photorespiration, higher productivity)
"3 for temperate (RiWhPoSo), 4 for fierce heat (MaSuSoPM)." Makhana (Bihar) is an aquatic C₃ plant.

  • Stomatal regulation: Guard cells open stomata by pumping K⁺ in (followed by water → turgid → stomatal pore opens). Close by pumping K⁺ out (flaccid → pore closes). ABA (abscisic acid) triggers K⁺ efflux → stomatal closure during water stress. Blue light stimulates opening.
  • Factors affecting transpiration: temperature (↑), light (↑), wind (↑), humidity (↓), soil water (↑ supply). Anti-transpirants (e.g., PMA, abscisic acid, coatings) reduce water loss.

H. Photoperiodism PYQ PATTERN

TypeFlowering TriggerExamples
Short-day plants (SDP / long-night)Flower when night length > critical duration (dark period matters)Rice, soybean, tobacco, chrysanthemum, cotton, maize (some), Xanthium

  • Photoperiodism discovered by Garner & Allard (1920). The critical factor is the length of uninterrupted dark period (night), not day length — proved by night-break experiments.
  • Phytochrome: pigment (Pᵣ ↔ P_fᵣ) that perceives light duration; P_fᵣ (far-red absorbing, 730 nm) is active form, accumulates in light; reverts to Pᵣ (red, 660 nm) in dark. Controls flowering, germination, etiolation.
  • Vernalisation: induction of flowering by cold treatment (low temperature) — e.g., winter wheat, biennials (carrot, cabbage). Discovered by Lysenko (USSR). Application: convert winter → spring varieties.

🔶 Bihar Connection
Bihar — Agriculture, Crop Physiology & Research Institutes
  • Rice (paddy) — Bihar's staple: Rice is a C₃ plant and a short-day plant (SDP). Bihar is a major rice-producing state (Kharif, June–October). Varieties: Rajendra Bhagwati, Rajendra Mansuri, CSR-36 (salt-tolerant). Productivity limited by photorespiration (C₃ physiology) — contrast with C₄ maize. South Bihar plateaus use SRI (System of Rice Intensification) to boost yields.
  • Maize — the "white gold" of Bihar: Maize is a C₄ plant (Hatch-Slack, Kranz anatomy) → higher photosynthetic efficiency, no photorespiration. Bihar is among India's top maize producers (Khagaria, Katihar, Purnia, Begusarai). Grown in 3 seasons: Rabi, Kharif, and spring/summer (unique to Bihar). Used for poultry feed, starch, biofuel. C₄ physiology explains higher water-use & nitrogen-use efficiency.
  • Wheat — Rabi crop: Wheat is a C₃ plant and a long-day plant (LDP). Bihar produces ~6 million tonnes annually. Varieties: HD-2967, PBW-343, Rajendra Wheat-4. Grown November–April. Cool-season C₃ crop (lower photorespiration than in heat).
  • Makhana (Euryale ferox): Bihar produces >80% of India's (and world's) makhana. Cultivated in ponds/wetlands of Darbhanga, Madhubani, Purnia, Katihar. Makhana is an aquatic C₃ plant (water lily family, Nymphaeaceae). GI tag sought. Rich in protein, low fat. Popping of seeds is the value-addition. Makhana Research Centre at Darbhanga (under RAU) develops varieties & popping technology.
  • Dr. Rajendra Prasad Central Agricultural University (RPCAU / RAU), Pusa, Samastipur: premier agricultural university of Bihar (originally Imperial Agricultural Research Institute est. 1905; shifted to New Delhi in 1936 becoming IARI). Pusa focuses on crop improvement for Bihar agro-climatic zones: rice, wheat, maize, makhana, pulses, oilseeds. Runs research on drought-tolerant & flood-tolerant rice (Sub1 gene — Swarna-Sub1).
  • ICAR Research Complex for Eastern Region (ICAR-RCER), Patna: established 2001; multi-disciplinary research for eastern India agro-ecosystems. Focus: rainfed agriculture, waterlogging ( Bihar plains), rice-fallow management, climate-resilient agriculture. Houses Rice Research Station & work on submergence-tolerant rice.
  • Mango & Litchi cultivation: Bihar is a leading litchi producer (Muzaffarpur "Shahi" litchi — GI tag). Litchi ripening involves ethylene-driven climacteric rise. Muzaffarpur litchi linked to AES (hypoglycin A, MCPG — see Topic 73). Mango (Bhagalpur, Darbhanga — "Banganapalli", "Langra") is also climacteric — ethylene treatment (Ethrel) used for uniform ripening.
  • Sugarcane (C₄) in Bihar: Gaya, Muzaffarpur, West Champaran have sugar mills. Sugarcane's C₄ physiology → high biomass. Bihar was once India's sugar heartland (pre-1950s shift to Maharashtra/UP).

  • Topic 69 (Cell & Division): Photosynthesis/respiration occur in chloroplasts/mitochondria (semi-autonomous organelles with own DNA, endosymbiotic origin). Cell division (mitosis) in meristems is stimulated by cytokinin & auxin. Plant growth = cell division + elongation (auxin-driven).

  • Topic 70 (Human Body Systems): Respiration in plants (cellular) parallels human respiration (Krebs, ETS in mitochondria). Both use O₂ as final e⁻ acceptor. Gas exchange: plants via stomata/lenticels, humans via lungs/alveoli.

Q1. The overall balanced equation of photosynthesis shows that the oxygen (O₂) released comes from which reactant?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Ruben and Kamen (1941) used the heavy isotope ¹⁸O to label either water or CO₂. When H₂₂¹⁸O was used, the released O₂ was ¹⁸O₂ (heavy); when C¹⁸O₂ was used, the released O₂ was normal. This proved the oxygen released in photosynthesis comes from water (photolysis at PS-II), not from CO₂. CO₂ is fixed into glucose (dark reaction). Earlier, Van Helmont (willow tree experiment) and Ingenhousz had hinted at water's role.
Q2. The "Hill reaction" in photosynthesis refers to which phenomenon?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Robert Hill (1937) showed that isolated chloroplasts, when illuminated in the presence of an artificial electron acceptor (Hill reagent, e.g., ferricyanide), release O₂ — even without CO₂. This "Hill reaction" proved that (1) O₂ evolution is a light reaction independent of CO₂ fixation, and (2) the source of O₂ is water. CO₂ is not required for O₂ release. ATP synthase (option B) is chemiosmosis, not Hill reaction.
Q3. In the Z-scheme (non-cyclic photophosphorylation), the electron flows in which sequence?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — In non-cyclic photophosphorylation (Z-scheme, named for the zig-zag energy diagram), electrons flow unidirectionally: water (split at PS-II/P680) → PS-II → plastoquinone (PQ) → cytochrome b₆f complex → plastocyanin (PC) → PS-I (P700) → ferredoxin (Fd) → NADP⁺ reductase → NADPH. This produces ATP (via proton gradient) + NADPH + O₂. The "Z" shape comes from the energy drops and boosts at the two photosystems. PS-II comes first (P680) because it splits water; PS-I (P700) comes second.
Q4. Which photosystem has a reaction centre absorbing light at the longest wavelength (far-red, 700 nm)?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Photosystem I (PS-I) has the reaction centre P700, whose chlorophyll-a absorbs light at 700 nm (far-red). PS-II has P680 (absorbs at 680 nm, red). The Emerson Enhancement Effect (1957) — that red (660 nm) + far-red (700 nm) light together give more photosynthesis than the sum of each alone — was the key evidence that TWO photosystems exist. Mnemonic: P700 = PS-I, P680 = PS-II.
Q5. Cyclic photophosphorylation differs from non-cyclic photophosphorylation in that it:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Cyclic photophosphorylation involves ONLY PS-I. Electrons from PS-I (excited P700) pass to ferredoxin → cytochrome b₆f → plastocyanin → BACK to P700 (cycle). This pumps protons → ATP synthesis, but produces NO NADPH and NO O₂ (water is not split). It occurs when the cell needs extra ATP (Calvin cycle consumes 3 ATP : 2 NADPH ratio). Non-cyclic (option A trap) produces ATP + NADPH + O₂. Both occur in thylakoid membranes, not stroma (option D wrong).
Q6. RuBisCO, the enzyme that fixes CO₂ in the Calvin cycle, is also known to act as an oxygenase. This dual activity leads to which process?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — RuBisCO (Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase-oxygenase) is the most abundant protein on Earth. It has dual activity: carboxylase (fixes CO₂ → photosynthesis) and oxygenase (binds O₂ → photorespiration). Photorespiration: O₂ binds RuBP → phosphoglycolate → glycolate → in peroxisomes/mitochondria → releases CO₂ (no ATP or sugar made; wastes fixed carbon & ATP). It is significant in C₃ plants (rice, wheat) under high temperature & high O₂/low CO₂. C₄ & CAM plants suppress photorespiration by concentrating CO₂ at RuBisCO.
Q7. The first stable product of CO₂ fixation in the C₄ (Hatch-Slack) pathway is which 4-carbon compound?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — In the C₄ (Hatch-Slack) pathway, the primary CO₂ acceptor is PEP (phosphoenolpyruvate, 3C), and the first stable product is oxaloacetate (4C) — formed by PEP carboxylase (PEPC). Oxaloacetate is converted to malate or aspartate (4C acids), transported to bundle-sheath cells, decarboxylated to release CO₂, which then enters the Calvin cycle (RuBisCO). In C₃, the first product is 3-PGA (3C) — hence the names C₃ and C₄. C₄ plants (maize, sugarcane) have Kranz anatomy and minimal photorespiration.
Q8. Glycolysis, the first stage of respiration, occurs in which part of the cell?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Glycolysis (EMP pathway) occurs in the cytoplasm (cytosol) and does not require oxygen (anaerobic). It splits one glucose (6C) into two pyruvate (3C) molecules, yielding a net 2 ATP (substrate-level phosphorylation) + 2 NADH. It is the common first step of both aerobic and anaerobic respiration. The Krebs cycle occurs in the mitochondrial matrix (option B), and the ETS on the inner mitochondrial membrane (option C). Chloroplast stroma (option D) is the site of the Calvin cycle (photosynthesis), not respiration.
Q9. The Krebs (TCA) cycle takes place in which location, and was elucidated by which scientist?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The Krebs cycle (tricarboxylic acid / citric acid cycle) occurs in the mitochondrial matrix. It was elucidated by Hans Krebs (Nobel Prize in Physiology/Medicine, 1953). Acetyl-CoA (2C, from pyruvate) condenses with oxaloacetate (4C) → citrate (6C); through a series of oxidations, 2 CO₂, 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, and 1 GTP (=ATP) are produced per acetyl-CoA (so doubled per glucose). The cycle is amphibolic (serves both catabolism and anabolism). Glycolysis (option A) is Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas in cytoplasm.
Q10. In the electron transport system (ETS) of aerobic respiration, the final acceptor of electrons is:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — In the ETS (on the inner mitochondrial membrane), electrons from NADH and FADH₂ pass through complexes I→IV. The final electron acceptor is molecular oxygen (O₂) at Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase): O₂ + 4e⁻ + 4H⁺ → 2H₂O. This is why the process is "aerobic." Without O₂, the ETS backs up, the Krebs cycle stops, and only glycolysis continues (with fermentation regenerating NAD⁺). NAD⁺ and FAD (options A, B) are electron CARRIERS, not final acceptors. Cytochrome c (D) is an intermediate carrier.
Q11. How many ATP molecules are approximately produced by the complete aerobic oxidation of one molecule of glucose (classical estimate used in most Indian textbooks)?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The classical textbook value for complete aerobic oxidation of one glucose is 36–38 ATP (depending on shuttle system used to transfer cytoplasmic NADH into mitochondria): Glycolysis = 2 ATP (net) + 2 NADH; Pyruvate oxidation = 2 NADH; Krebs cycle = 2 ATP (GTP) + 6 NADH + 2 FADH₂. Total 10 NADH × 3 = 30 ATP + 2 FADH₂ × 2 = 4 ATP + 4 substrate-level ATP = 38 (or 36 with glycerol-P shuttle). Modern estimates revise this to ~30–32 ATP (NADH≈2.5, FADH₂≈1.5), but BPSC/NCERT accept 36–38. Option A (2) is glycolysis only.
Q12. The upward transport of water and minerals from roots to leaves in tall trees is primarily explained by which mechanism?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The transpiration-pull cohesion-tension theory (Dixon & Joly, 1894) is the accepted mechanism for ascent of sap in tall trees. Water evaporates from mesophyll cell walls into intercellular spaces → exits via stomata → creates a negative pressure (tension) at the top → water is PULLED up the xylem. The cohesion of water molecules (hydrogen bonds) and adhesion to xylem walls maintain an unbroken water column. Root pressure (option A) is a minor positive push, effective only at night and in small plants/herbs, causing guttation. Xylem transport is passive (no ATP, option C wrong).
Q13. Translocation of organic solutes (sucrose) in the phloem is best explained by which hypothesis?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The pressure-flow hypothesis (Münch, 1930) explains phloem translocation. At the SOURCE (photosynthetic leaf), sucrose is actively loaded (uses ATP at companion cells) into sieve tubes → lowers water potential → water enters osmotically from xylem → high turgor pressure. At the SINK (growing roots, fruits, seeds), sucrose is unloaded (used or stored) → water leaves → low turgor. Sap flows from high-pressure source to low-pressure sink. Phloem transport is bidirectional (unlike xylem). Option A is xylem (water), not phloem. Diffusion (B) is too slow; active transport alone (C) doesn't explain bulk flow.
Q14. Stomata open when guard cells:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Stomata open when guard cells actively pump K⁺ ions IN (using ATP-driven proton pumps). K⁺ accumulation lowers water potential → water enters osmotically → guard cells become turgid. The guard cells have thicker inner walls and thinner outer walls, so turgidity causes them to curve outward, opening the pore. Blue light stimulates this K⁺ influx (stomata open in light). Conversely, ABA (abscisic acid, option D) triggers K⁺ EFFLUX → guard cells become flaccid → stomata CLOSE during water stress. Options A and B describe closing, not opening.
Q15. The loss of liquid water (containing dissolved salts) from leaf margins, especially at night/early morning during high humidity, is called:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Guttation is the loss of liquid water (xylem sap, containing dissolved minerals — NOT pure) from leaf tips/margins through specialised pores called hydathodes. It occurs at night or early morning when transpiration is low (high humidity) but roots are actively absorbing water → root pressure builds up → pushes xylem sap out through hydathodes. Transpiration (option B) is loss of water VAPOUR (pure) through stomata, driven by transpiration pull (negative pressure), and occurs mainly during the day. Common guttating plants: grasses, tomato, strawberry, Colocasia.
Q16. Apical dominance (suppression of lateral bud growth by the shoot tip) is caused primarily by which hormone?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Apical dominance is the suppression of lateral (axillary) bud growth by the terminal/apical bud. It is caused by auxin (IAA), produced in the shoot apical meristem and transported downward (polar transport). Auxin inhibits lateral bud growth. When the shoot tip is cut (decapitation/pruning), lateral buds grow out (bushier plant) because auxin supply is removed. Cytokinin (applied externally) COUNTERACTS auxin and promotes lateral bud growth — the two hormones are antagonistic in apical dominance. Gibberellin promotes stem elongation, not apical dominance specifically.
Q17. The hormone gibberellin was discovered in connection with which plant disease?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Gibberellin was discovered through the "Bakanae" (foolish seedling) disease of rice, in which infected seedlings grow abnormally tall and spindly. Japanese scientist Kurosawa (1926) showed that a culture filtrate of the fungus Gibberella fujikuroi (now Fusarium fujikuroi) caused the same elongation in healthy rice — proving a chemical (later named gibberellic acid, GA₃) was responsible. GA₃ is now used commercially to elongate grape stems (Thompson seedless) and induce malting in brewing. Auxin was discovered by F.W. Went (1928) from oat coleoptile tips.
Q18. Which is the ONLY gaseous plant hormone, primarily responsible for fruit ripening and senescence?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Ethylene (C₂H₄) is the only gaseous plant hormone (a simple two-carbon alkene). It promotes fruit ripening (especially in climacteric fruits — banana, mango, tomato, apple, which show a respiratory burst), senescence (ageing) of leaves and flowers, abscission (leaf/fruit drop), and epinasty (downward bending). Commercially, ethylene-releasing compounds like Ethephon/Ethrel are used to ripen fruits artificially. FSSAI limits ethephon use to ≤100 ppm; calcium carbide (carcinogenic) is banned. 1-MCP blocks ethylene action to extend shelf life. Auxin/cytokinin/gibberellin are not gases.
Q19. Abscisic acid (ABA) is known as the "stress hormone" because it:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Abscisic acid (ABA) is called the "stress hormone" because it helps plants cope with water/drought stress. Under water stress, ABA is synthesised in leaves → triggers K⁺ efflux from guard cells → guard cells become flaccid → stomata CLOSE → reduces water loss (transpiration). ABA also induces seed and bud dormancy (prevents germination under unfavourable conditions) and inhibits growth. Despite its name ("abscisic," suggesting abscission), its main role is stomatal regulation and dormancy, not abscission (which is ethylene's role). Options A, B, C are functions of cytokinin, ethylene, and gibberellin respectively.
Q20. The phenomenon by which plants flower in response to the relative length of day and night is called:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Photoperiodism is the response of plants to the relative lengths of light and dark periods (discovered by Garner & Allard, 1920). Plants are classified as: Short-day plants (SDP) — flower when night exceeds a critical length (rice, soybean, chrysanthemum, tobacco); Long-day plants (LDP) — flower when night is shorter than critical length (wheat, barley, spinach, radish); Day-neutral plants (DNP) — flowering unaffected by day length (tomato, cucumber, maize, sunflower). Crucially, the determining factor is the length of the UNINTERRUPTED DARK PERIOD (night), not day length — a flash of light in the middle of the night can prevent flowering in SDP. Vernalisation (A) is flowering induced by cold. Phototropism (B) is growth toward light (auxin).
Q21. Vernalisation refers to:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Vernalisation is the induction of flowering by exposure to prolonged low temperature (cold treatment). Winter varieties of wheat and biennial plants (carrot, cabbage, beetroot) require a period of cold (typically 0–5°C for weeks) to flower. Without vernalisation, they remain vegetative or flower late. Discovered/developed by Trofim Lysenko (USSR) — who applied it to convert winter wheat into spring-sown crops ("jarovisation"). Practical application: cold treatment of seeds/seedlings to induce early flowering, breeding programmes. It is distinct from photoperiodism (B) — vernalisation is about temperature, photoperiodism about day length.
Q22. Makhana (Euryale ferox), a major cash crop of Bihar (Darbhanga, Madhubani, Purnia), belongs to which plant family and physiological category?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Makhana (Euryale ferox) belongs to the family Nymphaeaceae (water lily family). It is an AQUATIC C₃ plant (not C₄ — it lacks Kranz anatomy and the Hatch-Slack pathway). Bihar produces >80% of India's and the world's makhana, cultivated in ponds and wetlands of Darbhanga, Madhubani, Purnia, and Katihar districts. The seeds are popped (roasted + pressed) to produce the edible puffed makhana — rich in protein, low in fat, with nutraceutical value. The Makhana Research Centre at Darbhanga (under RPCAU/RAU Pusa) develops improved varieties and processing technology. It is NOT a grass (Poaceae, option A — that's rice/maize/wheat).
Q23. Which institution, located at Pusa in Samastipur district of Bihar, is the premier agricultural university of the state?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Dr. Rajendra Prasad Central Agricultural University (RPCAU, formerly RAU) is located at Pusa in Samastipur district, Bihar. It is the premier agricultural university of Bihar. Historically, Pusa was the site of the Imperial Agricultural Research Institute (established 1905), which was shifted to New Delhi in 1936 (after the 1934 Bihar earthquake damaged the campus) and became IARI ("Pusa" campus in Delhi). The Pusa, Bihar campus continued as a research station and later became RAU/RPCAU (named after Dr. Rajendra Prasad, India's first President, who was from Bihar). ICAR-RCER (option C) is a separate ICAR research complex in Patna. IARI (B) is now in New Delhi.
Q24. Match the plant hormone with its primary function: 1. Auxin 2. Gibberellin 3. Cytokinin 4. Ethylene 5. Abscisic acid
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — 1. Auxin = cell elongation, phototropism (bending toward light), apical dominance. 2. Gibberellin = stem elongation (bolting), seed germination (breaks dormancy), fruit enlargement. 3. Cytokinin = cell division (cytokinesis), delays senescence, promotes lateral bud growth. 4. Ethylene = fruit ripening, senescence, abscission (only gaseous hormone). 5. Abscisic acid (ABA) = stomatal closure (drought stress), seed/bud dormancy, growth inhibition. Mnemonic: "All Good Crops Eat Apples" — Auxin (apical), Gibberellin (growth), Cytokinin (cell division), Ethylene (end/ripening), ABA (anti-stress). All other options mismatch the hormones with wrong functions.
Q25. CAM (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism) plants, such as pineapple and cacti, are adapted to arid conditions because they:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — CAM (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism) plants adapt to arid/desert conditions by a temporal separation of CO₂ fixation. Stomata open at NIGHT (when temperatures are cooler and humidity higher → less water loss) → CO₂ is fixed by PEP carboxylase into organic acids (malic acid/oxaloacetate), stored in vacuoles. During the DAY, stomata CLOSE (to prevent transpiration) → malic acid is decarboxylated → releases CO₂ internally → Calvin cycle operates using this CO₂ (with light providing ATP/NADPH). This temporal separation (night fixation, day Calvin cycle) minimises water loss. Examples: pineapple, cacti, orchids, Agave, Bryophyllum, Kalanchoe. C₄ plants (option trap) use SPATIAL separation (mesophyll vs bundle sheath). CAM plants have high water-use efficiency but slow growth.
