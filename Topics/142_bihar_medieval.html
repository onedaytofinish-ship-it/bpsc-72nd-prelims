Bihar in Medieval — Sher Shah Suri (Sasaram) & Reforms
📂 Medieval History ⚖️ Weight: 0.7 🎯 Expected: 1–2 Qs 🔶 Bihar: ENTIRE topic is Bihar-centric (Sher Shah from Sasaram)
📚 Core Content
EXAM TIP Sher Shah Suri (1486-1545, Sasaram, Bihar): Afghan ruler who interrupted Mughal rule (1540-1555). Born Farid Khan in Sasaram (Rohtas, Bihar). Defeated Humayun at Chausa (1539, Bihar) and Kannauj (1540). Ruled 5 years (1540-1545) but his reforms shaped India for 400+ years. Key reforms: (1) Zabt land revenue system (→ adopted by Akbar/Todarmal), (2) Rupiah silver coin (= modern ₹), (3) Grand Trunt Road (Sonargaon→Peshawar, 2,500 km, 1,700 sarais), (4) dagh/chihra military reforms, (5) 47 sarkars, (6) impartial justice. Tomb at Sasaram — Indo-Afghan architecture, artificial lake. Died at Kalinjar (1545). Reforms adopted by Akbar — Todarmal was the link.
Tomb of Sher Shah Suri at Sasaram (Rohtas district, Bihar) — 122 ft tall, built between 1545–1551 CE by his son Islam Shah. Architect: Mir Muhammad Aliwal Khan. Set in the middle of an artificial lake — one of the finest examples of Indo-Afghan architecture. A potential UNESCO World Heritage Site. Sher Shah (1486–1545) was born in Sasaram and ruled from Delhi (1540–1545).
Rohtas Fort (Rohtas district, Bihar) — captured by Sher Shah Suri in 1538 CE through a clever ruse (dancing women infiltrated the fort). 4 km circumference, one of the largest forts in India. ASI protected monument. Sher Shah's Bihar power base gave him the military and revenue experience that enabled his defeat of Humayun at Chausa (1539) and Kannauj (1540). The GT Road passed through Bihar (Patna, Aurangabad, Sasaram).

🔶 Bihar Connection
Bihar — Sher Shah's Homeland and Power Base
  • Sasaram (birthplace and tomb): Sher Shah was born at Sasaram (Rohtas district, Bihar) in 1486. His magnificent tomb (1540-1545) is at Sasaram — 122 feet high, set in an artificial lake, built by architect Mir Muhammad Aliwal Khan. It is one of the finest Indo-Afghan architectural works in India and a potential UNESCO World Heritage Site. Sasaram is on the Grand Trunk Road — the road Sher Shah built passes through his own birthplace.
  • Battle of Chausa (1539, Bihar): the battle that began Humayun's downfall was fought at Chausa (near Buxar, in Buxar district of Bihar). Sher Shah used clever tactics — breaching the Ganges embankment to flood Humayun's camp. Humayun barely escaped by floating on a water skin. This battle is a key Bihar connection — one of the most decisive battles in Indian history was fought on Bihar soil.
  • Bihar as Sher Shah's power base: before becoming emperor, Sher Shah served as deputy governor (and later governor) of Bihar. His experience in Bihar — managing land revenue, maintaining an army, governing a province — gave him the skills to later reform the entire empire. Bihar was where he built his career and military strength. All his major reforms were first tested in Bihar before being applied empire-wide.
  • Rohtas Fort (Rohtas district, Bihar): Sher Shah captured Rohtasgarh Fort (in Rohtas district, near Sasaram) from the local Hindu raja (c. 1538) by sending women disguised as dancing girls to open the gates. He strengthened and expanded the fort — one of the largest in India (4 km circumference). It is an ASI-protected monument. Both Sasaram (Sher Shah's tomb) and Rohtas Fort are in the same district — making Rohtas a key Bihar heritage district.
  • Grand Trunk Road through Bihar: the GT Road (Sadak-e-Azam) passes through Bihar — connecting Bengal to Punjab via Patna, Aurangabad, and Sasaram. The modern NH-19 follows this route. Bihar's cities (Patna, Sasaram, Aurangabad) are on one of Asia's oldest and longest major roads — built by a Bihar-born ruler. The sarais along the road provided free food and lodging for travellers — and served as postal stations.
  • Patna under Sher Shah: Sher Shah built a fort at Patna (on the site of the ancient Pataliputra). Patna became an important administrative centre and trading post. The English later established a factory at Patna (1620, Jahangir's reign) — for the saltpetre trade. Patna's strategic location on the Ganges and the GT Road made it a key commercial centre during the Sur and Mughal periods.
  • Todarmal's Bihar connection: Raja Todarmal, who later perfected Akbar's Zabt system, first served under Sher Shah in Bihar. He gained his revenue administration experience in Bihar — and then carried Sher Shah's system to the Mughal imperial level. This makes Bihar the birthplace of the Zabt land revenue system that shaped Indian administration for 400+ years.
🔗 Cross-Topic Connections
  • Topic 140 (Mughal Empire): Sher Shah Suri (Sasaram, Bihar) interrupted Mughal rule (1540-1555). His administrative reforms (Zabt, Rupiah, GT Road, dagh/chihra) were adopted by Akbar. Todarmal, who served under both Sher Shah and Akbar, was the key link. The Sur Dynasty's collapse (1555) allowed Humayun to return from Persia and restore Mughal rule.
  • Topic 138 (Delhi Sultanate): Sher Shah Suri was an Afghan — the Sur tribe had served the Lodi Sultans (the last dynasty of the Delhi Sultanate). The Sur Dynasty (1540-1555) was essentially an Afghan revival between the two phases of Mughal rule. Bihar was an important Lodi/Sur administrative centre.
  • Topic 141 (South & Marathas): After the Sur Dynasty's collapse and the Mughal restoration, Bihar became a Mughal Suba (province). The Maratha Bargi raids (1740s-50s) weakened Bengal-Bihar — leading to British dominance (Plassey 1757, Buxar 1764). Bihar's medieval journey: Sher Shah → Mughal Suba → Maratha raids → British rule.
📰 Current Relevance
  • The Indian Rupee (₹): Sher Shah's Rupiah (silver coin, 178 grains) is the direct ancestor of the modern Indian Rupee. The term 'Rupiah' comes from Sanskrit 'rupya' (wrought silver). Every time an Indian uses a Rupee, they are using a currency standard established by a Bihar-born ruler in the 1540s.
  • Grand Trunk Road (NH-19): parts of Sher Shah's original GT Road are now NH-19 (and other national highways). The road passes through Bihar (Patna, Aurangabad, Sasaram) — connecting the state to the national highway network. It is one of Asia's oldest and longest major roads, still in active use after 480+ years.
  • Sher Shah's tomb (Sasaram, Bihar): a potential UNESCO World Heritage Site. It is one of Bihar's most important historical monuments — attracting tourists, researchers, and architecture enthusiasts. The tomb represents the Indo-Afghan architectural tradition at its finest.
  • Rohtas Fort (Bihar): an ASI-protected monument in Rohtas district. One of the largest forts in India (4 km circumference). It is being developed as a heritage tourism site — showcasing Bihar's medieval military architecture.
  • Administrative legacy: the sarkar-pargana administrative structure introduced by Sher Shah influenced Indian district administration for centuries. The concept of measuring and classifying land for revenue assessment — first implemented in Bihar by Sher Shah — remained the basis of Indian land revenue until independence (1947).
  • Sher Shah Suri in popular memory: Sher Shah is remembered as one of the most just and efficient rulers in Indian history. His story — from the son of an Afghan jagirdar in Sasaram (Bihar) to the emperor of India — is an inspiration. The Indian Army's Sher-i-Kashmir Regiment and various institutions are named after him. Bihar celebrates Sher Shah as a native son.

Q1. Sher Shah Suri, the Afghan ruler who interrupted Mughal rule (1540-1555), was born at which place in Bihar?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Sher Shah Suri (Farid Khan) was born at Sasaram (Rohtas district, Bihar) in 1486 CE. His father was an Afghan jagirdar in Lodi service. His magnificent tomb at Sasaram (1540-1545) is one of the finest Indo-Afghan architectural works — set in an artificial lake, 122 feet high.
Q2. Sher Shah Suri defeated Humayun at which two battles to establish the Sur Dynasty?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Chausa (June 1539, near Buxar in Bihar) and Kannauj/Bilgram (May 1540). At Chausa, Sher Shah flooded Humayun's camp by breaching the Ganges embankment. Humayun fled to Persia. The Sur Dynasty ruled 1540-1555. Chausa is in Bihar — a key Bihar connection.
Q3. Sher Shah Suri's most famous administrative reform, later adopted by Akbar, was:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The Zabt system: land measured in bighas, classified (polaj/parauti/banjar), tax = 1/3 of produce payable in cash. Later perfected by Todarmal under Akbar (1582-89). Sher Shah's Zabt was the foundation of Mughal and later Indian land revenue administration for 400+ years.
Q4. The Grand Trunk Road (GT Road / Sadak-e-Azam) built by Sher Shah Suri ran from where to where?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The GT Road ran from Sonargaon (Bengal) to Peshawar (~2,500 km). Passed through Bihar (Patna, Aurangabad, Sasaram). 1,700 sarais, trees on both sides, postal system with horse couriers. Modern NH-19 follows parts of this route. One of Asia's oldest and longest major roads.
Q5. Sher Shah Suri introduced which currency reform that forms the basis of modern Indian currency?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Sher Shah introduced the Rupiah (silver, ~178 grains/11.5g) and Paisa (copper). 'Rupiah' from Sanskrit 'rupya' (wrought silver). This silver standard was adopted by Mughals and continued through British rule. The modern Indian Rupee (₹) is a direct descendant of Sher Shah's Rupiah.
Q6. Sher Shah Suri's tomb at Sasaram (Bihar) is notable for which architectural feature?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Sher Shah's tomb at Sasaram (1540-1545, architect Mir Muhammad Aliwal Khan) is set on a stone platform in the middle of an artificial lake — accessible by a causeway. 122 feet high, with a large dome and four chhatris. Finest Indo-Afghan architecture. Potential UNESCO site.
Q7. Sher Shah Suri died in 1545 during the siege of which fort?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Sher Shah died in May 1545 at the siege of Kalinjar Fort (Bundelkhand, UP) — killed when a gunpowder explosion set fire to his ammunition store. He was 59. After his death, his son Islam Shah ruled until 1554. The Sur Dynasty collapsed in 1555 — Humayun returned.
Q8. Before becoming emperor, Sher Shah Suri gained administrative experience in which region?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Sher Shah served as deputy governor (vakil) of Bihar under the Mughals. His Bihar experience — managing land revenue, maintaining an army, governing a province — gave him the skills to later reform the entire empire. Bihar was Sher Shah's power base. All his reforms were first tested in Bihar.
Q9. Sher Shah Suri's spy system was known for:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Sher Shah's barids (spies) reported directly to the Sultan on military movements, official corruption, and public opinion. His philosophy: 'It is the duty of a king to know what is happening in his kingdom.' Adopted by Akbar. Ensured official honesty and early rebellion detection.
Q10. Sher Shah Suri's military reform included which key innovation to prevent corruption?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Military reforms: (1) direct payment from state treasury (not through nobles); (2) dagh (horse branding) to prevent substitution; (3) chihra (soldier roll) to prevent ghost soldiers; (4) large standing army (~150,000 cavalry). Adopted by Akbar. These reforms reduced corruption and improved military efficiency.
Q11. The Sur Dynasty (1540-1555) was founded by Sher Shah and lasted for how many years?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The Sur Dynasty lasted 15 years: Sher Shah (1540-1545), Islam Shah (1545-1554), then collapse (1554-1555). In 1555, Humayun returned from Persia and recaptured Delhi. The Sur interregnum was brief but profoundly influential — Sher Shah's reforms were adopted by Akbar.
Q12. Sher Shah Suri's concept of justice was expressed in which famous statement?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — 'Justice is the most excellent of religious acts.' Sher Shah established an efficient judicial system — the Sultan was the highest court of appeal. Qazis in every province. He was known for impartial fairness — regardless of wealth, religion, or status. Stories of his justice are part of Indian folklore.
Q13. Sher Shah Suri built how many sarais (rest houses) along the Grand Trunk Road?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — 1,700 sarais along the GT Road — one every 2 kos (~4 km). Each had rooms, a mosque, a well, and stables. Sarais also served as postal stations (dak chowkis) — horse couriers carried messages. Bengal→Delhi (1,500 km) in just 2-3 days. Free food and lodging for travellers.
Q14. Sher Shah Suri's son Islam Shah Suri (1545-1554) is known for which contribution?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Islam Shah Suri (Salim Shah, 1545-1554) maintained his father's reforms, reorganised the army, built Shergarh (Purana Qila area, Delhi), and attempted to standardise weights. He died young (1554) — the dynasty collapsed within a year. His tomb (Sukha Rauza) is also at Sasaram.
Q15. The Battle of Chausa (1539), where Sher Shah defeated Humayun, took place in which modern Indian state?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Chausa is near Buxar (Buxar district, Bihar). The battle that began Humayun's downfall was fought on Bihar soil. Sher Shah flooded Humayun's camp by breaching the Ganges embankment. Humayun barely escaped on a water skin. Key Bihar connection — don't confuse with the Battle of Buxar (1764).
Q16. Sher Shah Suri's original name was:
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Sher Shah's original name was Farid Khan. He earned the title 'Sher Shah' (Lion King) after killing a tiger single-handedly while serving the Lodi Sultan. The Sultan gave him the title 'Sher Khan' (later 'Sher Shah' when he became emperor). Recorded in Tarikh-i-Sher Shahi by Abbas Khan Sarwani.
Q17. Which Mughal emperor adopted Sher Shah Suri's administrative reforms?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Akbar adopted and perfected Sher Shah's reforms: Zabt (perfected by Todarmal, 1582-89), Rupiah currency, dagh/chihra military system, spy system, and the sarkar-pargana structure. Todarmal, who served under both Sher Shah and Akbar, was the key link — carrying Sher Shah's Bihar-tested systems to the Mughal imperial level.
Q18. Sher Shah Suri's empire was divided into how many administrative units (sarkars)?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — 47 sarkars, each divided into parganas. At sarkar level: shiqdar (police), munsif (revenue/judicial), treasurer. At pargana level: shiqdar, munsif-amil, karkun (accountant), qazi (judge). This hierarchical system (sarkar→pargana→village) was adopted by Akbar as Mughal provincial administration. Bihar was a key sarkar.
Q19. Raja Todarmal, who perfected Akbar's land revenue system, previously served under which ruler?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Todarmal first served under Sher Shah Suri as a revenue official (in Bihar and other provinces). After the Mughal restoration (1555), he joined Akbar's service and used his Sher Shah experience to design the Mughal Zabt system (1582-89). He was the key link between Sher Shah's reforms and Mughal administration — one of Akbar's Navaratna.
Q20. Sher Shah Suri's rule, though brief (5 years, 1540-1545), is considered significant because:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Despite ruling only 5 years, Sher Shah's reforms (Zabt, Rupiah, GT Road, dagh/chihra, spy system, sarkar-pargana, justice) were all adopted by Akbar and formed the Mughal administrative foundation for 300 years. The British also retained many of his systems. Sher Shah proved that effective administration, not military conquest, is the true measure of a ruler's greatness.
Q21. The Rohtas Fort in Bihar (Rohtas district) was built/captured during which period?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Rohtas Fort (Rohtasgarh, Rohtas district, Bihar) was a Hindu hill fort. Sher Shah captured it (c. 1538) by sending women disguised as dancing girls to open the gates. He strengthened and expanded it — 4 km circumference, massive walls. One of India's largest forts, ASI-protected. Near Sasaram — both in Rohtas district.
Q22. Sher Shah Suri's sarais along the GT Road also served which important function?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Sarais served a dual purpose: (1) free food and lodging for travellers; (2) postal stations (dak chowkis) with horse couriers (qasids). Bengal→Delhi (1,500 km) in 2-3 days. One of the most efficient medieval postal systems. Adopted by Akbar and the British.
Q23. After Sher Shah's death (1545) and the collapse of the Sur Dynasty (1555), who returned to recapture Delhi?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — After the Sur Dynasty collapsed (1554-55) due to infighting, Humayun returned from Persia (where he had been in exile since 1540) with a Persian army from Shah Tahmasp. He recaptured Delhi in July 1555 — 15 years after losing it. He died 6 months later (January 1556, fell from library stairs). Akbar (age 13) became emperor.
Q24. The term 'Suri' in Sher Shah Suri refers to:
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — 'Suri' refers to Sher Shah's Afghan tribe — the Suri (or Sur) clan of Pashtuns from the Sulaiman Mountains of Afghanistan. His grandfather migrated from Afghanistan to Bihar during the Lodi period. Sher Shah's rise from a provincial Afghan jagirdar's son in Bihar to emperor of India is one of the most remarkable stories in Indian history.
Q25. Which of the following is the most important legacy of Sher Shah Suri for modern India?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Sher Shah's most enduring legacies: (1) The Indian Rupee (₹) — his Rupiah silver coin is the direct ancestor; (2) The GT Road — parts are now NH-19 through Bihar (Patna, Sasaram); (3) The Zabt land revenue system — shaped Indian administration for 400+ years. Sher Shah, from Sasaram (Bihar), shaped India's administrative and economic systems more than almost any other medieval ruler.
