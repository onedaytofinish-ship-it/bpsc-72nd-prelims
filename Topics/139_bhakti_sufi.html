TOPIC #139 • TIER B
Bhakti & Sufi Movement — Saints, Ideas & Impact
📂 Medieval History ⚖️ Weight: 0.7 🎯 Expected: 1–2 Qs 🔶 Bihar: Sufi saints in Bihar (Maner, Phulwari Sharif)
📚 Core Content
EXAM TIP Bhakti + Sufi (8th-17th century CE): Two parallel devotional movements. Bhakti originated in Tamil Nadu (Alvars=Vishnu, Nayanars=Shiva, 7th-9th c.), spread North. Two streams: Saguna (with form — Krishna/Rama: Mirabai, Tulsidas, Surdas, Chaitanya) and Nirguna (formless — Kabir, Nanak). Key philosophers: Shankara (Advaita), Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita), Madhva (Dvaita), Nimbarka (Dvaitadvaita), Vallabhacharya (Shuddhadvaita). Sufi orders (silsilas): Chishti (Muinuddin Ajmer, Nizamuddin Delhi), Suhrawardi (Bahauddin Zakariya Multan), Qadiri, Naqshbandi. Both movements rejected caste, used vernacular languages, emphasised direct devotion to God.
Nizamuddin Dargah (Delhi) — the shrine of Khwaja Nizamuddin Auliya (1238–1325 CE), one of the greatest Sufi saints of the Chishti order. His disciple Amir Khusrau (1253–1325) is considered the father of Urdu poetry and Hindustani classical music. The Chishti order emphasised love, devotion, and service to humanity — the core of Sufi philosophy that paralleled the Bhakti movement.
Bihar's spiritual heritage — the Sufi tradition in Bihar includes Maner Sharif (dargah of Sharfuddin Yahya Maneri, 13–14th c. CE, Makhdoom-e-Jahan of the Firdausi order), Phulwari Sharif (Patna, Pir Muzaffar Shams Balkhi, 14th c.), and Bihar Sharif (Nalanda, Chishti order). The Bhakti tradition in Bihar includes Vidyapati (Maithil Kokil, Krishna and Shiva padas) and the Mithila school of Vaishnavism.

🔶 Bihar Connection
Bihar — Sufi Saints of Maner & Phulwari Sharif
  • Sharfuddin Yahya Maneri (13th-14th c. CE): the most famous Sufi saint of Bihar. Known as 'Makhdoom-e-Jahan' (Master of the World). He belonged to the Firdausi order (a branch of the Suhrawardi). His khanqah (Sufi hospice) at Maner (near Patna) was a major spiritual centre. He wrote 'Maktubat-i-Sadi' — a collection of 96 letters on Sufi spiritual practices, still studied today.
  • Maner Sharif (Bihar): the dargah (tomb) of Sharfuddin Maneri at Maner (Patna district, Bihar) is one of the most important Sufi pilgrimage sites in eastern India. It attracts pilgrims of all faiths. The Bari Dargah (large tomb) and Chhoti Dargah (small tomb) at Maner are major landmarks.
  • Phulwari Sharif (Bihar): another major Sufi centre near Patna. It has the dargah of Hazrat Pir Muzaffar Shams Balkhi, a 14th-century Sufi saint. Phulwari Sharif was a centre of Islamic learning and Sufism in Bihar during the medieval period.
  • Bihar Sharif (Nalanda): the dargah of Sheikh Sharfuddin Ahmad (a Chishti saint, not to be confused with Maneri) is at Bihar Sharif (Nalanda district). It was an important centre of the Chishti order in Bihar. The annual Urs at Bihar Sharif is a major festival.
  • Sufism in Bihar: Bihar's Sufi tradition was important because Bihar was on the route between Delhi (Sultanate capital) and Bengal. Sufi saints travelling between Delhi and Bengal established khanqahs in Bihar. The Sufi tradition of religious tolerance influenced Bihar's syncretic culture.
  • Bhakti in Bihar: while Bihar is more famous for Sufism, the Bhakti tradition also had a presence — Vidyapati (14th-15th c. CE, Mithila, Bihar) was a great Maithili poet who wrote devotional songs to Krishna and Shiva. He is known as 'Maithil Kokil' (the cuckoo of Mithila). His padas (songs) are still sung in Bihar and Bengal.
🔗 Cross-Topic Connections
  • Topic 138 (Delhi Sultanate): The Sufi movement flourished during the Delhi Sultanate period (1206-1526). Nizamuddin Auliya (Delhi) lived during the reign of Alauddin Khalji and the early Tughlaqs. Sufi saints often critiqued the political establishment — Nizamuddin had tense relations with several Sultans.
  • Topic 140 (Mughal Empire): The Bhakti and Sufi movements reached their peak during the Mughal period. Akbar was influenced by Sufi saints (Salim Chishti of Fatehpur Sikri). Dara Shikoh (Shah Jahan's son) was a follower of the Qadiri Sufi order and translated the Upanishads into Persian. The Bhakti saint Tulsidas wrote the Ramcharitmanas during Akbar's reign (c. 1574).
  • Topic 141 (South & Marathas): The Bhakti movement originated in South India (Alvars and Nayanars, 7th-9th c. CE) before spreading to North India. The southern Bhakti tradition directly influenced the later Vijayanagara Empire's cultural life.
📰 Current Relevance
  • Ajmer Sharif Dargah: the dargah of Khwaja Muinuddin Chishti at Ajmer (Rajasthan) is one of the most visited Sufi pilgrimage sites in the world — attracting millions of pilgrims of all faiths annually. The Urs festival commemorating the saint's death is held every year.
  • Nizamuddin Dargah (Delhi): the dargah of Nizamuddin Auliya in Delhi is a major cultural and spiritual landmark. The qawwali tradition (devotional Sufi music) is performed here regularly. Amir Khusrau's tomb is also at the same complex.
  • Kabir's legacy: Kabir's dohas are quoted across India and are part of the school curriculum. His teachings of religious harmony and rejection of caste remain relevant. The Kabirpanth continues to have followers across India.
  • Guru Granth Sahib: the Sikh scripture includes hymns of Kabir, Namdev, Raidas, and Sheikh Farid — reflecting the synthesis of Bhakti and Sufi traditions. This interfaith scripture is a living legacy of the medieval devotional movements.
  • ISKCON: the International Society for Krishna Consciousness (ISKCON, founded 1966) traces its spiritual lineage to Chaitanya Mahaprabhu's Gaudiya Vaishnavism — a direct continuation of the Bhakti movement.
  • Vidyapati's padas (Bihar): Vidyapati's Maithili devotional songs are still sung in Bihar, Bengal, and Nepal. His influence on Bengali Vaishnava literature (Chaitanya's tradition) was profound. He is a cultural icon of Bihar's Mithila region.

Q1. The Bhakti Movement in India originated in which region during the early medieval period?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The Bhakti Movement originated in Tamil Nadu (7th-9th century CE) with the Alvars (devotees of Vishnu) and Nayanars (devotees of Shiva). They travelled from place to place singing hymns in Tamil, bypassing the Sanskrit priesthood. This southern Bhakti tradition later spread to North India.
Q2. The Alvars were devotees of which deity?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The Alvars ('those immersed in God') were 12 Tamil poet-saints devoted to Vishnu. Their hymns form the Divya Prabandham (Tamil Veda). The most famous Alvar was Andal (the only woman Alvar). The Nayanars were devoted to Shiva.
Q3. The Nayanars were devotees of which deity?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — The Nayanars were 63 Tamil poet-saints devoted to Shiva. Key Nayanars: Appar, Sambandar, Sundarar, and Manikkavasagar. Their hymns form the Tevaram. Like the Alvars, they challenged caste hierarchy and Brahminical orthodoxy.
Q4. Who was the only woman Alvar (and one of the most celebrated Bhakti saints)?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Andal (Goda Devi, 8th c. CE) was the only woman among the 12 Alvars. Daughter of Vishnuchitta (Periyalvar). She wrote Tiruppavai (30 hymns) and Nachiyar Tirumozhi. Considered an incarnation of Goddess Lakshmi. Her Tiruppavai is still recited in Tamil Nadu during Margazhi (December-January).
Q5. Ramanuja (11th-12th century CE), the great Bhakti philosopher, propounded which philosophical school?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Ramanuja (1017-1137 CE) propounded Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-dualism). God (Brahman/Vishnu) is the supreme reality, but the soul (chit) and matter (achit) are real and inseparable attributes of God. He founded the Sri Vaishnava tradition. Key work: Shribhashya.
Q6. Shankaracharya (8th century CE) propounded which philosophical school?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Adi Shankaracharya (788-820 CE) propounded Advaita (non-dualism). Brahman alone is real; the world is maya; the soul (atman) is identical with Brahman. He established four mathas: Sringeri, Dwarka, Puri, and Jyotirmath. He wrote commentaries on the Upanishads, Brahma Sutras, and Gita.
Q7. Shankaracharya established four mathas (monasteries) in four corners of India. Which of the following is NOT one of them?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — The four mathas are: Sringeri (south), Dwarka (west), Puri (east), and Jyotirmath/Joshimath (north). Bodh Gaya is NOT one of them — it is a Buddhist pilgrimage site in Bihar. Each matha was assigned one Veda to preserve.
Q8. Madhvacharya (13th century CE) propounded which philosophical school?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Madhvacharya (1238-1317 CE) propounded Dvaita (dualism). God (Hari/Vishnu) and the individual soul are eternally separate. God is supreme, souls are dependent. Founded the Dvaita Vedanta school, based in Udupi (Karnataka).
Q9. Nimbarka (12th-13th century CE) propounded which philosophical school?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Nimbarka propounded Dvaitadvaita (dualistic non-dualism), also called Bhedabheda. The soul is simultaneously one with and different from God. He was the first to introduce Radha-Krishna worship in the Bhakti tradition.
Q10. Vallabhacharya (15th-16th century CE) propounded which philosophical school?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Vallabhacharya (1479-1531 CE) propounded Shuddhadvaita (pure non-dualism). God (Krishna) is the only reality. He founded Pushti Marg (path of grace) — salvation through God's grace. Worshipped Krishna (Srinathji). Followers called Vallabh Sampradaya.
Q11. Ramananda (14th-15th century CE) was a Bhakti saint of which tradition?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Ramananda (c. 1400 CE) followed Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita but broke away to form his own egalitarian Bhakti tradition in North India (Varanasi). He accepted disciples from all castes — Kabir (weaver), Raidas (leather worker), Sena (barber), Pipa (Rajput). He worshipped Rama and Sita.
Q12. Kabir (15th century CE) was a disciple of which Bhakti saint?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Kabir (1440-1518 CE) was a disciple of Ramananda in Varanasi. Kabir was a Julaha (weaver) by caste. His teachings are in the Bijak (dohas and sakhis). He rejected both Hinduism and Islam's external rituals — emphasised nirguna bhakti (devotion to a formless God).
Q13. Kabir's teachings are compiled in which text?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Kabir's teachings are in the Bijak (three sections: Sakhi, Sabad, Ramaina). His verses also appear in the Adi Granth (Guru Granth Sahib) of the Sikhs. Kabir's dohas are famous for their directness: 'Dhai akshar prem ka padhe so pandit hoy.'
Q14. Kabir's followers are known as:
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Kabir's followers are called Kabirpanthis. They reject caste, idol worship, and ritual — emphasising a formless God (nirguna). The Kabirpanth has both Hindu and Muslim followers. Kabir's grave and samadhi are both in Magahar (Gorakhpur, UP).
Q15. The term 'Nirguna Bhakti' refers to:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Nirguna Bhakti = devotion to a formless (nir-guna, without attributes) God. Kabir, Nanak, and the Sants emphasised this. Saguna Bhakti = devotion to a God with form (sa-guna) — e.g., Krishna, Rama. Mirabai, Tulsidas, Surdas, and Chaitanya were Saguna Bhakti saints.
Q16. Mirabai (16th century CE) was a Bhakti saint devoted to which deity?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Mirabai (c. 1498-1546 CE) was a Rajput princess from Marwar (Merta) who devoted herself to Krishna (Giridhar Gopal). She married the crown prince of Mewar (Chittorgarh) but rejected married life. Her bhajans are famous. One of the most beloved Saguna Bhakti saints.
Q17. Tulsidas (16th century CE) wrote which famous text?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Tulsidas (1532-1623 CE) wrote the Ramcharitmanas (c. 1574) — the Hindi (Awadhi) retelling of the Ramayana. This made the Ramayana accessible to common people. He was a Saguna Bhakti saint devoted to Rama. The Ramcharitmanas is a cornerstone of Hindi literature.
Q18. Surdas (16th century CE) was a Bhakti saint devoted to which aspect of Krishna?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Surdas (c. 1478-1581 CE) was a blind poet who wrote the Sur Sagar (Ocean of Melody) — thousands of poems about the childhood and youthful leelas of Krishna. He was a disciple of Vallabhacharya. His poems describe Krishna's childhood with Yashoda and his love for the gopis.
Q19. Chaitanya Mahaprabhu (16th century CE) was a Bhakti saint from which region?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Chaitanya Mahaprabhu (1486-1534 CE) was from Nadia (Bengal). He founded Gaudiya Vaishnavism — devotion to Krishna and Radha through sankirtan (group singing and dancing). ISKCON (Hare Krishna movement) traces its lineage to Chaitanya.
Q20. The Sufi movement in India was primarily associated with which religion?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Sufism is the mystical tradition within Islam. Sufis seek direct experience of God through love, devotion, and self-discipline. In India, Sufis spread Islam through peaceful means — unlike military conquests. They emphasised love, tolerance, and the unity of all beings.
Q21. The Chishti Sufi order (silsila) in India was founded by which saint?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Khwaja Muinuddin Chishti (1141-1236 CE) founded the Chishti order in India, settling in Ajmer. His dargah (Ajmer Sharif) is one of the most important Sufi pilgrimage sites. Other famous Chishti saints: Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki, Fariduddin Ganj-i-Shakar, and Nizamuddin Auliya.
Q22. Nizamuddin Auliya (13th-14th century CE) was a famous Sufi saint of which order, based in which city?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Nizamuddin Auliya (1238-1325 CE) was the most famous Chishti Sufi saint based in Delhi. Known as 'Mahbub-e-Ilahi' (Beloved of God). His disciple Amir Khusrau was the father of Hindustani music and Hindi-Urdu literature. His dargah in Delhi is a major pilgrimage site.
Q23. The Suhrawardi Sufi order in India was founded by which saint?
✅ Correct Answer: (D) — Bahauddin Zakariya (1182-1262 CE) founded the Suhrawardi order in India, based in Multan. Unlike the Chishtis (who emphasised poverty), the Suhrawardis accepted government service and wealth. Other orders: Qadiri (Abdul Qadir Jilani), Naqshbandi, Firdausi.
Q24. Which Sufi saint's dargah is located at Ajmer (Rajasthan) and is one of the most visited pilgrimage sites in India?
✅ Correct Answer: (C) — Khwaja Muinuddin Chishti's dargah at Ajmer (Ajmer Sharif) is one of the most important Sufi pilgrimage sites in India. He is called 'Gharib Nawaz' (Benefactor of the Poor). Akbar walked on foot from Agra to Ajmer (1568) to offer gratitude after the birth of Salim (Jahangir).
Q25. The Bhakti and Sufi movements shared which common feature?
✅ Correct Answer: (B) — Both movements emphasised: (1) direct personal devotion to God, bypassing priests; (2) rejection of caste hierarchy; (3) use of vernacular languages (Hindi, Tamil, Punjabi); (4) promotion of social equality and harmony. They laid the foundation for a syncretic Indo-Islamic culture and influenced Sikhism.
