Morley-Minto Reforms (1909) — Deep Dive

  • Background: the Swadeshi Movement (1905) and the Muslim League (1906) created pressure for constitutional reform. The British wanted to conciliate the Muslims.
  • Key innovation: separate electorates for Muslims — only Muslims could vote for Muslim candidates in Muslim constituencies. This was the beginning of the "divide and rule" strategy.
  • Council expansion: Central Legislative Council (60 → 69 members) and Provincial Legislative Councils (non-official majority in provinces).
  • Franchise: very limited (about 1% of the population — based on property, education, and tax qualifications).
  • Indian reaction: Muslims (Muslim League) satisfied; INC disappointed (wanted more — the Morley-Minto Reforms were too limited). Gokhale called them "a thoroughly disappointing measure."
  • Significance: introduced communal representation — a major factor in the partition of India (1947). The system of separate electorates was extended and became entrenched.
🏛️ Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms (1919) — Deep Dive

Background

  • Montagu Declaration (August 20, 1917): Edwin Montagu (SoS) promised "gradual development of self-governing institutions" in India.
  • Montagu-Chelmsford Report (1918): drafted by Montagu and Chelmsford after visiting India — the basis for the GoI Act 1919.
  • WWI context: India's contribution to WWI (1.5 million soldiers, £500 million) created pressure for self-government.

Key Features

FeatureDetails
Diarchy in provincesReserved subjects (Governor's council): police, justice, land revenue, irrigation. Transferred subjects (Indian ministers): education, health, agriculture, local government, cooperatives.
Bicameral legislatureCouncil of State (Upper House, 60 members, 5-year term) + Legislative Assembly (Lower House, 145 members, 3-year term).
Direct electionsFirst time — with limited franchise (about 10% of the population).
Separate electoratesExtended to Sikhs, Christians, Anglo-Indians, and Europeans (in addition to Muslims from 1909).
Budget separationProvincial budgets separated from the central budget for the first time.
Public Service CommissionEstablished 1926 (for recruitment to civil services).

Assessment

  • Positives: introduced direct elections; Indian ministers got experience in governance (transferred subjects); bicameralism at the centre.
  • Negatives: diarchy was cumbersome and unworkable (friction between Governor's council and Indian ministers); franchise was extremely limited (~10%); the Rowlatt Act (1919) (detention without trial) undermined the reforms; the centre retained full control (no responsible government at the centre).
  • Indian reaction: disappointed — led to the Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-22). The INC demanded a review (which became the Simon Commission, 1927).
⚖️ Government of India Act 1935 — Deep Dive

Background

  • Simon Commission (1927-30) → Round Table Conferences (1930-32) → GoI Act 1935. The Act was the longest British parliamentary Act (475 sections).
  • It was based on the Simon Commission report, the Round Table Conferences, and the White Paper (1933).

Key Features

FeatureDetails
Provincial autonomyDiarchy abolished in provinces; elected ministries responsible to provincial legislatures. 11 provinces got self-government. Governors had special powers (emergency, minorities, princely interests).
Diarchy at centreReserved subjects (Viceroy): defence, foreign affairs, tribal areas. Transferred subjects (ministers): other subjects. NEVER implemented — All-India Federation never came into being.
All-India FederationBritish provinces + princely states. NEVER implemented — princes refused to join (needed a minimum number to accede). Only the provincial part came into force (1937).
Three listsFederal List (centre: defence, foreign affairs, currency). Provincial List (provinces: police, education, health). Concurrent List (both: criminal law, marriage, forests — federal prevails). Adopted by Indian Constitution (1950).
Federal CourtEstablished 1937 (Delhi). Jurisdiction over constitutional disputes. Predecessor of Supreme Court (1950).
RBIEstablished April 1, 1935 (under RBI Act 1934). Controlled currency, credit, banking. Nationalised 1949.
Franchise~10% of the population (property + education qualifications). NOT universal adult franchise.
Communal representationSeparate electorates for Muslims, Sikhs, Christians, Anglo-Indians, Europeans, and Depressed Classes (reserved seats after Poona Pact, 1932).

Assessment

  • Nehru: "a new charter of slavery" — limited provincial autonomy but British control at the centre.
  • Gandhi: the Act was a compromise that fell far short of Purna Swaraj.
  • Practical impact: the 1937 provincial elections were held under this Act — the INC won in 7 of 11 provinces. This gave the INC its first experience of governance.
  • Constitutional legacy: the Act was the constitutional framework for India until 1950. Many features (three lists, Federal Court → Supreme Court, RBI, bicameralism) were adopted by the Indian Constitution.
🔶 Bihar Connection — Deep Dive
1937 Elections in Bihar:
  • The 1937 provincial elections were held under the Government of India Act 1935. Bihar was one of the 11 provinces that held elections.
  • The INC won in Bihar — the Congress emerged as the single largest party.
  • Sri Krishna Singh (also known as "Bihar Kesari") became the first Premier (later Chief Minister) of Bihar (1937-1939). He was a key Bihar Congress leader who had participated in the Quit India Movement (1942).
  • Anugrah Narayan Sinha became the Deputy Premier (later Deputy Chief Minister). He was the key finance and development minister.
  • The Congress ministry implemented reforms in education, land revenue, and local government. However, the ministry was short-lived — it resigned in October 1939 (WWII declared without consulting Indian leaders).
  • Return to power (1946): after elections in 1946, Sri Krishna Singh again became Premier — and continued as Chief Minister after independence (1947-1961). He became the longest-serving CM of Bihar.
Significance for Bihar:
  • The 1937 elections were Bihar's first experience of elected self-government. The Congress's victory demonstrated popular support for the nationalist movement.
  • The short-lived Congress ministry (1937-39) gave Bihar's leaders experience in governance — which was crucial for the post-independence period.
