Topic 72 — Diseases and Pathogens: Deep Dive

Communicable vs non-communicable, pathogens, vectors, vaccines, Bihar-specific diseases

1. Disease Classification — Complete Framework

Diseases are broadly classified into two categories: communicable (infectious) and non-communicable (chronic). Understanding the difference is fundamental to public health.

Communicable (Infectious) Diseases

  • Definition: diseases caused by infectious agents (pathogens) that can be transmitted from one person to another, or from animals/insects to humans.
  • Pathogen types: bacteria, viruses, protozoa, fungi, helminths (parasitic worms), prions.
  • Transmission modes: airborne (droplets), waterborne, foodborne, vector-borne, direct contact, bloodborne, sexual, mother-to-child.
  • Examples: TB, malaria, cholera, dengue, COVID-19, influenza, polio, hepatitis, HIV/AIDS, leprosy, plague.
  • Prevention: vaccination, hygiene, sanitation, vector control, safe sex, safe blood transfusion.
  • WHO concern: outbreaks, epidemics, pandemics — can spread rapidly across populations.

Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)

  • Definition: diseases not caused by infectious agents — result from genetic, lifestyle, environmental, or aging factors. Cannot be transmitted between people.
  • Categories: (1) Cardiovascular (heart disease, stroke), (2) Cancer, (3) Chronic respiratory (COPD, asthma), (4) Diabetes, (5) Mental health, (6) Autoimmune, (7) Genetic/inherited.
  • Risk factors: tobacco use, alcohol, unhealthy diet, physical inactivity, air pollution, genetics, aging.
  • Examples: diabetes, hypertension, coronary heart disease, cancer, COPD, Alzheimer's, rheumatoid arthritis, sickle cell anaemia, haemophilia.
  • WHO concern: NCDs cause ~71% of all global deaths (41 million annually). Rising burden in developing countries due to urbanisation and lifestyle changes.
India's dual disease burden: India faces both communicable diseases (TB, malaria, dengue, kala-azar) AND rising non-communicable diseases (diabetes, heart disease, cancer). This "dual burden" is a major public health challenge. Bihar exemplifies this: infectious diseases (kala-azar, AES, TB, leprosy) coexist with rising NCDs (diabetes, hypertension).

3. India's Immunization Programme (UIP)

The Universal Immunization Programme (UIP) is one of the world's largest public health programmes. It covers ~2.7 crore (27 million) pregnant women and children annually.

Key milestones: India declared polio-free (WHO, 25 March 2014). Last polio case: 13 January 2011 (Howrah, West Bengal). Smallpox eradicated globally (WHO, 8 May 1980). India eliminated maternal and neonatal tetanus (2015). Mission Indradhanush (launched 2014) — aims to achieve 90% full immunization coverage.

4. Bihar-Specific Diseases — Complete Analysis

Kala-azar (Visceral Leishmaniasis)
  • Cause: Leishmania donovani (protozoan parasite)
  • Vector: Sandfly Phlebotomus argentipes (breeds in cracks in mud houses, cattle sheds)
  • Bihar share: ~70% of India's kala-azar cases
  • Endemic districts: Muzaffarpur, Vaishali, East Champaran, Sitamarhi, Saharsa, Purnia, Araria
  • Symptoms: Persistent fever, spleen/liver enlargement (splenomegaly/hepatomegaly), darkened skin (kala-azar = "black disease"), weight loss, anaemia
  • Diagnosis: rk-39 rapid diagnostic test (RDT), bone marrow aspiration (definitive)
  • Treatment: Miltefosine (oral), Liposomal Amphotericin B (AmBisome, IV), Paromomycin (IM)
  • Research: Rajendra Memorial Research Institute (RMRI), Patna — dedicated kala-azar research institute under ICMR
  • Elimination target: India aims <1 case per 10,000 population. Close to achievement (2024-2025)
  • Programme: Kala-azar Elimination Programme — indoor residual spraying (IRS with DDT/synthetic pyrethroids), active case detection, free treatment, bed nets (LLINs)
Acute Encephalitis Syndrome (AES) — Muzaffarpur
  • What: recurrent outbreaks of brain inflammation in children, primarily in Muzaffarpur district
  • Season: May-June (litchi harvest season)
  • Affected: mainly undernourished children (aged 2-10) from rural areas
  • Cause linked to: litchi fruit toxins (hypoglycin A and methylenecyclopropylglycine/MCPG) combined with malnutrition and heat. Toxins cause hypoglycaemia (dangerously low blood sugar) → brain inflammation
  • 2019 outbreak: >150 child deaths in Muzaffarpur
  • Treatment: SKMCH (Sri Krishna Medical College, Muzaffarpur) — primary treatment centre. Rapid glucose administration
  • Prevention: (1) ensure children eat evening meal, (2) limit early morning litchi consumption on empty stomach, (3) improve nutrition, (4) awareness campaigns during litchi season
  • Research: NCDC (National Centre for Disease Control), AIIMS Patna, ICMR studies
Leprosy (Hansen's Disease) in Bihar
  • Cause: Mycobacterium leprae (acid-fast bacillus, related to TB bacterium)
  • Affects: skin, peripheral nerves, upper respiratory tract → numbness, deformity, disability
  • Transmission: prolonged close contact via respiratory droplets (not highly contagious)
  • Treatment: Multi-Drug Therapy (MDT): rifampicin + dapsone + clofazimine. Fully curable if detected early
  • India status: achieved elimination (<1 per 10,000) in 2005, but still has highest global case load (~60% of world cases)
  • Bihar: one of India's high-burden leprosy states (along with UP, Maharashtra, Odisha, Chhattisgarh). Several districts above elimination threshold
  • Programme: NLEP (National Leprosy Eradication Programme) — active case detection drives (LCDC - Leprosy Case Detection Campaign), ASHA-based surveillance, MDT provision
Other Bihar-Relevant Diseases
  • Tuberculosis: Bihar is a high-TB-burden state. NTEP (National TB Elimination Programme) — target eliminate by 2025. Nikshay Poshan Yojana (₹500/month nutritional support). Challenges: undernutrition, delayed diagnosis, MDR-TB
  • Lymphatic Filariasis: endemic in Bihar (Wuchereria bancrofti, Culex mosquito). MDA (Mass Drug Administration) with DEC + albendazole. Elimination target: 2027
  • Japanese Encephalitis: affects children in northern Bihar districts (bordering UP). Culex mosquito, pigs as amplifying hosts. JE vaccine in UIP in endemic districts
  • Malaria: Bihar has moderate malaria burden, especially in tribal and forested areas. P. falciparum and P. vivax both present. NVBDCP interventions
  • Cholera: outbreaks during floods (monsoon season). Contaminated water. ORS treatment
  • Sickle cell anaemia: genetic blood disorder prevalent in tribal populations of Bihar. Non-communicable but significant health burden
