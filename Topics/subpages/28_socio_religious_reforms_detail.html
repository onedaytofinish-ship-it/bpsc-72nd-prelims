📖 Socio-Religious Reform Movements — Master Detail

Reformer-by-reformer analysis, reform legislations timeline, regional streams (Bengal / Maharashtra / Punjab / South), Bihar connections, 7 exam predictions

🌿 Two Streams of Reform — Deep Dive

1. Reformist / "Within Tradition" Stream

  • Approach: reinterpret existing scriptures using rationalism; accept useful Western ideas (education, science); oppose superstition and social evils; stay rooted in own faith.
  • Reformers: Raja Ram Mohan Roy (Brahmo Samaj), Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar, M.G. Ranade (Prarthana Samaj), Sir Syed Ahmad Khan (Aligarh Movement).
  • Goal: make Hinduism/Islam compatible with modernity — preserve the essence, discard the ossified customs (sati, caste rigidity, child marriage, polygamy, idolatry in some cases).

2. Revivalist / "Back to Roots" Stream

  • Approach: return to the "pure" original scriptures (Vedas, Quran, Gita); reject later "accretions" (Puranas, commentaries, medieval customs); assertive cultural pride; sometimes anti-Western.
  • Reformers: Swami Dayananda Saraswati (Arya Samaj — "Back to the Vedas"), Swami Vivekananda (Ramakrishna Mission — universal Vedanta), Theosophists (Blavatsky, Olcott, Annie Besant).
  • Goal: recover India's ancient spiritual glory; assert Hinduism/Islam as a world religion (Vivekananda at Chicago 1893); counter Western/Christian missionary influence.
🧠 Mnemonic — Reformist vs Revivalist:
Reformist (R-R-V-S-R): Ram Mohan, Ranade, Vidyasagar, Sir Syed — "Reform from within".
Revivalist (D-V-T): Dayananda, Vivekananda, Theosophists — "Back to roots".
"Reformists reinterpret; Revivalists return."
🏛️ Regional Streams — The Geography of Reform

Bengal Renaissance (Calcutta hub)

  • Hub: Calcutta (then British India's capital till 1911). Fort William College, Hindu College (1817, founded by David Hare and Radhakanta Deb), Sanskrit College (1824, founded by Ram Mohan Roy's circle).
  • Reformers: Ram Mohan Roy → Brahmo Samaj (1828); Debendranath Tagore → Tattwabodhini Sabha (1839); Keshab Chandra Sen → Brahmo Samaj of India (1866 split); Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar; Bankim Chandra Chatterjee (literary).
  • Key reforms: Sati abolition (1829), Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act (1856), women's education (John Elliot Drinkwater Bethune's school 1849, with Vidyasagar's support).

Maharashtra (Bombay/Poona hub)

  • Hub: Bombay and Poona. Elphinstone College (1856), Deccan Education Society (1884).
  • Reformers: Jyotirao Phule → Satyashodhak Samaj (1873); M.G. Ranade and R.G. Bhandarkar → Prarthana Samaj (founded 1867 by Atmaram Pandurang); G.K. Gokhale → Servants of India Society (1905); B.R. Ambedkar (later, from 1920s).
  • Focus: anti-caste (Phule, Savitribai), widow remarriage, women's education (Pandita Ramabai's Sharada Sadan, 1882).

Punjab & North India

  • Arya Samaj (1875, Dayananda): Lahore became a major Arya Samaj centre; founded DAV College (Lahore, 1886) and Dayanand Anglo-Vedic schools across North India.
  • Singh Sabha Movement (Sikhs, 1873): Amritsar (1873) and Lahore (1879) Singh Sabhas; Khalsa College Amritsar (1892); later SGPC (1925).
  • Ahmedia Movement (1881): Mirza Ghulam Ahmad (Qadian, Punjab) — modernist Islamic revival.

South India

  • Theosophical Society (Adyar, Madras — 1886): Blavatsky and Olcott; Annie Besant (joined 1889) founded Central Hindu College (Banaras, 1898) → became BHU (1916).
  • Kerala: Sri Narayana Guru (SNDP Yogam 1903); Ayyankali (Sadhu Jana Paripalana Sangam 1907, for Pulaya community); Chattampi Swamikal.
  • Tamil Nadu: Vaikunda Swamigal (Ayyavazhi, 19th c.); later Iyothee Thass (Buddhist revival among Dalits, 1890s); Justice Party (1916) — non-Brahmin movement.

Islamic Reform Movements

  • Aligarh Movement (Sir Syed, post-1857): modern Western education; loyalist to Crown; MAO College 1875 → AMU 1920. Tahzib-ul-Akhlaq journal (1870).
  • Deoband Movement (1866): Maulana Qasim Nanautvi and Rashid Ahmad Gangohi; traditional Islamic learning; anti-Western; Darul Uloom Deoband seminary. The Deoband-Aligarh dichotomy: traditional vs modern.
  • Nadwatul Ulema (1894, Lucknow): Maulana Shibli Nomani, Maulana Hali; bridge between Deoband (traditional) and Aligarh (modern).
  • Ahmadiyya (1881, Qadian): Mirza Ghulam Ahmad; considered heretical by mainstream Islam.

🔶 Bihar Connection — Deep Dive
1. Swami Sahajanand Saraswati (Bihar's own reformer):
  • A sanyasi-reformer; founded the Bihar Provincial Kisan Sabha (1929) and led the All India Kisan Sabha (1936).
  • Worked for Bihar's peasants (against zamindari exploitation in the Permanent Settlement zones of Bihar — Tirhut, Champaran, Saran, Muzaffarpur).
  • The 71st BPSC PYQ (p32) directly matched Sahajanand Saraswati with "Individual Satyagraha in Bihar 1940" (option c-3 in the matching).
  • His most famous work: Kisan Kya Sandesh (the peasant's message).
2. Champaran Satyagraha (1917) — Gandhi's social reform in Bihar:
  • Gandhi's first civil disobedience in India. The Champaran indigo farmers (the 'tinkathia' system — 3/20 of land forced to grow indigo for the British planters) were being exploited.
  • Gandhi set up schools and sanitation centres in Champaran villages — making this a social reform movement, not just a political protest.
  • He was assisted by Raj Kumar Shukla (the Champaran farmer who invited Gandhi), Braj Kishore Prasad, and Anugrah Narayan Sinha — all Bihar reformers.
3. Education Reform in Bihar (1854-1917):
  • Wood's Despatch (1854) recommended universities. The 1857 universities (Calcutta, Bombay, Madras) did NOT include Patna — Bihar was treated as part of Calcutta University's jurisdiction.
  • Patna University was founded only in 1917, when Bihar was separated from Bengal Presidency (1912).
  • Bihar's modern educational infrastructure lagged behind Bengal's reform milieu — a consequence of colonial neglect.
4. Bihar Bengal Partition (1905) — communal/religious angle:
  • Lord Curzon partitioned Bengal in 1905 — Eastern Bengal (with Dhaka as capital) included Bihar and Orissa within the broader Bengal Presidency jurisdiction.
  • Bihar was indirectly affected — the partition triggered Swadeshi and reform currents in Bihar's intelligentsia.
  • The partition was annulled in 1911; in 1912 Bihar and Orissa were separated from Bengal as a separate province (capital Patna).
5. Arya Samaj in Bihar:
  • The Arya Samaj (Dayananda, 1875) established institutions in Bihar — there are Arya Samaj mandirs in Patna, Muzaffarpur, Bhagalpur.
  • The DAV (Dayanand Anglo-Vedic) school network reached Bihar — DAV schools exist in Patna, Bhagalpur, Muzaffarpur.
6. Sati in Bihar:
  • Sati was practised in parts of Bihar (zamindari families, certain Rajput clans) until the 1829 abolition.
  • The Patna and Gaya regions had documented cases (though fewer than Bengal).
  • The 1829 Regulation XVII applied to all of British India including Bihar.
