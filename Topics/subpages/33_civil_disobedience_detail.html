🧂 Civil Disobedience — Dandi March, Round Table & GoI Act 1935: Master Detail

Complete analysis of Simon Commission, Nehru Report, Dandi Salt March, three Round Table Conferences, Gandhi-Irwin Pact, Poona Pact, GoI Act 1935, Bihar connections, 7 exam predictions

📖 The Build-Up (1927-1929)

Simon Commission (1927)

  • Appointed: 1927, to review the GoI Act 1919 and recommend reforms. Arrived in India February 3, 1928.
  • Composition: 7 British members, NO Indian member — led by Sir John Simon.
  • Boycott: all Indian parties (INC, Muslim League, Hindu Mahasabha) boycotted it. Slogan: "Simon Go Back."
  • Lala Lajpat Rai's death: lathi-charged during a protest in Lahore (October 1928); died November 17, 1928. His words: "Every blow aimed at me is a nail in the coffin of British rule."
  • Report: submitted 1930; led to the Round Table Conferences.

Nehru Report (1928)

  • Background: the INC (at its Madras session, December 1927) decided to draft an Indian constitution in response to the Simon Commission.
  • Committee: headed by Motilal Nehru; included Jawaharlal Nehru, Subhash Chandra Bose, and others.
  • Recommendations: dominion status, fundamental rights, unified electorate (no separate electorates), 19 fundamental rights, a federal structure.
  • Rejection: the Muslim League rejected it (Jinnah's 14 Points, 1929, were a counter-demand for separate electorates).
  • Significance: the first Indian attempt to draft a constitution; influenced the future Constitution of India (1950).

Lahore Session (December 1929) — Purna Swaraj

  • President: Jawaharlal Nehru.
  • Resolution: Purna Swaraj (complete independence) — a shift from dominion status.
  • Independence Day: January 26, 1930 declared and celebrated across India.
  • Significance: January 26 was later chosen as India's Republic Day (1950) in honour of the 1930 Purna Swaraj declaration.
🧂 The Dandi Salt March & Civil Disobedience (1930-1934)

The Dandi Salt March (March 12 – April 6, 1930)

  • Route: Sabarmati Ashram (Ahmedabad) → Dandi (Gujarat coast), 240 miles (384 km).
  • Duration: 24 days (March 12 – April 6, 1930).
  • Followers: 78 started from Sabarmati Ashram; the march grew as villagers joined along the route.
  • At Dandi (April 6, 1930): Gandhi picked up salt from the seashore, breaking the British salt law. This symbolic act launched the Civil Disobedience Movement.
  • Why salt? the salt tax was a universal grievance — every Indian used salt, and the British monopoly on salt was deeply resented. The salt satyagraha was a brilliant strategic choice by Gandhi — it was a simple, universal issue that mobilised masses across class, religion, and region.

At the Second, Gandhi was the sole INC representative — his demand for a unified India was opposed by the Muslim League (separate electorates), Ambedkar (Depressed Classes), and the princes (autonomy). The conference was inconclusive; Gandhi returned disappointed.
⚖️ The Government of India Act 1935

Key Features

  • Longest British Act: 475 sections (the longest Act of the British Parliament until then).
  • Provincial autonomy: diarchy abolished in provinces; elected ministries responsible to provincial legislatures. The 11 provinces got self-government (within limits).
  • Diarchy at the centre: reserved subjects (defence, foreign affairs, tribal areas) under the Viceroy; transferred subjects under elected ministers. NEVER implemented (the All-India Federation never came into being because the princes refused to join).
  • All-India Federation: British provinces + princely states. NEVER implemented (princes refused).
  • Reserve Bank of India: established 1935 (RBI Act 1934). Controlled currency and credit.
  • Federal Court: established 1937, at Delhi. Jurisdiction over constitutional disputes. Predecessor of the Supreme Court of India (1950).
  • Franchise: limited to about 10% of the population (property and education qualifications). NOT universal adult franchise.
  • Communal representation: separate electorates for Muslims, Sikhs, Christians, Anglo-Indians, and the Depressed Classes (after the Poona Pact, reserved seats).

Assessment

  • Nehru's critique: "A new charter of slavery" — limited provincial autonomy but British control at the centre.
  • Gandhi's view: the Act was a compromise that fell far short of Purna Swaraj.
  • Practical impact: the 1937 provincial elections were held under this Act — the INC won in 7 of 11 provinces. This gave the INC its first experience of governance.
  • Constitutional legacy: the Act was the constitutional framework for India until 1950. Many features (federal structure, RBI, Federal Court) were adopted by the Indian Constitution.
🔶 Bihar Connection — Deep Dive
1. Salt Satyagraha at Patna:
  • During the Civil Disobedience Movement (1930), salt law was violated on the banks of the Ganga at Patna. Bihar's salt satyagraha was part of the nationwide salt satyagraha movement that followed Gandhi's Dandi March.
  • Bihar's salt satyagraha was led by the Bihar Congress — including Rajendra Prasad and Anugrah Narayan Sinha.
2. No-Tax Campaigns in Bihar:
  • Bihar's villages, particularly in the Tirhut and Champaran regions, had no-tax campaigns during the Civil Disobedience Movement. Peasants refused to pay land revenue.
  • The Bihar Congress organised these campaigns — linking the national movement to local peasant grievances.
3. Tribal Satyagrahas in Chhotanagpur:
  • The tribal regions of Chhotanagpur (now Jharkhand, then part of Bihar) had satyagrahas against forest laws and land revenue. The Santhal and Munda tribal communities participated.
4. Rajendra Prasad's Role:
  • Dr. Rajendra Prasad was the key Bihar Congress leader during the Civil Disobedience Movement. He was arrested and imprisoned. After independence, he became the first President of India (1950-1962).
5. Anugrah Narayan Sinha:
  • A.N. Sinha was another key Bihar leader. He was imprisoned during the movement. After independence, he became Bihar's first Deputy Chief Minister.
6. Bihar's Limited Impact (compared to Gujarat/Maharashtra):
  • While Bihar participated, the Civil Disobedience Movement's strongest base was in Gujarat (Gandhi's home state) and Maharashtra. Bihar's contribution was significant but not as central as in the Champaran (1917) or Quit India (1942) movements.
