Three Principles of Cell Theory:
  1. All living organisms are composed of one or more cells
  2. The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life
  3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells (omnis cellula e cellula — Virchow, 1855)

2. Cell Organelles — Detailed Functions

The Nucleus

  • Structure: largest organelle in animal cells (~5-10 μm). Double membrane (nuclear envelope) with nuclear pores (selective transport). Contains chromatin (DNA + proteins) and the nucleolus.
  • Nucleolus: dense region inside the nucleus — site of ribosome assembly (rRNA synthesis). No membrane.
  • Function: stores genetic information (DNA); controls all cell activities (growth, metabolism, protein synthesis, reproduction); transcribes DNA into mRNA.
  • Exceptions: mature RBCs (no nucleus — to carry more haemoglobin), sieve tube cells in plants (no nucleus — companion cells support them).

Mitochondria — The Powerhouse

  • Structure: double membrane — outer (smooth) + inner (folded into cristae, increasing surface area). Matrix (fluid inside). Contains own circular DNA (mtDNA) and 70S ribosomes.
  • Function: ATP production via aerobic respiration: glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP (36-38 ATP per glucose molecule). Krebs cycle (in matrix) + electron transport chain (on cristae).
  • Endosymbiotic theory: mitochondria were once free-living bacteria (α-proteobacteria) engulfed by a host cell ~2 billion years ago. Evidence: own DNA (circular, like bacteria), double membrane, 70S ribosomes, reproduce by binary fission.
  • Maternal inheritance: mtDNA is inherited only from the mother (sperm mitochondria do not enter the egg). This makes mtDNA useful for tracing maternal ancestry.

Ribosomes — The Protein Factory

  • Structure: two subunits (large + small) made of rRNA and proteins. No membrane. 70S in prokaryotes (30S + 50S), 80S in eukaryotes (40S + 60S). 'S' = Svedberg units.
  • Location: free in cytoplasm (proteins for cell's own use) or attached to rough ER (proteins for secretion/membrane). Also found in mitochondria and chloroplasts (70S).
  • Function: protein synthesis (translation) — reads mRNA codons (3-nucleotide sequences) and assembles amino acids into polypeptide chains using tRNA.
  • Antibiotic target: many antibiotics (tetracycline, streptomycin, chloramphenicol) target bacterial 70S ribosomes without affecting eukaryotic 80S ribosomes.

3. Cell Division — Mitosis Deep Dive

PhaseKey Events
Interphase (S phase)DNA replicated (each chromosome → 2 sister chromatids joined at centromere). Cell grows and prepares for division.
ProphaseChromatin condenses into visible chromosomes. Nuclear membrane dissolves. Centrioles move to poles. Spindle fibers form.
MetaphaseChromosomes align at the cell's equator (metaphase plate). Spindle fibers from both poles attach to centromeres. Best phase for karyotyping (chromosome analysis).
AnaphaseSister chromatids separate at centromere. Each chromatid (now a chromosome) is pulled to opposite poles by spindle fibers. Cell elongates.
TelophaseChromosomes reach poles and decondense. Nuclear membrane reforms around each set. Nucleolus reappears. Spindle dissolves.
CytokinesisDivision of cytoplasm. In animal cells: cleavage furrow (contractile ring). In plant cells: cell plate (new cell wall forms). Result: 2 identical daughter cells.

4. Cell Division — Meiosis Deep Dive

PhaseKey Events
Meiosis I (Reductional)Homologous chromosomes separate. Diploid (2n) → Haploid (n). Crossing over occurs.
— Prophase I5 sub-stages: leptotene (chromosomes appear), zygotene (synapsis/pairing), pachytene (crossing over), diplotene (chiasmata visible), diakinesis (terminalisation).
— Metaphase IHomologous chromosome pairs align at equator. Independent assortment (random orientation) → genetic variation.
— Anaphase IHomologous chromosomes separate (not sister chromatids). One chromosome from each pair goes to each pole.
— Telophase I2 cells formed, each with haploid number (n) — but each chromosome still has 2 sister chromatids.
Meiosis II (Equational)Sister chromatids separate (similar to mitosis). No DNA replication between Meiosis I and II.
— Prophase II → Metaphase II → Anaphase II → Telophase IISame as mitosis but starting with haploid cells. Result: 4 haploid (n) gametes, each with one chromatid per chromosome.
Why Meiosis Matters:
  • Genetic variation: crossing over (Prophase I) + independent assortment (Metaphase I) → siblings look different
  • Chromosome number: halving (2n → n) ensures zygote gets correct number when sperm (n) + egg (n) = 2n
  • Sexual reproduction: produces gametes (sperm in testes, egg in ovaries) — basis of sexual reproduction and evolution
