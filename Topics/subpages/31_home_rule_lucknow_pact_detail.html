🏠 Home Rule Movement (1916) & Lucknow Pact (1916) — Master Detail

Two leagues deep dive (Tilak + Besant), Lucknow Pact terms, Moderates-Extremists reunification, Montagu Declaration, movement's decline, Bihar connections, 7 exam predictions

📖 The Two Home Rule Leagues — Deep Dive

Background: Why "Home Rule"?

  • WWI context (1914-1918): the British was preoccupied with WWI; India provided troops and resources. Indian nationalists saw an opportunity to press for self-government.
  • Irish inspiration: the Irish Home Rule Movement (campaigning for self-government within the Empire) was a parallel and inspiration. Both Tilak and Besant drew on the Irish model.
  • Post-1907 lull: the INC had been weak since the 1907 Surat Split — the Moderates controlled a moribund organisation, and the Extremists were outside. The Home Rule Movement (1916) revived the nationalist spirit.
  • Tilak's return (1914): Tilak was released from Mandalay prison in 1914 (after 6 years). He re-entered politics and founded his Home Rule League in April 1916. His slogan: "Swaraj is my birthright, and I shall have it".

Tilak's Home Rule League (April 1916)

  • Founded: April 1916, at Belgaum. Headquarters: Poona (Pune).
  • Coverage: Maharashtra (excluding Bombay city), Karnataka, Berar, Central Provinces.
  • Newspapers: Kesari (Marathi, since 1881) and Mahratta (English, since 1881).
  • Slogan: "Swaraj is my birthright, and I shall have it".
  • Methods: public meetings, libraries, reading rooms, newspaper propaganda, petition drives.
  • Membership: ~14,000 members (1916-1918). Mostly Maharashtrian middle class, students, and professionals.

Annie Besant's Home Rule League (September 1916)

  • Founded: September 1916, at Madras (Chennai). Headquarters: Adyar (Theosophical Society HQ).
  • Coverage: the rest of India — Bombay city, UP, Bihar, Bengal, Madras, Punjab, etc.
  • Newspapers: New India (daily) and Commonweal (weekly).
  • Background: Besant was a British-born Theosophist (joined 1889), president of the Theosophical Society (after Olcott's death, 1907). She founded the Central Hindu College (Banaras, 1898 — became part of BHU, 1916). She was also Congress President (1917, Calcutta).
  • Methods: newspapers, schools, libraries, public lectures, youth mobilisation.
  • Membership: ~7,000 members (1916-1918). Broader geographical spread but less concentrated than Tilak's.

Cooperation between the two leagues

  • The two leagues agreed to work separately but cooperatively — Tilak in the west, Besant in the rest of India. This division was agreed at the 1916 Lucknow INC session.
  • They shared the same goal: self-government (Home Rule) for India within the British Empire.
  • Both sent representatives to the 1916 Lucknow INC session, where the reunification of Moderates and Extremists and the Lucknow Pact were achieved.
71st PYQ Besant visited Bihar in 1918: Annie Besant visited Bihar in 1918 to strengthen the Home Rule League (71st BPSC PYQ — direct question). Her Bihar branches were in Patna, Muzaffarpur, and Bhagalpur. Tilak did NOT visit Bihar (his league covered only Maharashtra/Karnataka/CP).
📜 The Lucknow Pact (1916) — Terms and Significance

The Pact — Key Terms

TermDetail
Self-government demandJoint INC-League demand for self-government for India
Separate electoratesCongress accepted separate electorates for Muslims (already in place since 1909 Morley-Minto)
Muslim representationOne-third of seats in all legislatures reserved for Muslims
Communal vetoNo bill affecting a community could be passed if 3/4 of that community's representatives opposed it
WeightageMuslims got weightage (more seats than their population proportion) in Muslim-minority provinces (Bengal, Bombay, etc.)
In Muslim-majority provincesMuslims got seats in proportion to population (Punjab, Bengal, etc.) — no weightage for Hindus there

The Architects

  • Tilak (Congress side): the returning Extremist leader who pushed for the Pact. His credibility (after Mandalay) and energy made the reunification possible.
  • Muhammad Ali Jinnah (League side): then a Congressman-Nationalist (member of BOTH the Congress and the League). He convinced the League to accept the Congress's self-government demand. Jinnah was not yet a separatist — he was the "ambassador of Hindu-Muslim unity" (Sarojini Naidu's phrase). He would later lead the Pakistan Movement (from the late 1930s).

Significance

  • High-water mark of Hindu-Muslim political unity: the Pact was the peak of Congress-League cooperation before 1947. Both parties agreed on joint political demands.
  • Model for the future: the Pact's framework (separate electorates, weightage, communal veto) influenced subsequent constitutional discussions, including the Government of India Act 1919 (Mont-Ford Reforms).
  • Basis for future division: the acceptance of separate electorates and communal representation laid the groundwork for the communal politics that eventually led to Partition (1947). What seemed like a compromise in 1916 became the institutional basis for division.

Reunification (Moderates + Extremists, after 9 years) → Pact (Congress-League Lucknow Pact).

⚔️ The Movement's Decline (1917–1918)
  • Besant's arrest (June 1917): the British arrested Annie Besant under the Defence of India Act (June 1917). Her newspapers 'New India' and 'Commonweal' had been propagating Home Rule. The arrest paradoxically strengthened the movement (widespread protests, Tilak's league grew). The British released her in August 1917 under pressure.
  • Montagu Declaration (August 1917): Edwin Montagu (Secretary of State for India) promised "gradual development of self-governing institutions" in India. This took the wind out of the Home Rule Movement — it seemed the British were conceding the demand. The movement's momentum slowed.
  • Besant elected INC President (December 1917): after her arrest and release, Besant was elected President of the INC at the 1917 Calcutta session — the first woman President. But the movement was already declining.
  • Tilak's departure (1918-1919): Tilak went to England (1918-1919) for a libel case (against Valentine Chirol, author of 'Indian Unrest'). His absence weakened the movement.
  • Gandhi's rise (1919 onwards): the Rowlatt Satyagraha (1919), Non-Cooperation (1920), and Gandhi's mass movements took over the nationalist baton from the Home Rule Movement. The Home Rule Movement paved the way for Gandhi — but Gandhi's methods (satyagraha, mass mobilisation) went beyond Home Rule.
🔶 Bihar Connection — Deep Dive
1. Annie Besant's 1918 Bihar Visit (71st BPSC PYQ — direct):
  • Annie Besant visited Bihar in 1918 to strengthen the activities of the Home Rule League (71st BPSC PYQ — direct question, Q81).
  • The BPSC's official explanation: "Mrs. Annie Besant visited Bihar in 1918 in connection with the Home Rule Movement. She was leader of the Congress and presided over its 1917 session."
  • Her visit energised Bihar's nationalist circles — the Home Rule League had branches in Patna, Muzaffarpur, and Bhagalpur.
2. Tilak did NOT visit Bihar:
  • Tilak's Home Rule League covered Maharashtra, Karnataka, Berar, and CP — NOT Bihar. Bihar was in Besant's territory.
  • This is a potential trap in the exam: if the question asks "which Congress leader visited Bihar in 1918 for Home Rule?" — the answer is Annie Besant (NOT Tilak).
3. Bihar's 1912 Separation Context:
  • Bihar had just been separated from Bengal in 1912 (capital Patna) — a new province. The Home Rule Movement (1916) was the first major nationalist activity in the newly separated Bihar province.
  • The Bihar Provincial Conference (1912-1916) preceded the Home Rule League in Bihar. Bihar's new political identity (since 1912) made it receptive to the Home Rule message.
4. Champaran (1917) Connection:
  • Gandhi's Champaran Satyagraha (1917) occurred during the Home Rule era. Both movements (Home Rule and Champaran) were part of the nationalist revival of 1916-1918.
  • Besant's Bihar visit (1918) followed Gandhi's Champaran success (1917). Both contributed to Bihar's awakening.
5. Younger Bihar Leaders:
  • The Home Rule Movement in Bihar attracted younger leaders who would later join Gandhi's movements: Rajendra Prasad (future INC President, 1934; future President of India), Anugrah Narayan Sinha, and Sri Krishna Sinha.
