📖 Gandhi's Satyagraha Method

What is Satyagraha?

  • Satyagraha (Sanskrit: "truth-force" or "soul-force") was Gandhi's method of non-violent resistance. It was first used in South Africa (1906-1914) and then in India from 1917.
  • Principles: (1) Non-violence (ahimsa) — never harm the opponent; (2) Truth (satya) — the cause must be just; (3) Self-suffering — the satyagrahi accepts suffering to convert the opponent; (4) Fearlessness — no fear of arrest, imprisonment, or death; (5) Willingness to compromise — satyagraha seeks conversion, not victory.
  • Tools: (a) Fasts (hunger strikes); (b) Hartals (strikes/business closure); (c) Boycotts; (d) Civil disobedience (breaking unjust laws); (e) Peaceful picketing; (f) Constructive programme (khadi, village industries, Harijan uplift).
🧠 Mnemonic — Satyagraha "4 T-C":
Truth (satya) → Tolerance (non-violence, ahimsa) → Together (mass mobilisation) → Training (constructive programme).
Plus Constructive Programme: khadi, village industries, Harijan uplift, basic education, prohibition.
"4 T-C: the Gandhian method."
🌿 The Four Early Satyagrahas (1917-1918)

1. Champaran Satyagraha (1917) — Bihar's First Mass Movement

  • Place: Champaran district, Bihar (near the Nepal border).
  • Issue: the indigo farmers of Champaran were forced by British planters to grow indigo on 3/20 of their land (the "tinkathia" system) and sell it at below-market prices. The planters also extracted illegal dues (abwabs).
  • Trigger: Raj Kumar Shukla, a local farmer, met Gandhi at the Lucknow INC session (1916) and persuaded him to visit Champaran.
  • Gandhi's arrival (April 1917): Gandhi arrived in Champaran and began investigating the farmers' grievances. The British ordered him to leave; he refused and was arrested. The public protest forced the British to release him.
  • Champaran Agrarian Inquiry Committee (1917): formed in agreement between the Indian Government and Gandhiji (71st BPSC PYQ Q82). It had 4 members (including Gandhi). Its report led to the Champaran Agrarian Act (1918), which abolished the tinkathia system.
  • Bihar associates: Rajendra Prasad (71st PYQ matching), Anugrah Narayan Sinha, Braj Kishore Prasad, and Raj Kumar Shukla.
  • Significance: Gandhi's first satyagraha in India; Bihar's first mass nationalist movement; established the Bihar Congress as a major force.

2. Kheda Satyagraha (1918) — Gujarat

  • Place: Kheda district, Gujarat.
  • Issue: peasants hit by famine and crop failure; they demanded remission of land revenue. The British refused.
  • Method: Gandhi led a non-violent movement of non-payment of revenue. Vallabhbhai Patel was the key organiser.
  • Outcome: the British eventually suspended revenue collection for the affected peasants. Kheda was Patel's training ground — he became "Sardar" here.

3. Ahmedabad Mill Strike (1918) — Gandhi's First Fast

  • Place: Ahmedabad, Gujarat.
  • Issue: textile mill workers demanded a 35% wage increase (the plague had caused inflation). The mill owners (led by Ambalal Sarabhai) refused.
  • Method: Gandhi led the workers on strike. When the workers' morale wavered, Gandhi went on a hunger strike (his first fast) to pressure the mill owners and strengthen the workers' resolve.
  • Outcome: the mill owners agreed to a 35% wage increase (a compromise from the workers' original demand of 50%). The strike ended successfully.
  • Significance: Gandhi's first use of the fast as a tool of satyagraha.
⚔️ The Four Major Mass Movements (1919-1942)

1. Rowlatt Satyagraha (1919)

  • Cause: the Rowlatt Act (Anarchical and Revolutionary Crimes Act, 1919) allowed detention without trial for 2 years.
  • Gandhi's response: March 1919 — a nationwide hartal (strike) and protests. Gandhi's first all-India satyagraha.
  • Tragic outcome: the Jallianwala Bagh massacre (April 13, 1919) — Gen. Dyer fired on a peaceful crowd at Amritsar; 379 killed (official; unofficially up to 1,000). This shocked India and catalysed the Non-Cooperation Movement.

2. Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-1922)

  • Causes: Jallianwala Bagh (1919) + Rowlatt Act + Khilafat issue (Ottoman Caliphate's abolition).
  • Launch: September 1920 (Calcutta INC session); ratified at Nagpur (December 1920).
  • Methods: boycott of British schools, courts, councils, titles; swadeshi (Indian goods); peaceful protests.
  • End: withdrawn on February 12, 1922, after the Chauri Chaura incident (February 4, 1922 — a mob killed 22 policemen at a police station in Gorakhpur, UP). Gandhi believed Indians were not ready for non-violence.
  • Gandhi's arrest: March 1922; sentenced to 6 years (released 1924 for health reasons).

3. Civil Disobedience Movement (1930-1934)

  • Cause: the Purna Swaraj demand (1929 Lahore session, Nehru presided). January 26, 1930 declared Independence Day.
  • Launch: Dandi Salt March, March 12 – April 6, 1930 (240 miles, Sabarmati Ashram to Dandi). Gandhi broke the salt law by making salt from seawater.
  • Spread: the movement spread across India — no-tax campaigns, salt satyagrahas, forest satyagrahas, women's participation (Sarojini Naidu, Kasturba Gandhi).
  • Gandhi-Irwin Pact (March 5, 1931): Gandhi suspended the movement and agreed to attend the Second Round Table Conference (London, September 1931). The conference was inconclusive; Gandhi returned disappointed.
  • Resumption: the Civil Disobedience Movement was resumed in January 1932 and continued until 1934 (formally withdrawn in April 1934).

4. Quit India Movement (1942)

  • Cause: WWII (1939-1945); the British refused to grant immediate independence. The Cripps Mission (March 1942) failed; Gandhi called it "a post-dated cheque on a crashing bank."
  • Launch: August 8, 1942, at the Bombay INC session (Gowalia Tank Maidan, renamed August Kranti Maidan). Gandhi's slogan: "Do or Die" (Karenge ya Marenge) — "We shall either free India or die in the attempt."
  • British response: the entire Congress leadership was arrested on August 9, 1942. Gandhi was imprisoned at the Aga Khan Palace (Poona).
  • Spontaneous spread: despite the arrests, the movement spread spontaneously — students, workers, peasants, government employees participated. Underground groups like the Azad Dasta (Bihar, Shyam Narayan Singh) and the Congress Radio (Usha Mehta) operated.
  • End: the movement was brutally repressed by the British. It was the last major mass movement before independence (1947). The British realised after WWII (1945) that they could no longer hold India.
🔶 Bihar Connection — Deep Dive (71st PYQ Matching)
The 71st BPSC PYQ Matching (Q100) — The Highest-Yield Bihar Fact for this Topic:
  • Champaran Satyagrah → Dr. Rajendra Prasad: Gandhi's first satyagraha in India (1917) was at Champaran (Bihar). Rajendra Prasad was his key Bihar associate. The Champaran Agrarian Inquiry Committee was between the Indian Government and Gandhiji (71st PYQ Q82). The tinkathia system was abolished by the Champaran Agrarian Act (1918).
  • Azad Dasta → Shyam Narayan Singh: the Azad Dasta was an underground revolutionary group in Bihar during the Quit India Movement (1942). Shyam Narayan Singh led it in the Hazaribagh region. After the Congress leadership was arrested (August 9, 1942), underground groups like the Azad Dasta carried on the movement.
  • Individual Satyagrah in Bihar 1940 → Sahajanand Saraswati: Sahajanand Saraswati (Bihar's sanyasi-reformer, founder of the Bihar Provincial Kisan Sabha, 1929) led the Individual Satyagraha in Bihar in 1940 (during WWII). The Individual Satyagraha was Gandhi's response to the August Offer (1940) — carefully selected individuals offered satyagraha one at a time.
  • Kisan Sabha Bihar → Jayaprakash Narayan: Jayaprakash Narayan ("JP"), Bihar's most famous socialist leader, was associated with the Kisan Sabha (peasant movement) in Bihar. JP later led the Total Revolution (1974-75) against the Emergency. He was Bihar's homegrown revolutionary.
Correct code for the 71st PYQ: a-4, b-2, c-3, d-1 → 4, 2, 3, 1. Other Bihar Connections:
  • Raj Kumar Shukla: the Champaran farmer who invited Gandhi to Bihar (1917). Gandhi mentioned him in his autobiography.
  • Braj Kishore Prasad and Anugrah Narayan Sinha: other key Bihar associates at Champaran. A.N. Sinha later became Bihar's first Deputy Chief Minister (after independence).
  • Champaran = Bihar's first mass nationalist movement: the Swadeshi Movement (1905) bypassed Bihar; Champaran (1917) was Bihar's first true mass movement — 12 years after the Swadeshi Movement.
  • Quit India in Bihar: Bihar was a major centre of the Quit India Movement (1942). The Azad Dasta (Shyam Narayan Singh) was the underground group. The Bihar populace participated actively — students, peasants, and workers.
