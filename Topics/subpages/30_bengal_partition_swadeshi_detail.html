⚔️ Bengal Partition (1905) & Swadeshi Movement — Master Detail

Partition analysis, Swadeshi phases, leaders (Lal-Bal-Pal, Aurobindo, Tagore), cultural innovations, National Education, Muslim League (1906), Morley-Minto (1909), partition annulment (1911), Bihar connections, 7 exam predictions

📖 The Partition of Bengal — Why and How

Background

  • Bengal Presidency (pre-1905) included Bengal, Bihar, Orissa, and Assam — 78.5 million people, too large for efficient administration.
  • Official reason (Curzon): administrative efficiency — Bengal was too large to be governed as one unit.
  • Real motive (divide and rule): to separate Hindu-majority West Bengal from Muslim-majority East Bengal (with Assam), weakening the nationalist movement. Curzon calculated that a Muslim-majority East Bengal would be grateful to the British and serve as a counterweight to the Hindu-dominated nationalist movement.
  • Announcement: Curzon announced the partition plan in July 1905; it was implemented on October 16, 1905.

The Annulment (1911)

  • Date: December 1911 (King-Emperor George V at the Delhi Durbar).
  • Why: sustained Swadeshi resistance (boycotts, protests) made the partition untenable. The British also wanted to shift the capital from Calcutta to Delhi (announced at the same Durbar) — a strategic move to the historic Mughal capital.
  • Consequence for Bihar: in 1912, Bihar and Orissa were separated from Bengal as a new province (capital Patna). This gave Bihar its separate political identity — a direct outcome of the Swadeshi Movement.

🌿 Swadeshi Movement — Phases Deep Dive

Phase 1: Launch & Struggle (October 1905 – 1906)

  • Trigger: the Bengal Partition (October 16, 1905). Protests erupted across Bengal.
  • Methods: (a) Boycott of British textiles (especially Manchester cloth), sugar, salt; (b) Swadeshi — promotion of Indian-made goods (handloom textiles, indigenous products); (c) processions singing Vande Mataram; (d) Raksha Bandhan (Tagore's innovation) — tying rakhis between Hindus and Muslims as a unity symbol.
  • INC endorsement: the 1906 Calcutta session (Dadabhai Naoroji presiding) adopted the four-fold programme: Swaraj, Swadeshi, Boycott, National Education.
  • Mass participation: students, women, peasants, urban workers joined — the first mass nationalist movement in India.

Phase 2: Repression & Truce (1907 – 1908)

  • Government crackdown: the British repressed the movement — arrests, restrictions on newspapers, ban on public meetings.
  • Surat Split (1907): the Moderates and Extremists split at the Surat INC session (December 1907), weakening the movement. The Moderates retained control of the INC; the Extremists were expelled.
  • Tilak imprisoned (1908): Tilak was sentenced to 6 years in Mandalay prison (Burma) for his writings in Kesari.
  • Aurobindo's retreat (1910): Aurobindo Ghosh retired from politics and moved to Pondicherry (a French territory, beyond British jurisdiction), where he founded the Sri Aurobindo Ashram and developed Integral Yoga.
  • Movement weakened: by 1908–1909, the Swadeshi Movement was largely suppressed. This is the "truce" phase of the S-T-S strategy.

Phase 3: Renewed Struggle & Annulment (1909 – 1911)

  • Morley-Minto Reforms (1909): the British conceded limited reforms (Indian Councils Act 1909) — enlarged Legislative Councils and introduced separate electorates for Muslims. This was partly in response to Swadeshi pressures, partly to divide the nationalist movement further.
  • Sustained resistance: despite repression, sporadic Swadeshi activity continued.
  • Annulment (1911): King George V annulled the Bengal Partition at the Delhi Durbar (December 1911). The capital was shifted from Calcutta to Delhi at the same time. Bihar and Orissa were separated from Bengal in 1912.
🧠 Mnemonic — Swadeshi "S-B-N + V-R":
Swadeshi (Indian goods) → Boycott (British goods) → National Education (Bengal National College, 1906).
Vande Mataram (anthem) → Raksha Bandhan (Hindu-Muslim unity symbol, by Tagore).

🧠 Leaders of the Swadeshi Movement — Deep Dive

The Lal-Bal-Pal Trio (Extremists)

  • Lala Lajpat Rai ("Lal", Punjab, 1865–1928): "Lion of Punjab"; led Swadeshi in Punjab; propounded the Safety Valve Theory; later lathi-charged to death during the anti-Simon Commission protest (November 1928, Lahore).
  • Bal Gangadhar Tilak ("Bal", Maharashtra, 1856–1920): "Lokmanya Tilak"; "Father of Indian Unrest"; started Kesari (Marathi) and Mahratta (English) newspapers in 1881; started Ganapati (1893) and Shivaji (1895) festivals for nationalist mobilisation; imprisoned in Mandalay (1908–1914).
  • Bipin Chandra Pal ("Pal", Bengal, 1858–1932): fiery orator; journal New India; advocated Passive Resistance; spread the nationalist message in England and America.

Aurobindo Ghosh (1872–1950)

  • Extremist intellectual; started the Bande Mataram newspaper (1906) — the most radical journal of the era, advocating Purna Swaraj.
  • First principal of the Bengal National College (1906, Calcutta).
  • Involved in the Alipore Bomb Case (1908) — his brother Barindra was arrested; Aurobindo was acquitted but moved to Pondicherry (1910), where he founded the Sri Aurobindo Ashram and developed "Integral Yoga".

Rabindranath Tagore (1861–1941)

  • Cultural leader of the Swadeshi Movement; initiated Raksha Bandhan as a Hindu-Muslim unity symbol.
  • Led protest processions in Calcutta singing Vande Mataram.
  • Composed Amar Sonar Bangla (Golden Bengal) — later adopted as Bangladesh's national anthem (1972).
  • Won the Nobel Prize for Literature (1913) for Gitanjali.
  • Founded Shantiniketan (1901) — later Visva-Bharati University (1921).

Bankim Chandra Chatterjee (1838–1894)

  • Literary predecessor of the Swadeshi Movement; wrote Anandamath (1882) — the novel containing the song "Vande Mataram".
  • Also wrote Rajmohan's Wife (1864, the first English novel by an Indian) and Rajsimha.
  • Vande Mataram was adopted as the National Song of India (first two stanzas) on January 24, 1950.

Surendranath Banerjea (1848–1925)

  • "Surrender Not" Banerjea; founded the Indian Association (1876, Calcutta) — a precursor to the INC.
  • Initially supported the Swadeshi Movement, but remained a Moderate at heart. After the 1907 Surat Split, he stayed with the Moderates.
  • Accepted knighthood (1913) and became a minister in the Bengal government — distancing himself from the Extremists.

🔶 Bihar Connection — Deep Dive
1. Bihar was part of the Bengal Presidency (1905):
  • In 1905, Bihar was part of the Bengal Presidency — it was not a separate political entity. The Bengal Partition (1905) placed Bihar in West Bengal (the Hindu-majority province, along with Orissa).
  • Bihar was administered from Calcutta; its local administration was under the Bengal Lieutenant-Governor.
2. Bihar separated from Bengal in 1912:
  • When the Bengal Partition was annulled in 1911 (Delhi Durbar), Bihar and Orissa were separated from Bengal as a new province in 1912 (capital Patna).
  • This gave Bihar its separate political identity — a direct consequence of the Swadeshi Movement's success in forcing the annulment. Before 1912, Bihar had no separate political existence.
  • Patna became the capital of the new Bihar & Orissa Province (1912). Orissa was later separated in 1936.
3. Limited Swadeshi Impact in Bihar:
  • The Swadeshi Movement had limited impact in Bihar compared to Bengal. Reasons: (a) Bihar lacked a strong textile industry to boycott (Bihar was predominantly agricultural); (b) Bihar's nationalist consciousness was less developed than Bengal's; (c) the Bihar elite (lawyers, landlords) were more aligned with the Moderate wing of the INC.
  • There were some Swadeshi-inspired protests in Patna, Muzaffarpur, and Bhagalpur, but the mass mobilisation seen in Bengal was absent in Bihar.
4. Bihar's Nationalist Press:
  • The Searchlight (Patna, founded 1917) and the Hindustan Review (Sachchidananda Sinha) carried Swadeshi-era ideas to Bihar, but the impact was delayed and weaker than in Bengal.
5. Champaran (1917) — Bihar's First Mass Movement:
  • The Swadeshi Movement (1905) bypassed Bihar; Gandhi's Champaran Satyagraha (1917) was Bihar's first true mass nationalist movement — 12 years after the Swadeshi Movement.
  • Champaran was an indigo farmers' protest against the tinkathia system (forced indigo cultivation). Gandhi adapted the Swadeshi strategy — adding non-violence (satyagraha) and mass mobilisation.
6. Patna University (1917):
  • The National Education movement (a Swadeshi plank) had limited impact in Bihar. Patna University was established in 1917 — much later than Calcutta's nationalist institutions (Bengal National College, 1906). Bihar's modern educational infrastructure lagged behind Bengal's reform milieu.
