Topic 141 — South & Marathas: Deep Dive

Vijayanagara, Bahmani, Maratha Empire, Sikh Empire, Bihar connections

1. Vijayanagara Empire — Detailed Overview

The Vijayanagara Empire (1336-1646, 310 years) was the last great Hindu empire of South India. It was founded as a Hindu response to the southward expansion of the Delhi Sultanate and the Bahmani Kingdom. The empire was a bastion of Hindu culture, religion, and art — while also maintaining trade relations with the Portuguese, Arabs, and other foreign powers.

Krishnadevaraya — The Greatest Vijayanagara Ruler

  • Reign: 1509-1529 (Tuluva Dynasty)
  • Military: conquered the Raichur Doab (from Bijapur); defeated the Bahmani remnants and the Gajapati kingdom of Odisha; controlled all of South India south of the Krishna River
  • Titles: 'Andhra Bhoja' (patron of Telugu), 'Muru Rayara Ganda' (Lord of Three Kings — defeated three Deccan Sultans)
  • Literary works: Amuktamalyada (Telugu — about Goda Devi/Andal), Jambavati Kalyanam (Sanskrit)
  • Court poets: Ashtadiggajas (8 great Telugu poets) — Allasani Peddana (Andhra Pitamaha, Manucharitra), Tenali Ramalinga (Tenali Raman, known for wit), Nandi Timmana, Dhurjati, Ayyalaraju Ramabhadrudu, Pingali Suranna, Ramaraja Bhushana, Madayyagari Mallana
  • Foreign visitor: Domingo Paes (Portuguese, c. 1520) — described Vijayanagara as 'the best-provided city in the world' with 'all things in abundance'
  • Architecture: built and expanded temples at Hampi (Virupaksha, Hazara Rama), commissioned the Vittala Temple (with the famous stone chariot)

2. Bahmani Kingdom & Deccan Sultanates

The Bahmani Kingdom (1347-1527) was the first independent Muslim kingdom of the Deccan. Founded by Alauddin Hasan Bahman Shah (Hasan Gangu) who rebelled against Muhammad bin Tughlaq. The kingdom was known for its cultural synthesis — blending Indo-Islamic and Deccani traditions.

Mahmud Gawan (1411-1481) — The Great Prime Minister

  • Origin: Persian merchant-scholar who came to the Deccan and rose to become PM
  • Reforms: (1) land revenue system — measured land, classified by fertility; (2) army reforms — reduced noble power, direct royal control; (3) diplomatic relations with other kingdoms
  • Madrasa at Bidar: founded one of the finest Islamic educational institutions in medieval India — a three-storey building with a library, mosque, and lecture halls. Only ruins remain today (partially destroyed by lightning).
  • Death: falsely accused of treason by jealous nobles (who forged a letter implicating him). Sultan Muhammad Shah III ordered his execution (1481). His death marked the beginning of the Bahmani Kingdom's rapid decline.

3. Maratha Empire — Deep Analysis

Shivaji's Early Life and Rise

  • Birth: 1630 at Shivneri Fort (near Pune, Maharashtra), to Shahji Bhonsle and Jijabai
  • Upbringing: raised by Jijabai (who instilled Hindu pride and the ideal of 'Swaraj' — self-rule) and mentor Dadaji Kondadev (who trained him in administration and military affairs)
  • First conquest: at age 16 (1646), captured the Torna Fort — beginning his career of fort-building and expansion
  • Conflict with Bijapur: Shivaji's father Shahji was in Bijapur's service — Shivaji challenged Bijapur by capturing forts in the Pune region. Afzal Khan (Bijapur general) was sent to capture Shivaji — but Shivaji killed him with a concealed weapon (Tiger Claw/Wagh Nakh) at Pratapgad (1659)
  • Conflict with Mughals: Aurangzeb sent his uncle Shayista Khan (governor of Deccan) — Shivaji raided Shayista Khan's camp at Pune (1663) and cut off his fingers. Then Shivaji sacked Surat (1664, Mughal port)
  • Treaty of Purandar (1665): after Jai Singh I (Mughal general) defeated Shivaji, he signed the Treaty of Purandar — ceded 23 forts to Mughals, kept 12
  • Agra escape (1666): invited to Agra by Aurangzeb, placed under house arrest, escaped dramatically
  • Coronation (1674): crowned Chhatrapati at Raigad Fort (June 6, 1674) by Gaga Bhatt
  • Death: 1680 at Raigad (age 50)

Shivaji's Administrative System

  • Ashtapradhan (Eight Ministers): Peshwa (PM), Amatya/Majumdar (Finance), Sachiv (Secretary), Mantri (Intelligence), Senapati (Army), Sumant (Foreign Affairs), Nyayadhish (Justice), Pandit Rao (Religion)
  • Provincial administration: divided the kingdom into provinces (prant) under Sarsubedars, districts (pargana) under Havaldars, and villages under Patels
  • Army: cavalry (main force), infantry, navy (~500 ships). Direct payment to soldiers (no intermediaries). Branding of horses (dagh) to prevent corruption
  • Revenue: land revenue (kathi) — measured land; abolished the zamindari system in Maratha territory; chauth (1/4 of revenue) and sardeshmukhi (1/10 additional) collected from neighbouring territories
  • Justice: Nyayadhish (chief justice); panchayats at village level; Shivaji was known for impartial justice
  • Forts: ~300-360 forts (hill forts in the Sahyadri, sea forts on the Konkan coast). Key: Raigad (capital), Pratapgad, Sinhagad, Sindhudurg, Vijaydurg

4. Bihar Connection — Deep Dive

Bihar and the Maratha Power Dynamics
  • Bargi raids (1740s-1750s): Raghuji Bhonsle of Nagpur led Maratha cavalry raids (called 'Bargi' raids) into Bengal and Bihar. The Marathas extracted 'chauth' (1/4 of revenue) from the Nawab of Bengal's territories — including Bihar. The raids caused widespread devastation — villages were looted, people displaced. The folk memory of the Bargi raids survives in Bengali and Bhojpuri folk songs: 'Bargi elo deshe / bonchuli bisheshe' (the Bargis have come to the land / everything is ruined).
  • Alivardi Khan's struggle: Alivardi Khan (Nawab of Bengal, 1740-1756, whose territory included Bihar) spent much of his reign fighting the Marathas. He personally led campaigns against the Maratha cavalry in Bihar and Bengal. In 1751, he signed a treaty with the Marathas — ceding Orissa (Cuttack) and agreeing to pay 12 lakh rupees annually as chauth for Bengal and Bihar. This treaty brought temporary peace but permanently weakened the Nawab's finances.
  • Impact on Bihar: the Maratha raids weakened the Nawab of Bengal's control over Bihar — creating instability and a power vacuum. This instability made it easier for the British to intervene — first at Plassey (1757, after Siraj-ud-Daula succeeded Alivardi Khan) and then at Buxar (1764, in Bihar itself). Bihar became a British territory after the Diwani of Bengal, Bihar, and Odisha was granted to the East India Company (1765).
  • Battle of Buxar (1764, Bihar): while the Marathas were not direct participants at Buxar, the weakening of Bengal-Bihar by decades of Maratha raids created the conditions for British dominance. The combined armies of the Nawab of Bengal (Mir Qasim), Awadh (Shuja-ud-Daula), and the Mughal Emperor (Shah Alam II) were defeated by the British (Hector Munro) at Buxar (Bihar) on October 22, 1764. This battle was more decisive than Plassey — it gave the British Diwani rights over Bengal, Bihar, and Odisha (1765).
  • Maratha connection to Bihar's fate: the chain of events: (1) Maratha raids weaken Bengal-Bihar → (2) Nawab seeks British help → (3) Plassey (1757) → (4) Buxar (1764) → (5) Diwani (1765) → Bihar under British rule for the next 193 years. Bihar's fate was thus indirectly linked to the Maratha expansion in eastern India.
