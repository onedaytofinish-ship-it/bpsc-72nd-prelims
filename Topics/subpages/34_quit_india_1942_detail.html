Why the Cripps Mission Failed

  • INC: wanted immediate independence, not a post-war promise of dominion status. Gandhi opposed the right of provinces to secede (which would lead to partition). Called it "a post-dated cheque on a crashing bank."
  • Muslim League: wanted immediate Pakistan, not a post-war promise. Rejected the proposal because it didn't guarantee Pakistan.
  • Princes: refused to join the proposed federation without guarantees of their sovereignty.
  • Result: the failure of the Cripps Mission convinced Gandhi that the British would never voluntarily leave India — leading to the Quit India call.
🔥 The Movement — Phase by Phase

Phase 1: The Resolution (August 7-8, 1942)

  • Session: Bombay INC session at Gowalia Tank Maidan (August 7-8, 1942)
  • Resolution: "This Congress demands immediate ending of British rule in India"
  • Gandhi's slogan: "Do or Die" (Karenge ya Marenge) — "We shall either free India or die in the attempt"
  • Gandhi's message: "Every one of you should from this moment onwards consider yourself a free man or woman and act as if you are free."

Phase 2: Arrests and Spontaneous Eruption (August 9 onwards)

  • August 9, 1942: all top Congress leaders arrested before dawn — Gandhi, Nehru, Patel, Azad, Kripalani, Sarojini Naidu
  • Gandhi at Aga Khan Palace: Gandhi was taken to the Aga Khan Palace in Pune (house arrest). His wife Kasturba died there (February 22, 1944). His secretary Mahadev Desai died there (August 15, 1942).
  • Spontaneous movement: with no leaders, the movement became spontaneous. People attacked government buildings, railway stations, post offices, and telegraph lines. The British suppressed the movement with force — police firing, military action, mass arrests.
  • Militant character: unlike previous movements (Non-Cooperation, Civil Disobedience), Quit India turned militant in many areas. This was because the leadership was absent — people acted on their own initiative.

Phase 3: Underground Resistance

  • Jayaprakash Narayan (JP): escaped from Hazaribagh Jail (Bihar) in November 1942. Organised underground resistance. Arrested in 1943.
  • Ram Manohar Lohia: underground radio broadcasts from secret locations. Arrested in 1944.
  • Achyutrao Patwardan: ran the underground radio station ("Congress Radio").
  • Aruna Asaf Ali: "Heroine of 1942" — hoisted flag at Gowalia Tank Maidan on August 9. Went underground.
  • Shyam Narayan Singh: led the Azad Dasta (Bihar underground guerrilla group).

Phase 4: Suppression and Aftermath

  • British response: mass arrests (over 100,000), police firing (thousands killed), military deployed in many districts, fines imposed on villages.
  • Gandhi's fast (February 1943): 21-day fast in Aga Khan Palace — his health deteriorated. British released him on health grounds (May 1944).
  • Simla Conference (June-July 1945): Wavell proposed an executive council with Indian members — failed because the Muslim League insisted on nominating all Muslim members.
  • Aftermath: the movement demonstrated the depth of nationalist feeling. The British concluded that they could not rule India without Indian consent. After WWII ended (1945), the British initiated plans for withdrawal — Cabinet Mission (1946), Mountbatten Plan (1947), Independence (August 15, 1947).

🔶 Bihar Connection — Deep Dive
1. JP's Escape from Hazaribagh Jail:
  • Jayaprakash Narayan (JP) was imprisoned in Hazaribagh Jail (Bihar) during Quit India. He escaped in November 1942 — the escape was organised by his wife Prabhavati and fellow freedom fighters.
  • JP went underground and organised the Bihar resistance from secret locations. He was on the run for months before being arrested in 1943.
  • This escape is one of the most famous episodes of the Quit India Movement and highlights Bihar as a major hotspot.
2. Azad Dasta:
  • The Azad Dasta was the underground guerrilla group in Bihar, led by Shyam Narayan Singh (71st BPSC Q100: Azad Dasta → Shyam Narayan Singh).
  • The Azad Dasta conducted sabotage operations — attacks on railway lines, telegraph lines, and government property. It operated in the Bihar countryside.
3. Shri Krishna Singh (Bihar Kesari):
  • Shri Krishna Singh actively participated in the Quit India Movement (69th BPSC Q134). He was a key Bihar Congress leader.
  • He later became the first Chief Minister of Bihar (1946-1961) — the longest-serving CM of Bihar.
4. Attacks on Infrastructure:
  • Bihar saw widespread attacks on railway stations, post offices, police stations, and telegraph lines. The Bihar countryside was in rebellion for months.
  • The movement was strongest in the Tirhut and Chhotanagpur regions.
5. Sahajanand Saraswati (Individual Satyagraha 1940):
  • Sahajanand Saraswati led the Individual Satyagraha in Bihar (1940) — matched in 71st BPSC Q100. He was the founder of the Kisan Sabha (peasant movement) in Bihar.
6. Anugrah Narayan Sinha:
  • A.N. Sinha was another key Bihar leader arrested during Quit India. He later became Bihar's first Deputy Chief Minister.
7. Bihar's Historical Significance:
  • Bihar was one of the four major hotspots of Quit India (alongside Bombay, Bengal, and UP). The underground movement in Bihar was particularly well-organised.
  • Both the 69th and 71st BPSCs had Bihar-specific Quit India questions — this is a recurring pattern in BPSC exams.
