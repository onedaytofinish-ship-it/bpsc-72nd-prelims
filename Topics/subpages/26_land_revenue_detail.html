💸 The Drain of Wealth — Complete Analysis

Dadabhai Naoroji's Theory (1860s–1901)

  • The core argument: British rule was transferring India's wealth to Britain without any economic return — a unilateral extraction that structurally impoverished India. This was not trade or investment; it was a one-way drain.
  • The estimate: India was losing £30–40 million per year (in 19th-century values) — a massive sum relative to India's GDP. Over decades, this compounded into trillions in lost development potential.
  • The book: 'Poverty and Un-British Rule in India' (1901) — Naoroji's magnum opus, presenting the drain theory with detailed calculations.
  • Political impact: the drain theory became the economic foundation of the Indian National Congress's early critique of colonialism. Naoroji, a founding member and thrice President of the INC, used this theory to argue for self-government (swaraj).

🔶 Bihar's Zamindari Experience & the Bakasht Movement
1. Bihar's major zamindars under the Permanent Settlement:
The Permanent Settlement (1793) created a class of powerful hereditary zamindars in Bihar who dominated the agrarian economy for over 150 years:
  • Darbhanga Raj: the largest zamindari in Bihar (and one of the largest in India), covering parts of Darbhanga, Muzaffarpur, and Saharsa districts. The Maharaja of Darbhanga was among the richest men in India.
  • Bettiah Raj: the second-largest, covering parts of West Champaran. The Bettiah zamindars were also major opium traders.
  • Hathwa Raj: a major zamindari in Gopalganj/Saran, known for its oppressive rent collection.
  • Tikari Raj, Dumraon Raj, and others: dozens of smaller zamindaris across Bihar.
2. Peasant exploitation under Bihar zamindars:
Bihar's peasants were among the most exploited in India. Zamindars extracted:
  • Rent: often exceeding 50% of the produce (far above the 10/11th the zamindar paid to the government)
  • Abwabs (illegal cesses): arbitrary additional levies for festivals, births, deaths, and "good conduct"
  • Begar (forced labour): peasants were required to provide free labour for zamindar estates
  • Eviction: peasants could be evicted at will — they had no legal protection under the Permanent Settlement
3. The Bakasht movement (1930s–1950s):
A landmark peasant movement led by Swami Sahajanand Saraswati and the Kisan Sabha (founded 1936):
  • "Bakasht" land: land that was cultivated by the tenant (ryot) but legally owned by the zamindar. The movement demanded that bakasht land be transferred to the cultivating tenant.
  • Key demands: abolition of zamindari, reduction of rent, security of tenure, and the transfer of bakasht land to tenants.
  • Scale: the movement was particularly strong in Bihar's Darbhanga, Muzaffarpur, Saran, and Champaran districts.
  • Outcome: the movement built the political pressure that led to the Bihar Land Reforms Act (1950) — one of the first zamindari abolition acts in independent India.
4. Zamindari abolition (1950):
Bihar was one of the first states to abolish the zamindari system after Independence (the Bihar Land Reforms Act, 1950, under Chief Minister Sri Krishna Sinha). This was the direct culmination of:
  • The decades of peasant exploitation under the Permanent Settlement
  • The Bakasht movement's political mobilisation (1930s–50s)
  • The Kisan Sabha's advocacy (Swami Sahajanand Saraswati)
  • The post-Independence political consensus on land reform
Bihar's zamindari abolition was faster and more comprehensive than in many other states — a legacy of the Permanent Settlement's particularly harsh impact on Bihar's peasantry.
