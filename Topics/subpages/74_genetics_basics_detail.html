Key Discoveries Timeline

  • 1869: Friedrich Miescher — discovered "nuclein" (DNA) from pus cells
  • 1928: Frederick Griffith — "transforming principle" (bacterial transformation)
  • 1944: Avery, MacLeod, McCarty — DNA is the genetic material
  • 1952: Hershey–Chase experiment — confirmed DNA (not protein) as genetic material
  • 1953: Watson & Crick — double helix model (using Franklin's X-ray data)
  • 1958: Meselson–Stahl — DNA replication is semi-conservative
  • 1961: Nirenberg & Matthaei — cracked the genetic code (first codon: UUU = phenylalanine)
  • 2003: Human Genome Project completed (~3 billion base pairs, ~20,000–25,000 genes)

The Genetic Code

  • 64 codons (4 bases × 3 positions = 4³ = 64): 61 sense codons (code for 20 amino acids) + 3 stop codons.
  • Start codon: AUG (codes for Methionine; initiates translation).
  • Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA (no amino acid — terminate translation).
  • Degenerate/redundant: multiple codons can code for the same amino acid (e.g., UUU and UUC both = Phe).
  • Universal: same code in nearly all organisms (evidence of common ancestry).
  • Non-overlapping & commaless: read continuously in groups of 3 from the start codon.
🧠 Genetic Code Quick Facts:
64 codons total → 61 sense (amino acids) + 3 stop (UAA, UAG, UGA)
Start = AUG (Methionine) — "AUG starts, UAA/UAG/UGA stop"
20 amino acids, but 61 codons → code is degenerate (redundant) but unambiguous (each codon = one amino acid)

Non-Mendelian Inheritance

  • Incomplete dominance: heterozygote is intermediate (e.g., snapdragon: RR=red, Rr=pink, rr=white → 1:2:1 phenotype).
  • Codominance: both alleles fully expressed (e.g., AB blood group: IAIB = both A and B antigens).
  • Multiple alleles: >2 alleles in population (e.g., ABO blood group: IA, IB, i).
  • Polygenic inheritance: multiple genes control one trait (e.g., human height, skin colour — continuous variation).
  • Pleiotropy: one gene affects multiple traits (e.g., sickle cell gene → anaemia + malaria resistance + organ damage).
  • Epistasis: one gene masks/modifies another (e.g., 9:7, 9:3:4, 12:3:1 ratios — deviations from 9:3:3:1).
  • Sex-linked: genes on sex chromosomes (X-linked recessive: haemophilia, colour blindness).
  • Mitochondrial inheritance: mtDNA inherited only from mother (e.g., Leber hereditary optic neuropathy).

🔶 Bihar Deep Dive — Genetic Disease & Research
Bihar's Genetic Health & Research Landscape
  • Sickle Cell in Bihar: Tribal districts (Jamui, Banka, Bhagalpur, Munger, Katihar, Purnia) have significant HbS carrier rates. The Sickle Cell Institute of India (ICMR) screens tribal populations. AIIMS Patna offers Hb electrophoresis, genetic counselling, hydroxyurea therapy, and bone marrow transplant referrals. The trait (HbAS) protects against P. falciparum malaria — explains high frequency in malaria-endemic Bihar belt.
  • Thalassemia Burden: Bihar has ~3–8% β-thalassemia carrier rate. Major centres: AIIMS Patna (thalassemia day-care), IGIMS, PMCH. Bihar Thalassemia Samiti advocates premarital screening. Consanguineous marriages (in some communities) increase homozygous disease risk. Blood transfusion demand strains Bihar's blood banks (~80 blood banks statewide).
  • Genetic Counselling Centres: AIIMS Patna (Department of Medical Genetics) — prenatal diagnosis (amniocentesis at 15–20 weeks, chorionic villus sampling at 10–12 weeks), newborn screening, karyotyping, Hb electrophoresis, molecular testing. IGIMS Patna — genetics unit. These centres are critical for prevention of recessive disorders in a high-birth-rate state.
  • Makhana Genetics (Darbhanga): ICAR-Research Institute for Makhana (RIU), Darbhanga — germplasm bank, polyploidy research (makhana is a tetraploid, 2n=58), selection of high-yielding lines, disease resistance. "Mithila Makhana" GI tag (2010). Bihar >85% of national production. APEDA promotes export. Makhana value chain supports lakhs of farmers in Mithila region — a genetics-to-livelihood success story.
  • Down Syndrome care: AIIMS Patna, PMCH provide multidisciplinary care. Advanced maternal age (≥35) is the key risk factor — relevant in Bihar. Prenatal screening: dual marker (9–13 weeks), triple/quad marker (15–20 weeks), NT scan, NIPT (non-invasive prenatal testing) increasingly available.

📖 Quick Revision Checklist
  • ✅ DNA = deoxyribose + A,T,G,C, double helix (Watson & Crick 1953, Nobel 1962)
  • ✅ RNA = ribose + A,U,G,C, usually single-stranded
  • ✅ Base pairing: A=T (2 H-bonds), G≡C (3 H-bonds); Chargaff: A=T, G=C
  • ✅ Purines (A,G) = 2 rings; Pyrimidines (T,C,U) = 1 ring
  • ✅ Central Dogma: DNA →(transcription, RNA polymerase)→ mRNA →(translation, ribosome)→ Protein
  • ✅ DNA replication = semi-conservative (Meselson–Stahl 1958); each new DNA = 1 old + 1 new strand
  • ✅ RNA types: mRNA (messenger), tRNA (transfer — carries amino acid), rRNA (ribosomal)
  • ✅ Genetic code: 64 codons (61 sense + 3 stop); Start = AUG (Met); Stop = UAA, UAG, UGA
  • ✅ Mendel (pea plant, 1866): Law of Dominance, Segregation (purity of gametes), Independent Assortment
  • ✅ Monohybrid: phenotype 3:1, genotype 1:2:1. Dihybrid: phenotype 9:3:3:1. Test cross: 1:1
  • ✅ Sex determination: XX (female), XY (male) — father determines sex; SRY gene on Y
  • ✅ X-linked recessive: haemophilia (factor VIII/IX), colour blindness — males more affected
  • ✅ Sickle cell: Glu→Val at pos 6 of β-globin (chr 11), autosomal recessive, HbA→HbS, malaria resistance (trait)
  • ✅ Thalassemia: reduced α/β globin synthesis, autosomal recessive, transfusion-dependent
  • ✅ Down = Trisomy 21; Turner = 45,X0 (female); Klinefelter = 47,XXY (male); Edwards = 18; Patau = 13
  • ✅ Bihar: sickle cell in tribals, thalassemia burden, AIIMS Patna genetic counselling, makhana genetics at Darbhanga (GI tag 2010)
