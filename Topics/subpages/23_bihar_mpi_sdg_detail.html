🔶 Bihar MPI & SDG Index — Master Detail

Complete methodology, indicator breakdown, NFHS-4 vs NFHS-5 comparison, and Bihar's indicator-by-indicator MPI contribution

📖 Core Concepts — What Is the SDG India Index?
  • SDG India Index: NITI Aayog's biennial (every 2 years) state-level dashboard measuring progress on the UN Sustainable Development Goals. First edition: 2018. Latest: 2023-24 (4th edition).
  • Scope: 16 SDGs ranked (SDG 17 excluded — treated as a shared national goal). Each state gets a composite score (0–100) and a per-SDG score.
  • Performance bands: Aspirant (0–49), Performer (50–64), Front Runner (65–99), Achiever (100).
  • Bihar's position: consistently the lowest-ranked state. In 2023-24: composite score 57 (Performer band). The two weakest goals are SDG 2 (Zero Hunger) = 24 and SDG 4 (Quality Education) = 32.
  • Publisher: NITI Aayog — NOT UNDP (which publishes the global SDG report), NOT MoSPI.
📖 Core Concepts — What Is the National MPI?
  • MPI (Multidimensional Poverty Index): measures overlapping deprivations across 3 dimensions and 12 indicators. It goes beyond income poverty to capture the multiple ways people experience deprivation.
  • Publisher: NITI Aayog (India's National MPI). The Global MPI is separately published by UNDP & OPHI (Oxford) — different methodology, different publisher.
  • Data source: NFHS (National Family Health Survey). NFHS-4 (2015-16) and NFHS-5 (2019-21) are the two directly observed reference periods. NFHS-6 has not been conducted.
  • Three key measures: Headcount ratio (H) = % of people who are multidimensionally poor; Intensity (A) = average share of deprivations experienced by the poor; MPI value = H × A.

📊 Background — Why Bihar Ranks Low
  • Historical context: Bihar's economic decline began with the partition of Bengal (1905) and the permanent settlement land revenue system. Post-independence, slow industrialisation and infrastructure gaps persisted.
  • Demographic pressure: Bihar has India's highest TFR (total fertility rate, ~3.0 as per NFHS-5), putting pressure on health, education, and employment systems.
  • Rural predominance: >85% of Bihar's population is rural, and rural MPI headcount (36.95%) is more than double urban (16.67%). Infrastructure and service delivery gaps are worse in rural areas.
  • Nutrition crisis: Bihar's nutrition indicator contributes 27.95% to MPI — the single largest factor. High stunting (32.9% per GHI) and wasting (18.7%) rates drive SDG 2 down to 24.
  • Improvement trajectory: Despite being lowest, Bihar's MPI fell from 51.89% to 33.76% (NFHS-4 → NFHS-5) — an 18.13 pp drop. NITI estimates a further decline to 26.59% by 2022-23. State schemes (Saat Nischay, MMRY, free power, housing) are credited with the improvement.
