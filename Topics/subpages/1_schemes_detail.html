Scheme Architecture: Central Sector vs. Centrally Sponsored

For BPSC, understanding the structural planning of government schemes is essential for the Polity and Economy sections. Government initiatives are broadly divided based on their funding and implementation responsibility.

Government growth sectors: Welfare, Rural Development, and Digital Infrastructure.

1. Core Classifications (STATICS)

A. Central Sector Schemes

  • Funding: 100% funded by the Union Government.
  • Implementation: Implemented directly by Union Ministries or their agencies.
  • Subjects covered: Primarily Union List subjects (e.g., Defence, Railways, Foreign Affairs, Space, Banking).
  • Examples: PM-Kisan (income support), PM Jan Dhan Yojana, Fasal Bima Yojana (premium share rules differ but structural funding is central).

B. Centrally Sponsored Schemes (CSS)

  • Funding: Shared between the Union and States/UTs.
  • Implementation: Implemented by the State Government departments.
  • Subjects covered: State List or Concurrent List subjects (e.g., Agriculture, Education, Health, Rural Roads).
  • Core Funding Ratio Models:
    • General States: 60:40 (60% Union, 40% State).
    • North-Eastern & Himalayan States: 90:10 (90% Union, 10% State).
    • Union Territories without Legislatures: 100:0 (funded fully by Union).

2. Core Concepts: Schemes vs. Policies

💡 Concept Check:
• Scheme (Yojana): A specific action plan with dedicated budgetary allocation, targets, timeline, and implementing guidelines.
• Policy (Neeti): A broad vision document or statement of intent outlining the government's objectives and philosophy (e.g., National Education Policy 2020). Policies generally do not have direct household disbursements.

3. Background and Historical Evolution

Under the Planning Commission era (pre-2015), Centrally Sponsored Schemes were categorized as **Normal Central Assistance** and **Additional Central Assistance**. Post-2015, following the recommendations of the **Sub-Group of Chief Ministers on Rationalisation of Centrally Sponsored Schemes**, the CSS were grouped into:

  • "Core of the Core" Schemes: Underprivileged social sectors (e.g., MGNREGA, National Social Assistance Program). Mandatory funding models.
  • "Core" Schemes: Major developmental schemes (e.g., Jal Jeevan Mission, Swachh Bharat Abhiyan, PM Awas Yojana).
  • "Optional" Schemes: Local developmental requirements. States can opt-out.

4. Current Ongoing Relevance (2025-2026)

In recent budgets, the Union Government has emphasized the **National Portal for Public Finance Management (PFMS)** to track the Single Nodal Agency (SNA) model of CSS. Under this model, central funds are released to a single bank account of the state implementing agency, minimizing idle funds and improving cash-flow transparency.
