Topic 140 — Mughal Empire: Deep Dive

Six Great Mughals, battles, administration, Sher Shah's reforms, Bihar connections

1. The Mughal Empire — Overview

The Mughal Empire (1526-1857, 331 years) was one of the most powerful and culturally rich empires in Indian history. Founded by Babur after the First Battle of Panipat (1526), it reached its zenith under Aurangzeb (covering almost the entire subcontinent). The 'Great Mughals' — Babur, Humayun, Akbar, Jahangir, Shah Jahan, and Aurangzeb — ruled from 1526 to 1707 (181 years), after which the empire declined rapidly.

2. Mughal Administrative System — Deep Analysis

The Mansabdari System

Introduced by Akbar in 1571, the Mansabdari system was a unique military-civil bureaucracy. Each officer (mansabdar) was assigned a zat (personal rank) and sawar (cavalry rank). The zat determined the salary, and the sawar determined the number of horsemen the officer had to maintain. Salaries were paid in cash or through jagir (land revenue assignment). The system ensured that the emperor had direct control over the military and administration — no hereditary nobility.

Land Revenue System (Zabt)

The Zabt system (standardized by Todarmal, 1582-1589, based on Sher Shah Suri's earlier reforms) was one of the most efficient land revenue systems in pre-modern history:

  • Land measurement: land measured in 'bighas' using a standard measuring rod
  • Classification: (1) Polaj (cultivated every year, fertile), (2) Parauti (fallow for a year), (3) Chachar (fallow for 3-4 years), (4) Banjar (uncultivated for 5+ years)
  • Tax rate: one-third of the average produce, payable in cash (later commuted to a fixed cash rate per bigha)
  • Assessment: based on 10-year average yield (dash sala)
  • Collection: through amils (revenue collectors) at the pargana level
Key connection: Akbar's Zabt system was based on Sher Shah Suri's (Sasaram, Bihar) earlier revenue reforms. Sher Shah was the first to systematically measure land and classify it by productivity. Todarmal, who served under both Sher Shah and Akbar, perfected the system.

3. Sher Shah Suri — Bihar's Greatest Medieval Ruler

Sher Shah Suri (1486-1545) — From Sasaram to the Throne of Delhi
  • Origin: born Farid Khan in Sasaram (Rohtas district, Bihar), to an Afghan noble (Hasan Khan Suri). His grandfather had migrated from Afghanistan to Bihar during the Lodi period.
  • Rise to power: started as a minor administrator in Bihar. Gained military experience fighting for the Lodi Sultans and then for the Mughals (Humayun). Gradually built up his power base in Bihar and Bengal.
  • Defeat of Humayun: defeated Humayun at the Battle of Chausa (1539, Bihar) and the Battle of Kannauj/Bilgram (1540). Humayun fled to Persia. Sher Shah founded the Sur Dynasty (1540-1555).
  • Reign (1540-1545): though brief (only 5 years), Sher Shah's administrative reforms were revolutionary and were adopted by Akbar:
  • (1) Zabt system: land measured and classified; tax = 1/3 of produce; based on productivity
  • (2) Currency reform: introduced the Rupiah (silver coin, ~178 grains) and Paisa (copper coin) — the basis of modern Indian currency
  • (3) Grand Trunk Road (Sadak-e-Azam): ~2,500 km from Sonargaon (Bengal) to Peshawar (Kabul); 1,700 sarais (rest houses); trees planted on both sides; postal system with horse couriers
  • (4) Military reform: direct payment to soldiers (branding of horses — dagh); efficient spy system
  • (5) Justice: 'justice is the most excellent of religious acts' — impartial justice for all
  • Tomb at Sasaram: built 1540-1545, architect Mir Muhammad Aliwal Khan. Set in the middle of an artificial lake — one of the finest Indo-Afghan architectural works. 122-foot high, with a dome and four chhatris. A potential UNESCO World Heritage Site.
  • Death: killed in a gunpowder explosion at the siege of Kalinjar Fort (1545). His son Islam Shah Suri ruled until 1554. The Sur Dynasty collapsed in 1555, allowing Humayun to return.

4. Bihar Under Mughal Rule

  • Bihar as a Mughal Suba: under Akbar, Bihar was one of the 15 Subas (provinces). The Suba of Bihar included modern Bihar and parts of Bengal. The Subedar (governor) of Bihar was a key official — Bihar was strategically located between Delhi (imperial capital) and Bengal (the richest province).
  • Patna (Azimabad): Patna was the provincial capital of the Bihar Suba. It was renamed Azimabad by Prince Azim-us-Shan (grandson of Aurangzeb, governor of Bihar 1697-1712). Patna became a major trading centre — for saltpetre (used in gunpowder), cotton, silk, and opium.
  • English factory at Patna: the English East India Company established a factory at Patna in 1620 (during Jahangir's reign) — one of the earliest English trading posts in India. This was for the saltpetre trade (Patna was the world's largest saltpetre supplier). The factory laid the groundwork for later British expansion into Bihar.
  • Sher Shah's legacy in Bihar: the Grand Trunk Road passes through Bihar (Patna, Aurangabad, Sasaram). Sher Shah's tomb at Sasaram is one of Bihar's most important monuments. His administrative reforms originated from his experience governing Bihar before he became emperor.
  • Bihar's Sufi tradition under Mughals: the Sufi centres of Bihar (Maner Sharif, Phulwari Sharif, Bihar Sharif) continued to flourish under Mughal patronage. Bihar remained an important spiritual centre — on the route between Delhi (Chishti order) and Bengal (Firdausi order).
  • Bihar and the 1857 Revolt: Bihar played a significant role in the Revolt of 1857 — the Indian sepoys at Danapur (Patna) rebelled on July 3, 1857. Kunwar Singh (from Jagdishpur, Arrah, Bihar) led the revolt in Bihar at the age of 80 — one of the oldest and most heroic rebel leaders. He fought bravely but was wounded and died in 1858.

5. Decline of the Mughal Empire

  • Aurangzeb's policies: reimposed jaziya (1679), destroyed temples, alienated Rajputs, Marathas, and Sikhs. His 25-year Deccan campaign exhausted the treasury and army.
  • War of Succession: every Mughal succession after Shah Jahan involved bloody civil wars among princes — weakening the empire.
  • Rise of regional powers: Marathas (Shivaji), Sikhs (under Guru Gobind Singh), Rajputs, and Nawabs (Bengal, Awadh, Hyderabad) became virtually independent after Aurangzeb's death (1707).
  • Foreign invasions: Nadir Shah sacked Delhi (1739) — looted the Peacock Throne and treasury. Ahmad Shah Abdali invaded five times (1748-1761), culminating in the Third Battle of Panipat (1761).
  • Rise of the British: after the Battle of Plassey (1757) and Buxar (1764), the British East India Company became the dominant power. The Mughal emperor became a puppet — and finally, Bahadur Shah Zafar was exiled in 1858.
