🏛️ INC Formation (1885) — Master Detail

Session-by-session chronology (1885–1916), Moderates vs Extremists deep dive, the Safety Valve Theory, four-fold programme, Surat Split, Lucknow Pact, Bihar connections, 7 exam predictions

📖 The Foundation — How the INC Came to Be

Pre-Congress Organisations (the base)

  • The Indian Association (1876): founded by Surendranath Banerjea and Anand Mohan Bose in Calcutta. It organised the Indian National Conference (1883) — a precursor to the INC. Surendranath is sometimes called the "uncrowned king of India" (a phrase D.R. Vali by Naoroji).
  • Poona Sarvajanik Sabha (1870): founded by Ganesh Vasudev Joshi and others in Poona — an early nationalist body in Maharashtra.
  • Madras Mahajana Sabha (1884): founded by M. Viraraghavachariar, P. Ananda Charlu, and G. Subramania Iyer.
  • Bombay Presidency Association (1885): founded by Pherozeshah Mehta, K.T. Telang, and Badruddin Tyabji — just months before the INC.
  • The East India Association (1866): founded by Dadabhai Naoroji in London — to advocate Indian interests in Britain.

The Foundation — December 1885, Bombay

  • Initiator: A.O. Hume, a retired British civil servant (retired 1882), circulated a letter in March 1883 calling for a national political organisation.
  • First Session: December 28–31, 1885, at the Gokuldas Tejpal Sanskrit College, Bombay.
  • President: Womesh Chunder Bonnerjee.
  • Delegates: 72 (lawyers, journalists, businessmen, landlords, teachers — all urban, educated elites).
  • Viceroy: Lord Dufferin (1884–1888).
  • Resolutions passed: reconstitution of Legislative Councils, reduction of military expenditure, repeal of the Arms Act, salt duty reduction, and the demand for a Royal Commission on India.
EXAM TIP Foundation triplet: (a) Date → December 28, 1885; (b) Place → Bombay (Gokuldas Tejpal Sanskrit College); (c) First President → W.C. Bonnerjee. "Father of the INC" → A.O. Hume. Viceroy → Lord Dufferin. Memorise these five facts — they recur.
🌿 The Safety Valve Theory — Deep Analysis
  • Propounder: Lala Lajpat Rai, in his book Youngman (1916). He argued that Hume, with the approval of Viceroy Dufferin, founded the INC as a "safety valve" — a controlled outlet for Indian discontent, preventing a violent revolution.
  • Supporting view: R. Palme Dutt (in India Today, 1940) also argued that the British created the INC to co-opt the nationalist movement.
  • Modern critique (Bipan Chandra): in India's Struggle for Independence, Bipan Chandra rejects the Safety Valve Theory as overstated. He argues: (a) the INC had genuine Indian roots (the pre-Congress organisations and the Indian Association's National Conference of 1883); (b) Hume helped organise it but did not "create" it; (c) Indian leaders were already seeking a national body; Hume provided the platform.
  • Bipan Chandra's nuance: Hume did act with some awareness of the government's interest (Dufferin's tacit approval), but the INC's agenda and leadership were Indian, not British-imposed.
  • Exam framing: "The Safety Valve Theory was propounded by?" → Lala Lajpat Rai. "In which book?" → Youngman. "It concerns?" → Hume's role in founding the INC.
🧠 Moderates vs Extremists — Deep Comparison

The Moderates (1885–1905)

  • Approach: constitutional methods — prayers, petitions, protests ("PPP"). Belief in British sense of justice. Faith in constitutional reform.
  • Leaders: Dadabhai Naoroji (Grand Old Man of India, Drain of Wealth); G.K. Gokhale (Servants of India Society, 1905); Pherozeshah Mehta (Lion of Bombay); Surendranath Banerjea (Indian Association); R.C. Dutt (Economic History of India); G. Subramania Iyer (The Hindu).
  • Contributions: (a) Economic critique — Drain of Wealth (Naoroji), deindustrialisation (Dutt). (b) Demand for reforms in Legislative Councils (Indian Councils Act 1892). (c) Built the INC as a national organisation with annual sessions.
  • Limitations: narrow social base (elites only), no mass mobilisation, faith in British benevolence (which proved misplaced), fear of Extremist direct action.

The Extremists (1905–1919)

  • Approach: direct action — Swadeshi, Boycott, National Education, passive resistance, mass mobilisation. No faith in British sense of justice.
  • Leaders: Bal Gangadhar Tilak (Maharashtra); Lala Lajpat Rai (Punjab); Bipin Chandra Pal (Bengal) — the "Lal-Bal-Pal" trio; Aurobindo Ghosh (Bande Mataram); Bankim Chandra Chatterjee (Anandamath, Vande Mataram song).
  • Ideology: Indian cultural pride, Vedanta, Hindu revivalism (Tilak's Ganapati and Shivaji festivals from 1893-94), Swaraj as the goal (Tilak: "Swaraj is my birthright").
  • Rise: triggered by the 1905 Bengal Partition (Curzon) and the Swadeshi movement. The 1906 Calcutta session's four-fold programme was their victory.
  • Decline: the 1907 Surat Split marginalised them; Tilak was imprisoned in Mandalay (1908–1914). They returned to the INC in 1916 (Lucknow).

⚔️ Key Events Deep Dive

The 1906 Calcutta Session — The Four-Fold Programme

  • President: Dadabhai Naoroji (his 3rd and final presidency).
  • The four-fold programme: (1) Swaraj (self-government), (2) Swadeshi (use Indian goods), (3) Boycott (of British goods), (4) National Education.
  • Significance: the first formal demand for "Swaraj" — a major shift from the Moderates' gradualist approach. The Extremists' agenda was accepted by Naoroji, who used his prestige to push the Moderates to accept it.

The 1907 Surat Split

  • Context: the Extremists (Lal-Bal-Pal) wanted to extend the 1906 four-fold programme; the Moderates (Pherozeshah Mehta, Gokhale) wanted to return to constitutional methods.
  • Trigger: dispute over the presidency — the Moderates elected Rash Behari Ghosh (a Moderate); the Extremists wanted Tilak to preside or at least co-preside.
  • The split: on December 26, 1907, the Surat session broke up in chaos — physical clashes, adjournment. The Moderates re-convened separately; the Extremists were expelled.
  • Consequence: the Moderates retained the INC name and organisation (1908 Madras session formalised the split); the Extremists were outside the INC until 1916. Tilak was imprisoned (1908–1914, Mandalay).

The 1916 Lucknow Pact

  • Two achievements: (1) Reunification of Moderates and Extremists (after 9 years of split, since 1907). (2) The Lucknow Pact between the INC and the Muslim League.
  • President: Ambika Charan Mazumdar.
  • Architects: Bal Gangadhar Tilak (returning from Mandalay) and Muhammad Ali Jinnah (then a Congressman-Nationalist).
  • Pact content: joint political demands (self-government); separate electorates for Muslims accepted; one-third Muslim representation in legislatures.
  • Significance: the high-water mark of Hindu-Muslim political unity before 1947. Annie Besant's Home Rule League (1916) influence was evident. The Pact paved the way for the 1917 Congress-League joint demands.
🔶 Bihar Connection — Deep Dive
1. The 1912 Bankipore (Patna) INC Session — Sachchidananda Sinha:
  • The only INC session held in Bihar in the pre-Independence era. Presided over by Sachchidananda Sinha (1871–1950).
  • Sinha was a leading Bihar Congressman, lawyer, journalist. He founded the Hindustan Review (a major nationalist journal) and was closely associated with the Patna legal and political circles.
  • He was also the interim President of the Constituent Assembly in 1946 (the first session, December 1946), before Dr. Rajendra Prasad was elected as the permanent President. This makes him a doubly historic Bihar figure.
  • The 1912 session was held at Bankipore, Patna — the same year Bihar was separated from the Bengal Presidency (1912) and became a separate province (capital Patna). The session symbolised Bihar's new political identity.
2. Annie Besant's 1918 Bihar Visit (71st BPSC PYQ):
  • Annie Besant, the first woman President of the INC (1917) and founder of the Home Rule League (1916), visited Bihar in 1918 to strengthen the Home Rule movement.
  • The 71st BPSC asked: "Which Congress leader visited Bihar in 1918 in order to strengthen the activities of Home Rule League?" — answer: Annie Besant.
  • Her visit energised Bihar's nationalist circles. The Home Rule League in Bihar had branches in Patna, Muzaffarpur, and Bhagalpur.
3. Rajendra Prasad — Bihar's Link to the Later INC:
  • Dr. Rajendra Prasad (from Bihar) presided over the 1934 Bombay INC session — Bihar's link to the later, mass-mobilisation phase of the INC.
  • He went on to become the first President of India (1950–1962). His involvement begins after the Moderates-Extremists era but is Bihar's strongest link to the INC.
4. Bihar's Separation from Bengal (1912):
  • Bihar was separated from the Bengal Presidency in 1912 (capital Patna) — the same year as the Bankipore INC session.
  • The 1905 Bengal Partition (Curzon) had united Eastern Bengal (with Dhaka as capital) and Bihar within the broader Bengal Presidency; the 1911 annulment (King-Emperor George V's Delhi Durbar) led to Bihar's separate identity.
5. Swadeshi in Bihar:
  • The 1905 Swadeshi movement had limited impact in Bihar (compared to Bengal) — Bihar lacked a strong textile industry to boycott.
  • But Bihar's nationalist press (the Searchlight of Patna, founded 1917) carried the INC's message. Champaran (1917, Gandhi) was Bihar's first mass nationalist movement.
