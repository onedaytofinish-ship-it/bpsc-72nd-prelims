Topic 142 — Bihar in Medieval: Deep Dive

Sher Shah Suri (Sasaram) — life, reforms, military, architecture, Bihar legacy

1. Sher Shah Suri — From Bihar to the Throne of Delhi

Sher Shah Suri (1486-1545) is Bihar's greatest medieval ruler and one of the most consequential administrators in Indian history. Despite ruling for only 5 years (1540-1545), his reforms became the foundation of Mughal administration under Akbar — and their legacy continues in modern India (the Rupee, the GT Road, the land revenue system). Sher Shah's story — from the son of an Afghan jagirdar in Sasaram (Bihar) to the emperor of India — is one of the most remarkable in Indian history.

2. Sher Shah's Administrative Reforms — Deep Analysis

The Zabt Land Revenue System

The Zabt system was Sher Shah's most important reform — and the one with the longest legacy:

  • Measurement: all land was measured using a standard measuring rod — the 'gaz-i-Sher Shahi' (Sher Shah's yard). Land was measured in 'bighas' (a unit of area).
  • Classification: land was classified into three categories by productivity:
    • Polaj: land cultivated every year (most fertile) — full tax
    • Parauti: land left fallow for a year (moderate fertility) — reduced tax
    • Banjar: uncultivated for 5+ years (wasteland) — no tax, or nominal tax to encourage cultivation
  • Tax rate: one-third (1/3) of the average produce, payable in cash (at prevailing market rates) or in kind (grain directly).
  • Assessment: based on average yield over several years — to ensure fairness in good and bad years.
  • Patta: each farmer was given a 'patta' (document) specifying the tax amount — providing legal security.
  • Kabuliat: the farmer signed a 'kabuliat' (acceptance document) — agreeing to pay the specified amount.
Legacy: This system was perfected by Raja Todarmal under Akbar (1582-1589) and remained the basis of Indian land revenue administration until 1947 — 400+ years. Todarmal, who first served under Sher Shah in Bihar, was the key link between Sher Shah's system and the Mughal system.

Grand Trunk Road (Sadak-e-Azam)

  • Route: from Sonargaon (near Dhaka, Bengal) to Peshawar (near the Khyber Pass) — approximately 2,500 km. Later extended conceptually from Chittagong to Kabul.
  • Through Bihar: the road passed through Bengal → Bihar (Patna, Aurangabad, Sasaram) → UP (Varanasi, Allahabad, Kanpur, Delhi) → Punjab (Lahore) → Peshawar.
  • 1,700 sarais: rest houses built along the road — one every 2 kos (~4 km). Each sarai had: rooms for travellers, a mosque, a well, and stables for horses. Free food and lodging.
  • Trees: trees planted on both sides of the road — providing shade for travellers.
  • Postal system: sarais also served as postal stations (dak chowkis). Horse couriers (qasids) carried messages between sarais — enabling a message to travel from Bengal to Delhi (1,500 km) in just 2-3 days.
  • Trade: the road facilitated trade between Bengal, Bihar, and North India — boosting the economy. Merchants could travel safely with sarais providing rest and security.

4. Sher Shah's Military and Justice System

Military Reforms

  • Direct payment: soldiers were paid directly from the state treasury — not through intermediaries or nobles. This prevented corruption and ensured loyalty to the Sultan.
  • Dagh (horse branding): every horse was branded with the royal mark. This prevented the substitution of good horses with inferior ones — a common form of military corruption.
  • Chihra (soldier roll): a descriptive roll (with physical description) of each soldier was maintained. This prevented 'ghost soldiers' (non-existent soldiers whose pay was pocketed by officers).
  • Standing army: ~150,000 cavalry, 25,000 infantry, 5,000 elephants, and artillery. A large, well-equipped standing army that was directly under the Sultan's control.
  • Legacy: Akbar adopted the dagh and chihra systems. These reforms reduced corruption and improved military efficiency — and became standard Mughal military practice.

Justice System

  • Philosophy: 'Justice is the most excellent of religious acts' — Sher Shah believed that justice was the king's primary duty.
  • Impartiality: justice was administered without regard to wealth, religion, or social status. Hindus and Muslims were treated equally.
  • Structure: the Sultan was the highest court of appeal. Qazis (judges) were appointed in every province and district. Village disputes were settled by panchayats.
  • Efficiency: cases were heard and decided quickly — no long delays. Sher Shah personally heard important cases and was known for his fairness.
  • Folklore: stories of Sher Shah's justice (like the case of a poor widow vs a powerful noble) became part of Indian folklore — immortalised in the Tarikh-i-Sher Shahi by Abbas Khan Sarwani.
