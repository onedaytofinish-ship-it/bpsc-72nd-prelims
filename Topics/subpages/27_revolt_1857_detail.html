⚔️ Revolt of 1857 — Master Detail

Battle-by-battle timeline, causes (P-E-S-M-I), leaders, Bihar's Kunwar Singh campaigns, consequences (Act 1858), 7 exam predictions

📖 Causes of 1857 — Five Categories Deep Dive

1. Political Causes

  • Doctrine of Lapse (Lord Dalhousie): if a ruler died without a natural male heir, the state would 'lapse' to the British. Adopted sons could not inherit. States annexed: Satara (1848), Jaitpur & Sambalpur (1849), Baghat (1850), Udaipur (1852), Jhansi (1853), Nagpur (1853).
  • Awadh annexation (1856): Awadh was annexed on grounds of 'misgovernance' (not strictly the Doctrine of Lapse, but under the same annexationist spirit). The Nawab Wajid Ali Shah was deposed and exiled to Calcutta — this angered both the Nawab's supporters and the sepoys (many of whom came from Awadh).
  • Pension discontinued: Nana Saheb's pension (as adopted son of the last Peshwa) was discontinued after the Peshwa's death. Rani Lakshmibai's adopted son was not recognised as heir to Jhansi.
  • Bahadur Shah Zafar's title: Lord Dalhousie announced that after Bahadur Shah Zafar's death, his heirs would not use the title 'King' — only 'Prince' — effectively ending the Mughal dynasty's symbolic prestige.

2. Economic Causes

  • Land revenue pressure: the Permanent Settlement (1793), Ryotwari, and Mahalwari systems extracted maximum revenue, leaving peasants impoverished (see Topic #26).
  • Deindustrialisation: British manufactured goods (textiles) flooded India, destroying traditional handicrafts. Indian artisans (weavers, spinners, blacksmiths) lost their livelihoods.
  • Zamindar grievances: zamindars who defaulted on revenue payments lost their land at auction — creating insecurity even among the landowning class.

3. Social & Religious Causes

  • Interference in customs: abolition of sati (Bentinck, 1829); legalisation of Hindu widow remarriage (1856); the Widow Remarriage Act was seen as interference.
  • Fear of conversion: missionaries were increasingly active; the Education Despatch of 1854 promoted English education, seen as a step toward Christianisation.
  • Loss of caste for sepoys: sepoys were required to serve overseas (in Afghanistan, Sind, Burma) — crossing the sea meant loss of caste for Hindu sepoys.

4. Military Causes

  • Discrimination in pay and promotion: Indian sepoys were paid less than European soldiers and had no promotion prospects beyond Subedar (the highest Indian rank).
  • Batta (foreign service allowance) abolished: the allowance for serving outside their home region was cut, reducing sepoy income.
  • Greased cartridges: the Enfield rifle required biting the cartridge, which was greased with cow and pig fat — the immediate trigger.

5. Immediate Cause

  • Mangal Pandey (March 29, 1857): at Barrackpore, he attacked British officers — hanged April 8, 1857.
  • Meerut (May 10, 1857): 85 sepoys of the 3rd Native Cavalry refused the greased cartridges, were court-martialled and imprisoned. Their comrades broke them out and began the full-scale revolt.
🔶 Bihar's Role in the 1857 Revolt — Kunwar Singh Deep Dive
Kunwar Singh's campaigns — full account:
  • Early life: born 1777 in Jagdishpur (Ara, Bhojpur district, Bihar). He was the zamindar (landlord) of a substantial estate covering several villages in the Ara region. He was known for his generosity and his resentment of British interference in his zamindari rights.
  • Joining the revolt: when the sepoys at Danapur (near Patna) revolted on June 25, 1857, Kunwar Singh emerged as the natural leader of the Bihar revolt. He was 80 years old but possessed extraordinary energy and military skill.
  • Battle of Ara (July 1857): Kunwar Singh's forces engaged the British at Ara (the district HQ near Jagdishpur). He won the first engagement, defeating a small British force. The British then sent a larger force under Captain Dunbar, which was also defeated — Kunwar Singh's military acumen was remarkable for an 80-year-old.
  • March to UP: after consolidating his position in Bihar, Kunwar Singh marched westward into UP. He reached Azamgarh and relieved a besieged Indian garrison there — a remarkable feat of military logistics and leadership.
  • Battles in MP: from UP, he moved into the Rewa/Bundelkhand region of MP, fighting British forces in several engagements.
  • Return to Jagdishpur: in early 1858, Kunwar Singh fought his way back to Bihar, aiming to recapture Jagdishpur. He engaged British forces led by William Taylor in his final battle near Jagdishpur.
  • His final battle and death (April 26, 1858): Kunwar Singh was mortally wounded in this battle. According to legend, he cut off his wounded hand and offered it to the Ganga river (a traditional act of sacrifice in Hindu warrior culture) before dying. His younger brother, Amar Singh, continued the fight for several more months.
Other Bihar connections to 1857:
  • Danapur revolt (June 25, 1857): sepoys at the Danapur cantonment (near Patna) revolted — the Bihar chapter of the 1857 Revolt. This preceded Kunwar Singh's involvement.
  • Patna: after the Danapur revolt, British control of Patna was temporarily broken. The revolt at Danapur-Patna was a significant but often overlooked Bihar event.
  • Chhotanagpur: the Santhal and other tribal revolts in the Chhotanagpur region (now Jharkhand, then part of Bihar Presidency) were related to the same colonial exploitation that fuelled 1857. Birsa Munda's later revolt (1895–1900) was a continuation of this tribal resistance.
  • Legacy: the Veer Kunwar Singh University (Ara), the Veer Kunwar Singh Bridge (over the Ganga at Ara), and Veer Kunwar Singh Kranti Mela (annual fair at Jagdishpur) commemorate his contribution. The Bihar government celebrates his birth anniversary (November 22) as a day of state remembrance.
📜 Consequences of the Revolt of 1857
ChangeDetail
EIC rule endedThe East India Company's governance (since 1765 Diwani) was ended by the Government of India Act 1858. Power transferred to the British Crown.
Viceroy replaces Gov-GenThe Governor-General was redesignated as Viceroy (Lord Canning became the first Viceroy). The Viceroy represented both the Crown and the British government.
India Office createdA new India Office was created in London, headed by the Secretary of State for India (a cabinet minister). The India Council advised the Secretary.
Doctrine of Lapse endedThe Queen's Proclamation (Nov 1, 1858) ended the Doctrine of Lapse — no more annexations based on lack of natural heirs. Adopted sons were recognised.
Religious neutrality promisedThe Proclamation promised religious neutrality and non-interference in Indian customs — a direct response to the greased cartridges grievance.
Army reorganisationThe British reorganised the Indian Army: reduced the proportion of high-caste sepoys, increased the proportion of Sikhs, Gurkhas, and Pathans (who had stayed loyal), and introduced the policy of 'divide and rule' in military recruitment.
Ratio of British to Indian troopsIncreased from ~1:5 to ~1:2 (or ~1:3 in some accounts) — to prevent another mass sepoy revolt.
Princely states appeasedThe Proclamation assured princely states that their treaties would be honoured — a deliberate strategy to win the loyalty of Indian rulers against any future nationalist movement.
EXAM TIP Queen's Proclamation date: November 1, 1858. "What did it promise?" → religious neutrality, end of Doctrine of Lapse, equal opportunity in government service. "What did it NOT promise?" → self-government (swaraj). "Who issued it?" → Queen Victoria (read by Lord Canning at Allahabad Darbar).
