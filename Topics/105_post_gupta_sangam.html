🔶 Bihar Connection
Bihar — Nalanda in the Post-Gupta Period
  • Nalanda University (Bihar): Hiuen Tsang (Xuanzang) studied at Nalanda for 5 years (c. 630-645 CE) during Harsha's reign. Nalanda was at its peak — the world's greatest Buddhist university. Harsha patronised Nalanda and organised the Kanauj Assembly (643 CE) in honour of Hiuen Tsang.
  • Political shift: after the Guptas, political power shifted from Pataliputra (Bihar) to Kannauj (UP) under Harsha. Bihar was no longer the political capital — but Nalanda kept Bihar at the centre of Buddhist learning.
  • Harsha's patronage: Harsha patronised Nalanda (Bihar) — he donated land and resources. Nalanda flourished under Harsha's support, maintaining its position as the premier educational institution of ancient India.
  • Hiuen Tsang's account: his 'Si-yu-ki' provides a detailed account of Nalanda (Bihar) — its architecture, curriculum, teachers, students, and the daily life of the university. This is the most comprehensive description of Nalanda that survives.
🔗 Cross-Topic Connections
  • Topic 104 (Gupta Empire): After the Gupta decline (Hun invasions, 5th-6th c. CE), Harsha (606-647 CE) briefly reunified northern India — but from Kannauj (UP), not Pataliputra (Bihar). Nalanda (founded by Kumargupta I) continued to flourish under Harsha.
  • Topic 102 (Mauryan Empire): Both the Mauryas and Harsha ruled large parts of northern India. But while the Mauryas ruled from Pataliputra (Bihar), Harsha ruled from Kannauj (UP) — reflecting the political shift from Bihar to UP after the Gupta period.
📰 Current Relevance
  • Thirukkural: Thiruvalluvar's 1,330 couplets on ethics, politics, and love — translated into many languages. A statue of Thiruvalluvar was unveiled in Haridwar (2021). The Thirukkural is studied worldwide as a universal text on morality.
  • Mahabalipuram (Pallava): UNESCO World Heritage Site (1984). The Shore Temple and Pancha Rathas are major tourist attractions — showcasing the beginning of Dravidian temple architecture.
  • Pattadakal (Chalukya): UNESCO World Heritage Site (1987). Temples in both Nagara (North) and Dravida (South) styles — a unique synthesis. Shows the Chalukyas as a bridge between northern and southern architectural traditions.
  • Nalanda University (Bihar): UNESCO World Heritage Site (2016). A new Nalanda University was established (2014) — reviving the ancient legacy of Bihar as a centre of learning.
  • Sangam literature: the Sangam texts (Silappadikaram, Thirukkural) are recognised as classical literature — the foundation of Tamil cultural identity. Tamil was declared a classical language by the Government of India (2004).

Q1. Harshavardhana (c. 606-647 CE) ruled from which capital?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Harsha ruled from Kannauj (UP). Originally from Thanesar (Haryana), shifted to Kannauj after conquering it. Last great Hindu emperor of north India — Punjab to Bengal, south to Narmada.
Q2. Harsha's biography 'Harshacharita' was written by which author?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Banabhatta wrote 'Harshacharita' (Biography of Harsha, Sanskrit prose). Also wrote 'Kadambari' (completed by son Bhushanabhatta). One of the few biographical works in ancient Indian literature.
Q3. The Chinese pilgrim Hiuen Tsang (Xuanzang) visited India during whose reign?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Hiuen Tsang visited during Harsha's reign (630-645 CE). 15 years in India, 5 years at Nalanda (Bihar). Met Harsha at Kannauj (643 CE). 'Si-yu-ki' = crucial source for post-Gupta India.
Q4. Harsha was defeated by which southern king at the Battle of Narmada (c. 634 CE)?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Pulakeshin II (Chalukya) defeated Harsha at Narmada (634 CE). Aihole inscription (Ravikirti): 'Harsha's harsha (joy) melted away through fear.' Narmada = boundary between Harsha and Chalukyas.
Q5. Harsha was a patron of which religions?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Harsha was Shaivite (Hindu) who later patronised Buddhism. Organised Kanauj Assembly (643 CE, Buddhist council) + Prayag Assembly (annual charity). Wrote 3 plays: Ratnavali, Priyadarshika, Nagananda.
Q6. The Chalukya dynasty of Badami ruled which region (c. 6th-8th century CE)?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Chalukyas of Badami (543-757 CE) ruled Karnataka + Andhra (Deccan). Capital: Badami (Vatapi). Founder: Pulakeshin I (543 CE). Greatest: Pulakeshin II (610-642 CE) — defeated Harsha.
Q7. Pulakeshin II's Aihole inscription (c. 634 CE), which records his victory over Harsha, was composed by which poet?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Ravikirti (Jain poet) composed the Aihole inscription (634 CE). Records Pulakeshin's victory over Harsha. At Meguti Jain temple, Aihole. One of the finest Sanskrit inscriptions.
Q8. The Pallava dynasty ruled which region (c. 4th-9th century CE)?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Pallavas (4th-9th c. CE) ruled northern Tamil Nadu (Tondaimandalam). Capital: Kanchipuram. Greatest: Mahendravarman I + Narasimhavarman I (defeated Pulakeshin II, 642 CE). Mahabalipuram temples.
Q9. The famous rock-cut temples at Mahabalipuram (Mamallapuram, Tamil Nadu) were built by which dynasty?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Mahabalipuram (Mamallapuram) built by Pallavas — Narasimhavarman I ('Mamalla') + Rajasimha. UNESCO (1984). Pancha Rathas, Shore Temple, Descent of the Ganges (bas-relief).
Q10. The Sangam Age (c. 300 BCE - 300 CE) refers to the period of Tamil literary assemblies. Where were the Sangams held?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Sangams at Madurai (1st, no texts survive), Kapadapuram (2nd, only Tolkappiyam survives), North Madurai (3rd, most surviving Sangam literature). Patronised by Pandya kings of Madurai.
Q11. The Sangam literature was patronised by which three dynasties (the 'Muvendar')?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Three Tamil kingdoms ('Muvendar'): Cheras (Kerala, bow emblem), Cholas (northern TN, tiger emblem), Pandyas (southern TN, fish emblem). Ruled South India during Sangam Age (300 BCE - 300 CE).
Q12. The greatest Chera king of the Sangam Age, known for his generosity, was:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Senguttuvan (Red Chera, 2nd c. CE). 'The Chera who gave without stint.' Founded Pattini cult (worship of Kannagi as ideal wife). Crossed the Ganga (north India expedition, per Silappadikaram).
Q13. The greatest Chola king of the Sangam Age, known for building the Kallanai dam (Grand Anicut) on the Kaveri, was:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Karikala (2nd c. CE). Built Kallanai dam (Grand Anicut) on Kaveri — one of the oldest dams in the world (still in use). Won Battle of Venni (defeated Cheras + Pandyas). Imperial Cholas claimed descent from him.
Q14. The greatest Pandya king of the Sangam Age, known for his patronage of Sangam literature, was:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Nedunjeliyan (2nd c. CE). 'Ariyapadai Kadantha' (defeated the Aryans). Court at Madurai = great literary centre. Pandyas patronised 3rd Sangam at Madurai — most surviving Sangam literature compiled here.
Q15. The Silappadikaram (one of the three Tamil epics) was written by which author?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Ilango Adigal (Chera prince, 2nd c. CE). Silappadikaram = Kovalan & Kannagi story. Kannagi burns Madurai after husband unjustly executed. Celebrates Pattini (chaste wife) cult. One of five great Tamil epics.
Q16. The Thirukkural (a classic Tamil text of 1,330 couplets on ethics, politics, and love) was written by:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Thiruvalluvar (1st-2nd c. CE). 1,330 couplets in 133 chapters. Three books: Aram (Virtue), Porul (Wealth/Politics), Inbam (Love). 'Tamil Veda.' Translated into many languages.
Q17. The Manimekalai (the second Tamil epic, a sequel to the Silappadikaram) was written by:
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Sattanar (2nd c. CE). Sequel to Silappadikaram — story of Manimekalai (daughter of Kovalan + Madhavi). Buddhist themes — Manimekalai becomes Buddhist nun. One of five great Tamil epics. 'Twin Epics' with Silappadikaram.
Q18. The Sangam literature is divided into how many anthologies (the 'Ettuthogai' or Eight Anthologies)?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Ettuthogai (Eight Anthologies) + Pattupattu (Ten Idylls). Core of Sangam literature. Poems classified into Aham (love, inner) and Puram (war, heroic, outer).
Q19. The Sangam poems are classified into which two main genres?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Aham (love/inner): love, romance, separation, five landscapes (tinai). Puram (war/outer): war, kings, battles, heroism, death in battle. Unique Aham-Puram binary of Tamil Sangam literature.
Q20. Bihar's connection with the Post-Gupta period is primarily through which fact?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Hiuen Tsang studied at Nalanda (Bihar) for 5 years (630-645 CE) during Harsha's reign. Nalanda = world's greatest Buddhist university. Harsha patronised Nalanda. Bihar = centre of Buddhist learning, but political power shifted to Kannauj (UP).
Q21. After Harsha's death (647 CE), his empire collapsed. Who was the Chinese ambassador at Harsha's court who left a valuable account?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Wang Xuance (Chinese ambassador). After Harsha's death (647 CE), attacked by new king Arjuna. Escaped, returned with Tibetan + Nepali army, defeated Arjuna. Accounts provide valuable info about post-Harsha India.
Q22. The Chalukya dynasty is known for which architectural contribution?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Chalukyas: temples at Badami, Aihole ('Cradle of Indian Temple Architecture,' ~125 temples), Pattadakal (UNESCO 1987, Nagara + Dravida styles). Bridges northern and southern architectural traditions.
Q23. The Sangam Age economy was characterized by which feature?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Flourishing international trade. Roman trade = major wealth source (Roman gold coins found in South India). Ports: Muziris (Kerala), Puhar/Kaveripattinam (Chola). Exports: spices, pearls, muslin, gems. Periplus of the Erythraean Sea (1st c. CE).
Q24. Which of the following is NOT a feature of the Sangam Age society?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Sangam society did NOT have a rigid caste system. Had occupational groups (arasar = rulers, vaisiyar = traders, vellalar = farmers) — based on occupation, not birth. Women had relatively high status (women poets like Avvaiyar).
Q25. The Chalukya king Pulakeshin II (c. 610-642 CE) is also known for which achievement?
✅ Correct Answer: (A) — Pulakeshin II defeated Pallava Mahendravarman I, extended Chalukya empire (Gujarat to Andhra), resisted early Arab incursions (c. 636 CE). Aihole inscription (Ravikirti, 634 CE). Defeated and killed by Narasimhavarman I (Pallava) c. 642 CE.
